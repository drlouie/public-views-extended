4 and 2
by

The gen 1 scopes(PVS-2 is 3 stage gen 1) typically have much better resolution, and the 3 stage has pretty decent gain. The PVS-2 uses a similar photocathode material as well, but does not have microchannel plate. In reality the tubes are very similar except the PVS-4 has the microchannel plate and the PVS-2 has 3 stages. Anyway, the PVS-4 can be upgraded to gen 3 which will give much better performance overall, but the gen 2's just are not real hot performers in my experience with them. The 4 is much more versatile and has nicer optics and much smaller and lighter too I think. The adjustable reticle on the 4 is a big plus and the mounting system is a plus. Unless you are a die hard, gotta have military PVS-4 rugged size and look, there are other scopes that will do the job in gen 3 for the same amount new that you would pay for used PVS-4 gen 3 stuff. It is a rugged reliable unit though, and can be a good shooter.

Zeroing a PVS-4
by

Of course it makes sense that you can't zero a PVS-4
at 25yards like you can with the iron sights. The centerline of the scope is about two inches higher.

The best approach is to get a ballistic program and calculate the trajectory at various ranges given the hight of the centerline above the bore. There may not even be two usable points of aim at 100 yards with a scope that high. If there is it will be well beyond 25 yards. Otherwise just zero it at the range you're going to shoot at with the lens cap in place. The's why they put the little holes in a symetrical pattern around the centerline in the lens caps of weapons scopes. That way it doesn't change the point of aim when you take it off.

filters for NV
by

Consider this. A halogen lamp puts out a broad spectrum of wavelengths from around 5000 nm to 300 nm, the long end limited by the bulb and the short end by the temperature. A Gen 1 or Gen II scope typically has a range of about 350 to 1000nm. The human eye from about 380 to 800 (for 1x10-6 reduction). If you want to filter an incandescent light you want to block as much visible light as possible, but pass as much IR as you can which is in the range of the NV tube. If you had a perfect long pass filter you'd want it to cut at about 800nm. The trouble is that absorbtion filters aren't sharp.
see:
http://www.noao.edu/kpno/filters/sgt/127_sm.gif.

RG-850 is about the best you can do with a halogen light but it still leaks a bit of light short of 800nm and with a bright light and dark adapted eyes you can still see a glow. RG-1000 blocks too much of the IR to be very good with NV tubes. It does ok with some CCD extended red cameras.

You can get sharper cutoff longpass performance with interference filters.
Look at the curves at:
http://www.andcorp.com/Web_store/Edge_Filters/ir_long_pass.html
but also look at the prices.. $166 for a 2" diameter

My first question would be who is the manufacture of the RG880 filter of which you speak? I assume from the words "high quality filter coatings" that may be the name of the company. If so then this filter is in fact an interference filter since coatings are exactly what an interference filter is. An interference filter is simply multiple layers of metallic and/or dielectric films applied in a vacuum chamber. The thickness of each layer has to be accurately controlled. Usually several filter can be coated simultaniously which shares the cost of the expensive equipment it takes to make one.

The RG880 is certainly not a Schott absorbtion filter as they don't make one with that number. It's not uncommon to use glass absorbtion filters as a substrate for interference filters to give extra blocking away from the cutoff wavelength. If that's the case then this is exactly the kind of filter I've been talking about. If a company made a batch order they could probably sell them for $150 or less and have them custom made to fit a particular flashlight. You won't by just one from
a filter manufacturer for that price. Andover gets
$166 for their 850nm cutoff 2" diameter stock interference filter. Andover isn't know for low prices
as these are "research grade" quality.

The primary functional difference between absorbtion filters and interference filters is that interference can be made with a sharper cutoff. They usually include an absorbtion filter. Scratching the coated surface of an interference filter will cause it to leak along the scratch unlike an absorbtion filter which uses the bulk of the glass. Interference filters reflect the part of the light which isn't passed, though that may be absorbed by the substrate. These filters typically look like a mirror on one side or perhaps both sizes. Infrared absorbtion filters look black on both sides. An interferece filter can be sandwitched between two absorbtion filters so it looks black on both sides too.

See http://www.optodiode.com for high power 880nm LED's. High power is about 100mw for a single approximately 1mm size diode. Halogen bulbs
do better for a given emitting surface area. Short arc xenon lamps do much better. None of these are suitable
as a pointer as they cannot be focused to a tight beam.
Smaller than about 1/2 degree is practical only with a laser.

RG filters
by

If by RG filters you mean the glass filters beginning with the letters RG fillowed by the 1/2 power wavelenght in nanometers (such as RG-850, RG-1000, etc) The glass is made by Schott Optical of Germany. They sell sheets of their glass to many fabricators around the world. I usually buy from ESCO Products. http://www.escoproducts.com
Be warned, a 2" square RG-850 filter is $36. They do custom sizes but it costs $$$. You can get 6" squares from some other companies.


Gen 1 and Gen II NV scopes (at least US made ones) use the the same multialkalai photocathodes which have very similar spectral response. Gen 1 gets useful gain though stacking multiple tubes. GenII get's useful gain with a microchannel plate. GenIII uses a Galium Arsinide photocatode which has higher sensitivity and lower noise out to about 950 nm where it falls off sharply. Beyond that GenII is perhaps a bit more sensitive.

A 3mm thick RG-1000 filter doesn't pass a lot of IR light from a white light source which can be seen with any NV scope, and even it will pass a very slight amount of visible light. The RG-850 will have a visible glow when used with a white light source. You can do pretty well with a Schott RG-850 filter in front of an 880 nm high power LED. 880 to 900 nm lasers generally don't need a filter to be invisible.

The human eye cannot see 880 nm. Period. THe problem is that LED's which have a center wavelength of 880 nm have about 1% of their energy shorter than 800nm. LED's are NEVER monochromatic and typically have half power bandwidths of 50nm with typical bell shaped edges. The human eye has about 1 part in a million of it's sensitivity at 800 nm relative to it's peak green repsonse.

IR filters have a similar problem. Their stated wavelength is normally the cut point or 50% transmission wavelength. Glass and plastic filters are of the absorbtion type and don't have a sharp cutoff. From the 1% pass to 99% pass (99% of whatever maximum they have) is typically around 100nm wide.

A laser will have 99% of it's energy within 1 nm of it's peak wavelength. If you want a truly invisible illuminator with zero perceptable glow which will work with Gen2 and GenIII scopes there are two choices. One is a laser in the 880 to 905 wavelenght range (perhaps with a blocking filter to remove spurrious noncoherent emissions, the other is to use interference filters with an LED or white light source. These can be made with 10/90% cutoff width of a few nanometers, but they
are typically more expensive than lasers, especially in
large diameters.

If a narrow beam is desired a laser is the only practical solution.

880 nm glow?
by

The human eye cannot see 880 nm. Period. THe problem is that LED's which have a center wavelength of 880 nm have about 1% of their energy shorter than 800nm. LED's are NEVER monochromatic and typically have half power bandwidths of 50nm with typical bell shaped edges. The human eye has about 1 part in a million of it's sensitivity at 800 nm relative to it's peak green repsonse.

IR filters have a similar problem. Their stated wavelength is normally the cut point or 50% transmission wavelength. Glass and plastic filters are of the absorbtion type and don't have a sharp cutoff. From the 1% pass to 99% pass (99% of whatever maximum they have) is typically around 100nm wide.

A laser will have 99% of it's energy within 1 nm of it's peak wavelength. If you want a truly invisible illuminator with zero perceptable glow which will work with Gen2 and GenIII scopes there are two choices. One is a laser in the 880 to 905 wavelenght range (perhaps with a blocking filter to remove spurrious noncoherent emissions, the other is to use interference filters with an LED or white light source. These can be made with 10/90% cutoff width of a few nanometers, but they
are typically more expensive than lasers, especially in
large diameters.

If a narrow beam is desired a laser is the only practical solution.
