focus
by

As for fixed focus, I have not heard of anything for the PVS-7, that does not mean there is nothing, but since the unit focuses from extremely close to infinity, I doubt you would be able to find a lens system that would do that. Using an aperature control(pinhole cover) will increase the depth of field, but will also reduce the system gain. As for focusing on the cat, something is either wrong with the unit, or perhaps you have not learned to adjust it properly to your eyes? Study the manual on diopter adjusting and focusing. If you still cannot focus on a cat at 25 yards, something is wrong. You should be able to adjust for a nominal maximum depth of field by focusing at something around 4 or 5 yards away, and you will be able to see from about 2 or 3 yards to 100 yards. It will not be perfect, but you should be able to get a pretty good DOF at that approx adjustment.

don't worry Dave, temp color loss is normal
by

Dave what you've noticed with the eye that has been viewing through the NV device seeming to have a redder hue is normal. What you have done by viewing a steady green image is depleted the photo-receptor chemicals in your eye for seeing green. It takes a few moments for them to replentish after you stop viewing. You can cause the same effect by staring at a green circle on a white page for a minute and then looking at a pure white page. You will see a red residual spot the same size as the green spot you stared at until the photo chemicals replentish in your eye. There is no permanent damage or harm in this.

Thermal imaging in principal is far better than light amplification, but the high cost of high resolution thermal imaging equipment makes it impractical unless you use it for business. My Agema 595 thermal camera was over $50,000 but I built my gen 3 ANVIS 6 goggles with mostly new parts for $2000 and I prefer the view through the ANVIS 6 (very high resolution compared to the 640 x 480 digitally generated thermal camera image).

PVS-2 left mount.
by

You have to modify your mount by moving the carry handle plate to the right side, and removing the pin from the mount, and putting it on the other end of the rail.

Or you can try to swap the scope end-for-end, but that requires some machining of the mount itself, or the bottom of the scope (which is very soft! Be Careful!)

I know it is not much help, but my advice to you is to sell the PVS-2 on EBay for around $450, and find yourself a nice PVS-502.

The PVS-2 is crap compared to the 502. And I say that because the Elcan solution for the -2 is completely worthless. I had one, I know!

Even with the issue mount, you will have a terrible time hitting anything. I know, I had one, and I am right handed! Do not expect to shoot anything better than an 8" circle at 100 yards, if you are lucky!

Those external windage and elevation adjustments are not accurate at all, and I won't even get into the return to zero problems.

Here are the differences.

502 has reticle that is adjustable for brightness (a MUST on a NV weapons scope, period.) -2 does not.

502 has internal windage/elevation adjustments, same as PVS-4, -2 has worthless ELCAN, or external mil issue mount (not as bad - but damn close!)

502 has STANAG mounts, FAL mounts, M16 carry handle and flatop (ARMS #19 ~ thanks Lanny!) mounts, etc.
-2 has none (see above).

502 is lighter/smaller in size, mounts closer to the bore.

502 has a much better tube, performance is very close to PVS-4, price is 1/2 the cost! (502 runs $500-$600)

Fit and finish, user controls, etc. is much better on the 502 than the -2.

You can expect 1"-2" 5-shot groups at 100 yards all day long with the 502.

Just my $.02, but I would sell the -2, and then find a 502. If you can get $450 out of your -2, which is about the price they seem to sell for on ebay (a nice one with case and all acc.), then kicking in the extra $100-$200 is WELL worth it, believe me!

Gen III eyepieces
by

I've used Gen III tubes on astronomical telescopes, not the I3 in particular, but they give rather unimpressive results because of the long focal ratio of most astronomical scopes. Yes, it makes the fuzzy galaxies brighter but the loss of resolution on star images is annoying. Besides, since there's not much changing with time you get much more impressive results with an integrating CCD camera. Image intensifers used with the human eye just aren't used much in astronomy. Some intensified ccd's (fiber coupled) are useful for acquisition and guiding. Their nonlinearity and gain stability don't make them very useful as scientific detectors.

Regular 1x NV scopes are fun for watching meteor showers and satellites. Not of much scientific value though.

Waivers
by Sid

When I was thinking of buying 2nd Gen from Lanny I did a little bit of research into the US export laws and it wasn't very encouraging.

If I read it all correctly it went like this :-

Gen I is freely exportable without license.

Any device that ever had a military contract number and is Gen II or above is classified as a 'munition' according to the 'Munitions Control List' and needs US Department of Defense paperwork to export. Fat chance of that happening I suspect.....

Civilian Gen II or above can only be freely exported to Canada, export to other 'friendly' nations is restricted for reasons of 'non-proliferation' whatever the hell that means. This requires a license from the US Department of Commerce. What I would do and why...
by

I'm not an expert, but here's my two cents worth and a guess on it.

All three are good systems.

The 260 is six ounces or so heavier than the 220 and not headmountable. I don't know how well the 220 works headmounted, I'd imagine the PVS-7 may be a little better headmounted. The 260 uses electronic focus, the other two are manual. The PVS-7 will have a wider variety of accessories also, such as demist shields and sacrificial lenses. Not sure of anything like that for the other two. The 260 does not have a built in short range IR illuminator, the other two do.

The PVS-7s are excellent as well. They are four ounces heavier than the 220, but probably much more rugged. The 220 floats and is waterproof, the PVS-7's don't float, but should be fairly waterproof (depends on which version, the "C" version of it is waterproof to 20 meters. Most others are the one-meter-for-one hour military test). All three should stand up to driving rain or getting splashed.

One advantage of the 220 over the PVS-7's is adjustable gain. That may come in real handy at times.

The f-stop should be 1.4 on the 260 and 220 compared to 1.2 on the PVS-7, so the faster lens on the PVS-7 is a bit better. All are standard 1x lenses and offer magnification options.

The 260 is discontinued, but can still be found new in places on the web and some catalogs and stores. The 220 pretty much replaced it.

If you want to get into higher performance level tubes, the PVS-7 is the way to go as the 220 and 260 are only available with the 45 or 51 lp/mm tubes (as far as I know) and not with 64lp/mm OMNI IV or V tubes. Never heard of the 260 or 220 being available with tube or system data sheets to prove performance either. Overall the PVS-7 should be the better system, it certainly has the potential to be barring cost as a factor.

So I'd go with PVS-7's first, then the 220 and lastly the 260, assuming price is not much of a factor. If price is a factor the 260 should be cheaper, and maybe you can find one at a sweet price since they are no longer produced.

peep sights and night vision
by

While a "pinhole lens" does offer a greater depth of field than a larger lens looking through it, it does so by increasing the effective F ratio of the lens. If you try to look though a 1 millimeter rear rifle sight with a 25mm or larger diameter NV lens you aren't going to see either the front blade of the rifle or the target beyond. Well, you might see the target from the light going around the peep sight, but it will useless for aiming. Head position and alignment aren't going to help a bit. It's not that the image of the front blade insn't in focus or isn't present, it's that it will be too dim to see, even for a genIII tube in full starlight.

A fair portion of the gain of a night vision scope comes not from the electronic gain, but from the fact that a 1" lens takes in 16 times more light than a 1/4" dark adapted eye. It does that because the image tube can work with a much faster f ratio lens than the human eye.

Much of the reason you can't use peep sights naked eye in moonlight even though you can see the target or the front sight not looking though the peep is that you only get about 1/40th of the light though a 1mm peep sight as through your 1/4" dark adapted eye.
