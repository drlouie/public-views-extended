Re: Re: If the tube met the OMNI Spec, it would be...
by NightVision Fan

For the tubes the military buys, yes it is mandatory that the meet the current specifications, which requires that the tubes be tested and have a factory data sheet for them.

Many tubes in commercial systems are "fallout" tubes, meaning they have failed in one or more areas the military tests for, being COMSPEC instead. Multi LED arrays - range
by

In theory multiple LED's should increase the viewing
distance by the square root of the number of LEDs over a single LED of the same type. That rule will give equal surface brighness at the target and equal returned light per angular area. IE, the image will appear equally bright if it fills the screen. It does not consider atmospheric attenuation.

So figure 6 or 8.4 times the range for 36 and 72 LED's respectively. The individual LED in your viewer could have a higher or lower power than the ones in the array.

The square root rule doesn't work at long distances and high power levels where nearfield backscatter reduces the contrast and atmospheric scattering and absorbtion may be significant. For most locations backscatter is the most limiting. How far that is depends on the atmosphere at your location.

The LED arrays are good for wide field viewing with 1X scopes. You'll find a halogen spotlight with a filter gives longer range because the produce a more focused beam.

eye resolution and latest intensifiers.
by

The performance of the human eye doesn't translate directly into "line pair per millimeter".

It's generally accepted that a normal human eye can resolve a pair of lines separated by 1 arc minute. This test is sometimes done by asing a subject the orientation of a screen of equal width black and white lines. and common eye test charts do essentially the same thing.

Assume the eyepiece on a NV scope gives an apparent field of view of 60 degrees (about right but will vary with the design of the scope). and the tube gives 72 line pairs/mm then the line resolved line pairs would have an angular separation of:

72 l/mm * 18 mm = 1296 lines pairs across the screen

1296 lines/60 degrees field = 21.6 line pairs/degree

60/21.6 line pairs/degree - 2.8 arcminutes/line pair

The human eye's resolution in the center of the fovea
is about 2.8 times better. It's only the fact that the eye moves around the scene to allow specific objects to be viewed in detail. Outide of the fovea the human eye is at least ten times worse. The apparent resolution will still be about 2.8 times worse looking through a good quality low power riflescope or binoculars but it's still very good.

test targets
by

MIL-HDBK-141 "Military Standardization Handbook - Optical Design" has good information on the optical performance of human eyes. It has little on night vision equipment as it was published in 1962. It's still a fine reference.

I'd also recommend "Experimental Sensory Psychology" by Scharf,1975 for light reading about the performance limitations of human eyes. It covers other senses besides vision.

And a lot of technical information on night vision systems is available on line at:
http://www.usaarl.army.mil/hmdbook/cp_0001_forward.htm

Doing the tests yourself at home might not be precise numerically, but it's probably better than studying the literature for gaining an understanding of the principles and limitations of eyes and intensifiers.

Id suggest doing the tests in a dark room with a variable light source. One way to get a variable and repeatable attenuated light is to use a weak desk lamp and place layers of a folded white sheet over it. Varying the power to a light bulb doesn't work well as it changes the color. A standard eye chart would suffice. It's fast and easier to use than the USAF resolution chart. You can (somewhat) calibrate the relative light intensity on the screen. Doubling the distance of a small lamp from the chart cuts the surface brightness by a factor of four. Knowing that you can get an idea of the attenuation from each layer of cloth placed over the lamp.

It's entertaining to see how the resolution of the eye drops in low (dark adapted) light. And I agree that using the scope at 1x is the best measure of the tube's performance if you have that option.

by

That would depend on what you're trying to demonstrate. Did you do the test with various (especially very low) light levels?

Did you notice that if you move both the lamp and the scope you should see the chart considerably better in very low light at 10' with the 25mm lens than at 20' with the 50mm, but if you leave the lamp 10' from the chart they images should look about the same if you only move the scope between the lens changes? In brighter light it shouldn't make much difference where the lamp is located.

If there's enough light the 75mm f/1.3 should give better resolution at 20' than the 50mm at 20'. but as the light is reduced that may switch. It might even switch twice depending on the tube characteristics. At what light levels do you see with better resolution naked eye than through the scope with each lens? Can you stil see color (colored objects near the chart) at that light level?

There are a lot of experiments you can do to gain insight about the performance of eyes and intensifiers. Since these aren't formal tests the setup is only limited by imagination.

The thing which is not easy to set up is a stable calibrated light source so you actually know what light levels you're working with.
