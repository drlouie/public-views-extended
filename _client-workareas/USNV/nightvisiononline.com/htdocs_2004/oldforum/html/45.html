MrH is right,
by

I have a PVS-14 and a set of PVS-7s. The 14 in a headmount as a monocular works for walking around, but it's definitely something I had to practice at in uneven terrain.

With either one, using the normal Tritium night sights is tough, they're WAY too bright and if you focus on them the target is very hard to see. Either NVD with an IR laser on the weapon is a much more workable solution out to quite considerable ranges. A visible laser on the weapon doesn't work nearly as well, but is visible, barely.

If you were interested in using NV with a rifle there's another practical option, one of the red-dot sights which has night vision levels of brightness control. Aimpoint makes a good one that I use with the PVS-14 in a weapon mount directly behind it. The 14 can also be used mounted behind a conventional scope, but the field of view that's adequate in the daytime now has to be used to find the target as well as align the weapon, and it's a lot tougher.

The final option I like is one of the holo-sights with night-vision capable brightness controls on a handgun or rifle and either NVD in the headmount. It actually works tolerably well with practice with a handgun, still pretty clumsy with a rifle. Getting your head and the NVD aligned with the sight on a rifle is just really awkward.

My recommendation is buy what you want, try it out, and sell/trade to what your experiences suggest. These things do hold their value. You'll also probably get a lot of responses here with more info than I've offered.

Decisions
by

You will need to decide whether you want an eye "free" for peripheral use or just because you are more comfortable that way. If you have ski or dirt bike goggles try putting some black paper with a 3/4" hole for each eye, then walk around that way. Of course the darkness is another whole story, but lots of guys like one eye "free" for normal night vision while having a monocular on the one eye. Without the eyecups it is actually a lot nicer, as you don't feel as closed in, and you can see around the goggle.
As for handgunning with either using the tritium sight, it is very awkward and not very good at all. You cannot focus on the sight and anything more than 7 or so feet away at the same time. If that is your plan, better to invest in a small laser aimer and filter it, as guys have discussed, or you can use the IR stuff that is available to law enforcement (or to anyone from some dealers). The headgear is the same for the PVS-7 and 14, you can get all the same image tube specs for them. One thing with the monocular is that you will make better use of the tube,as it is basically a straight through light path, so the image will always look better through the mono.

Pulsed laser
by

To the first part of your question, the photocathode and electron multiplier have very fast response but the phosphor on a NV tube has a persistance in the order of 50 milliseconds. The human eye persistance is about that speed too. Some of the older tubes are a little slower. If you pulse a laser at 50 hz or faster you won't see any flicker. At 30 hz you might see a bit. At 10 hz you will see pulsation.

Read the specs on the laser carefully. The 34 watt rating of the LD68 M/A-Com laser is PEAK power. These lasers have a fairly low duty cycle. You won't get 1 watt average out of it. Perhaps 100mw or a little more, the specs shown don't say and I don't know. I have a couple of LD65 models (~12 watt rating). I paid $25 each for mine surplus but sealed in it's factory package. I plan to use them in a wind velocity meter which can operate with a 1:1000 duty cycle

The one on ebay is likely to be good but it doesn't sound like it's guaranteed and it is out of the package. Note the 1983 date on the box. The seller calles it "rare" but thats no doubt because they haven't been made for years. You'll probably have trouble finding data about duty cycle and power dissapation. I haven't found any for mine.

Most diode lasers don't handle pulses more than about 2 times their CW rating. The M/A-COM 904nm ones do have a much higher peak to average ratio than most. Their typical use was for time-of-flight rangefinders. It may make a decent illumintor but the requirement to pulse it makes it more difficult to use and it certainly won't meet the expectations you stated above.

A driver can be made with a few resistors, a capacitor, and an SCR or power MOSFET as a switch plus an oscillator to trigger it. You should be able to run it from small 12v battery. The capacitor has to put out 75 amps (or a little less) pulses but the battery doesn't. If you can get the laser cheap it might be fun to experiment with. Set up to produce 1 microsecond pulses at a 3khz rate you might get over 100 mw average with only a few watts drawn from the battery. It's also very easy to turn one of these into junk by over driving it.

You are right about the grainy look of lasers. Any stable monocrhomatic light source will do that. The advantage of lasers is they (most) have a very tiny emission area which can be focused with a small lens or slightly defocused to a small round spot. With appropriate optics there will be little off axis light. If you only illuminate a small area even a long range it doesn't take a lot of power.

One solution to the "grainy" problem is to use multiple wavelengths. Two lasers are very unlikly to be on exactly the same wavelegth so when the two beam add their patterns the fringes move around at such high speed the motion isn't visible. Two lasers will require two sets of optics to make them add at the target.

New (not surplus) CW lasers I've looked at with
reasonable prices which might be suitable for NV illuminators include:

Sanyo 785nm 80mw $68 Should lite up a 2 meter spot at 500 meters with gen II, 1000m with gen III. Probably slighty visible.

IMC 808nm 20 Watt $700 (line output 46 diodes) (Could easily light up a 50 meters of horizon at a mile for a gen II or III with an appropriate lens I'm not recommending it at all.)

Sanyo 830nm 100mw $323 (probably about ideal for a gen II or gen III spot illuminator though no brighter for gen II than the 785 nm 80mw unit. Should be totally invisible).

Thorlab 980nm 1 watt $210 (too far in the IR for gen II or III but would work great with the extended IR Gen III and some CCD cameras.

Another long range available with a fairly big spot is a MaxiBeam light. This is a short arc 70 watt xenon lamp in a portable spotlight size package. With an IR filter you'd have no trouble seeing with a decent gen II at a mile. It will give around 10 watts of IR. They cost about $1700.

Actually a flashlight with decent focus and an IR filter will give good illumination with a Gen II to 400 yards or more.

I like the Streamlight "Ultrastinger". It's just a tiny halogen bulb powered by nicads with a 2" parabolic reflector. That works quite well. It's about $90 with charger plus you need a filter. That should be about $50 for a good glass one. It puts out about a watt of IR in a <5 degree beam. Quite a bit of IR off axis too.

There are also high power LED's which are much cheaper than lasers. They're problem is that they have a fairly large emission area, larger even than some halogen bulb filaments. They do put out IR only but typically over a band from 750 to 950 nm, peaking between 850 to 880 nm. To make that into a bright spot at 400 yards requires about a 4" fast lens or paraboliic mirror. The LED;s are fairly cheap. 100mw for about $10. There are some 1/2 watt and higher LED's on the market but they are usually multiple chips presenting even a worse problem in trying to focus them to a reasonable spot at several hundred yards. You could use several of single chip ones with a 30mm or so lens aimed at the same spot. If compact is the objective you'll do better with a laser. The LED's are the way to go if you want a 5 to 10 degree beam for viewing out to a couple of hundred meters. They're very easy to use and efficient.

Any of these IR light sources can be unsafe to look at if focused or collimated, or directly at close range. The higher power ones could cause blindness and not just the lasers. By using them in a diverging mode only with moderatly large beams none present an extreme hazard. If you build one of these treat it with respect. Understand the energy densities involved and the potential of danger from reflections. Eye protection from IR light at these levels is inexpensive and available.

gen 1 @ 850nm
by

A gen 1 or gen II tube (normally) has an S20 multialkali photocathode. That has it's peak response about 470 nm and falls off fairly smoothly to near zero around 1000nm. A gen I or II tube has about 30% of it's peak sensitivity at 850nm.

If you want a broad beam and plenty of IR light you'll probably get better illumination with the standard Maglite bulb and a filter which cuts around 790 nm. 78mw is a lot for a collimated laser pointer but it isn't much as an illuminator. The LED may give less battery drain. That's the only advantage I can see.

Even 880nm LED's have a visible glow. The stated wavelength is the peak and unlike a laser the spectral range of an LED is pretty broad. I don't the specs of your diode but some 100mw 880 nm diodes are only down to 10% ouput at 780nm and 970nm. (per http://www.optodiode.com). One with a peak at 850 will probably be more visible.

Three stage Gen 1 design practice
by

I have a question regarding the symptom of a bright emmission of light blooming forth from the top corner of the phosphor screen, and question whether or not it may have originated from my assembly techniques... If nothing else, the following experience has led to my interest in NV.

One cold Winter night, About 7 or 8 years ago, I was lying in bed suffering from insomnia, reading a copy of 'Popular Electronics', when I came across an advertisment entitled "Build Your Own Night Vision". Seeing that the company was located in Australia (I think it was Oakley electronics), and I was wide awake at 4 AM, I ordered what turned out to be a used three stage US Gen 1 tube assembly, and a handfull of high voltage diodes and capacitors for making a 18 kV power supply. I assembled the components, mounting the tube in a 3 inch PVC pipe, with lenses etc., and mounting the power supply below. I used high voltage rated wire from the power supply to each tube. Since I saw no good way to solder onto the metal contact bands around the tube, I wire wrapped the cathode and anode connections, and taped over each with PVC. 2 possible gen1 stack light bloom problem causes....
by

I can only think of two possible causes to the "light blooming" problem you describe. One would be very easy to rule in or out. It is if the high voltage tube connection is near where you are seeing this "light bloom", the tube is probably picking up the corona discharge from the HV terminal. You have to keep in mind though that this point could be inverted through the image intensifiers.

The best fix for this is to go to a TV repair shop or an electronics parts supply store and buy a bottle of corona dope. It's like a thick browny-red nail polish, and is used to stop corona leaks around the HV components in TV's or video monitors. Paint all metal parts of the tubes that are at HV potential. If you get any on areas you don't want, it will clean up easily before it dries with alcohol. This stuff will stop all HV corona emissions better than RTV silicone, because it coats better, and traps no air pockets or channels where HV can worm it's way out of.

The other thing that could be causing the problem is you might have a slightly flakey tube in the stack and be a little over voltage with your HV supply. I have seen the problem myself when I used to build gen 0 IR viewers 20 years ago. It's like once you exceed a certain point in HV, there will be a constant electron flow (break down) inside the tube, and this will cause a spot to be lit up on the output window. I used to just keep adding 1 meg ohm 1/8 watt resistors in series with the HV feed until the tube settled down. I just used the ordinary common resistors you get at Radio Shack, and not the fancy expensive HV ones and they worked fine. I suspect that you may just have too much HV, since the problem clears itself up as soon as you kill the juice and the excessive electron flow in the flakey tube stops instantly. It was probably this that caused the stack you have to be pulled and surplused in the first place.

Good luck with your project,

Noise in NV equipemnt.
by

When light strikes the photocathode of an intensifier electrons are freed from the surface. At a given wavelength light has a minimum energy with which it can interact with an electron. That energy is called a photon. It has no rest mass. It's not like a little billiard ball as it has wave properties too. Even though it has enough energy, each photon does not necessarily knock loose one photon. Over a range of wavelength which a photocathode is sensitive it can be rated as to it's quantum efficiency. S20 cathodes for example have a peak quantum efficiency of around 25 percent in the blue-green falling smoothly to near zero around 1100nm, well into the IR. A GaAs photocathode has 40 to 50% QE which is fairly constant out to 950nm where it then falls rapidly. For the s20 that doesn't mean it takes 4 photons to knock loose one electron, only that about 1 in four succeed in doing it. Some CCDs have QE's of over 90% at some wavelengths, and if exposed to shortwave UV or Xrays some detectors can have more than one electron released by a single photon giving a QE of over 100 percent.

Electrons are also released just by the thermal vibration of the photocathode molecules. At room temperature thats most of the the weak grainy and "crawly" looking background noise you see when you look at a high quality tube in complete darkeness. That can be reduced by cooling the tube, but NV equipment is designed so it's not bothersome. GaAs has a little less thermal noise than S20. There are materials better than GaAs for low noise but they don't have the good infrared response.

With an old tube there may be some gas in the tube and the gas ions hitting the cathode or multiplier channels can release electrons. That's what causes most of the brighter sparkles you see in old tubes as each collision can release a number of electrons.

After my first post.....
by

I considered that that but it didn't make sense to me. The only intensifiers which are red sensitive only (without good green sensitivity) are the Gen III's with GaAs photocathodes which are filtered specifically not to have visible sensitivity. Inherently a Gen III tube's spectral reponse is quite flat from about 330nm (near ultraviolet and limited by the glass window) to about 950 nm (near infrared and limited by the nature of GaAs). To not have green sensitivity something has to be added to block it. GaAs is use because it has the best quantum efficiency in the near IR of any practical photoemissive material. Some (most?) military Gen III intensifiers are blocked from yellow through ultraviolet. I'm not sure of all the reasons for doing that, no doubt for some performance improvement in military conditions. The spectral range specs on a particular tube show if it's blocked.

Gen II and III intensifiers already have as much gain as is practical to use. When simply increasing the voltage a little brings the cathode thermal noise up to the point where the phosphor glows adding another tube would just give a bright screen from the first tube's thermal noise. A gen I or gen II tube (S20 response) has it's peak sensitivity in the mid visible anyway (green).

It might be possible to use two stages of intensifcation and get some benefit if the first stage could be made to have very low noise. With GaAs or S20 multialkali that means cryogenic cooling in the order of -40 to -80 degrees C. You can get that from multistage thermoelectric cooling or mechanical refigeration. Even then the gain available in a single tube by increasing the voltage would probably be sufficient. Intensifiers used in astronomy were typically cooled. They aren't commonly used any more since thinned rear illuminated CCDS have lower noise, better quantum efficiency, less geometric distortion, better linearity and wider spectral response. The parameter where intensifiers are better is response time. That sort of a CCD however would not be very practical for an NV scope and it would resemble a thermal view (in complexity) if one were used.

Another problem is that except for the Gen1's made specifically for the purpose, few intensifiers can be physically stacked because they don't have flat fiber optic anode and cathode windows which can be put in direct physical contact. To use lens coupling either results in inefficient coupling or an very expensive lens. The use of lenses will make a very bulky assembly.

Anyway, a few red phosphored intensifiers may have been build as experiments, perhaps to see if it would help preserve dark adaptation for the user. I'd be surprised if designers haven't tried it. To my knowledge though none are commonly manufactured or available, at least I've never seen one in manufactures spec sheets. To have one custom made would be extremely expensive.

Lithium batteries - batter drain
by

An AN/PVS-5 is rated about 30 hours on a pair of AA cells. It uses two MX9916 tubes. A pair of AA alkaline (to give 3 volts) cells is rated about 1500 mahr depending on the brand. Lithium "coin" cells come in a wide variety sizes and ratings. The largest that's commonly available is the Panasonic CR2477 which costs under $3.00, is 1.00" by 0.276" thick and is rated 3.0V at 1000mahr. One of those should give in excess of 30 hours operation with a single MX9916 tube.

It might be of interest that I've come up with a way to use the CR2477 cell to fit in the standard BA-5576/U holder compartment of a PVS-4 or TVS-5. It takes a different spring, a nylon washer, and a strip of 1 mil mylar. The BA-5576/U has a rating of 800mahr so the Panasonic cell gives about the same life. The Panasonic cell may not meet the Mil Specs of 175 G acceleration, 50,000 ft altitude, and -20F to +130F operation which the BA-5567/U does. The Panasonic cell is about half the size and weight of the BA-5567/U. The conversion is a drop in requiring no machining and no modification of the scope's battery compartment. The parts to make 3 units (with pieces left over) costs about $20 from McMaster. The tools requireded are a pair of scissors, a pair of pliers, and under 5 minutes of time.

If anyone is interested I'll give more complete details. I don't want to be in the business of making kits. I don't know if this method will work on the several other NV scopes which use the BA-5567U since I don't have access to them to them to try.

Light on photocathode
by

Bright light on the photocathode of an intensifier can damage the cathode with the tube on or off. It can damage the phosphor on the output size only with the tube powered. Damage to the cathode can take place almost instantaniously with direct sunlight, laser pulse, flash bulb or strobe. Such damage can leave a dark or light spot or possible kill the tube completely. Damage to the phosphor is almost always just the dimming of an area. Sweeping the view across a light will sometimes leave a "trail" but that's usually temporary. Leaving an intensifier looking at a moderately bright night scene, such a fixed intensifier on a camera in a warehouse will eventually "burn in" the image on the cathode. That could take days or weeks. As a good rule of thumb, don't look a anything with your intensifier that gives a spot bright enough to "bloom" at all. By bloom I mean that the image will be larger on the phosphor than it would be if it were dimmer. Don't look at any light source for an extended period of time.

It's mostly just common sense. You can clean your equipment in a moderately illuminated room but I'd be careful not to point the objective directly at a lightbulb. Don't be too anxious to remove the dust. Careless cleaning is much more damaging to optics than leaving the dust on them. Wiping dusty optics with a clean cloth or lens paper is about the worst thing you can do. Proper cleaning of optics warrants a separate discussion.

Cleaning optics.
by

First and most obvious although often ignored is to not get the optics dirty in the first place. At least no more than necessary. That means:

Keep lenses covered when not in use.
Keep out of rain/snow/blowing dust when possible.
Prevent condensing moisture which will desolve dust and stick it to the lenses. Dust sitting on a lens generally won't hurt it or the coatings, but if it's disolved in water some dust may be alkaline or acidic and etch the surfaces. Moisture with dust left on optical surfaces for an extended period of time does more damage to optics than anything else. Avoid pollen which isn't just dusty but also oily and sticky. Don't touch optics with your to prevent body oil from getting on the lenses. Eyelashes on eyepieces are a common problem.

The idea of cleaning is to remove unwanted material without harming the surfaces. Most optical surfaces are fairly hard, but a lot of the particles in dust are even harder. Most material which you'll find on lenses other than dust is organic based. It will usually be soluable in water, alcohol, or acetone. Glass and coated optics won't be harmed by those but they can spread oils around without removing them. Acetone can ruin plastics.

You need to know the optical material to know if they can stand the solvents. Usually its not the lenses that's the problem, it's the housings and seals. Most military optics can be cleaned with soapy water without damage.

Here's the process I use on my telescope mirors and other external optical surfaces of my astronomical telescopes. These are typically $30,000 pieces of precision low expansion glass with aluminized and SiO overcoated surfaces. They are at least as delicate as typical NV optics. These mirrors have to sit out all night every reasonably clear night and they get dirty from whatever is in the air which settles on them. I clean them about every two months, or when their reflectivity drops by 5%. Cleaning more often than necessary is just causes excess wear.

The process is to first flood them with a weak solvent and water. I use a mix of 10% windex (yep, common grocery store windex) and 90% distilled water. The surface doesn't get blasted. just a gentle "rain" from a spray bottle. I set the optics so they face down and spray upward. THat way the dirt ladened water falls from the glass rather than sliding over it. This spraying goes on for a couple of minutes giving stuck particles time to disolve. Then I spray the surface with distilled water only, let most of that drain off then blow it dry with a stream of air filtered to be free from oil, dust, or water. The idea is to knock the water off rather than letting it evaporate. I then inspect the mirror carefully for anything which didn't wash off. If there are spots from insect or bird droppings I may attempt to remove that with more water or use alcohol, or acetone. On rare occasons I'll use a small soft brush plus plenty of water to wash away any residue but I'm caredful not to touch any of the mirror with the brush that isn't contaminated already. It can only do more damage. Ocasonally something will actually damage a spot on the surface. It's best to just leave those alone since the damage is already done. Some of the mirrors have been in continuous use for over 20 years and still don't need recoating. Optics which have damaged coatings can be stripped and recoated if the glass underneath hasn't been damaged. It's not cheap since it requires a vacuum chamber and special equipment to monitor the coating thickensses. The coatings aren't easy to remove either.

Water is the best universal solvent and its cheap enough to use in large quantities. Acetone is my second favorie as it is very pure by the nature of the way it's manufactured, is great at desolving oil and grease. When it evaporates though whatever is suspended in it doesn't evaporate, so you have to actually wash the acetone with the grease or oil away rather than letting it evaporate. Acetone and water mix. Alcohol will desolve a few things that water or acetone don't, but I find it's more likely to leave a residue than water or acetone.

I don't like to blow dust off of mirrors or lenses with air or bottled freon. For just the light dust found in a house its ok if done gently, but for the "desert sand" kind of dust it can just be a form of sandblasting. I find liquids are gentler. Some observatories use CO2 to clean large mirrors. It's about the only thing which works well on bare aluminum mirrors with no coatings. The method is to aim a stream of liquid CO2 from a tank at the mirror and let the point where it changes to the gas phase just contact the surface. The rapidly expansion of the "boiling" CO2 lifts the dirt particles away from the mirror with little tendancy to drive the particles against the surface.

I cringe every time I see lens paper. It can be used after dust has been removed to remove oils, particularly by soaking it with a solvent and lightly draging it across the optical surface, but too many people use it to wipe away dust. You might as well use a piece of sandpaper. Lens brushes aren't so bad if used gently. Don't touch the brush as it can lay down body oil like a paintbrush.

To really clean optics well, partecularly internal surfaces, requires a clean enviroment. Just because you see a couple of pieces of dust beind the lens or you NV scope avoid the temptation to remove it. Think about the surface area of the dust particles compared to the area of the lens. You can live just fine with some dust particles. On the other hand if you mess up the coatings you may lose several percent of the light even if the surface is dust free.

Bright light on NV tubes.
by

The problem isn't the photocathode not having enough electrons to give up. It's that bright light and heat can damage the microscopicly thin photocathode material. It can change the molecular struture of either the multialkalai cathode of gen 1 or II or the galium arsinide cathode of a gen III. There are many materials which if you leave them in sunlight they will deteriorate and if you focus the sun on them with a large magnifier they'll be damaged almost instananeously. For most common objects the deterioration of a tiny amount of surface material doesn't matter. For an image tube cathode that's all there is.

The phosphors on NV equipment deteriorate primarily from the bombardment of electrons. The rate of electron impact times the time determines the total loss of sensitivity with time. There are 8760 hours in a year and most gen III tubes are rated for 10,000 hours of normal use. But a few minutes of all the energy the tubes power supply can deliver beating on a small spot is enough to cause similar deterioration. The latter will be more annoying because it will be a spot on the image rather than a uniform dimming of the whole image. That why having a laser pointer attached to a weapon so the bright spot is always in one place is bad for the tubes.

Sunlight can damage the phosphors if it's allowed to shine in the eyepiece end of NV equipment since the eyepiece will focus the sun to a tiny hot spot on the phosphor.

Without the optics in place intensifier tubes are less likely to be damaged by bright lights than after they're installed in the equipment. Chris's statement about letting them sit under room light during assembly isn't surprising. I use both GaAs and S20e photomultipliers in my telesocopes. Their photocathodes are similar to those in intensifiers. They can be handled in normal room lighting with no problem. They take a few hours to recover once their put in service in low light conditions but no permanent damage occurs even if they're exposed briefly to the daytime sky. I'd never let direct sunlight, even unfocused, fall on them.

LIF filters
by

Lanny, before your first post I'd never heard the term "LIF" so I didn't respond. On this post I did a bit of a web search and found it means "Laser Interference Filter". This site doesn't tell how they work but it does tell what they do:

http://www.littoneos.com/pdf_library/o_lif.pdf

From the name and that bit of info I think I can deduce
what they are and how they work, but not the exact details.

An interference filter is made by depositing layers of transparent material with two very different refractive indexes on a substrate with very tightly controlled thicknesses. The substrate can be glass, quartz, or similar. As light (or IR or UV) passes through the layers the reflection from the boundries can be additive or subtractive as a function of wavelength and the thickness of each layer. Think about the colors you see from an oil film on water. The colors come from the fact the oil is not of uniform thickness and the fact that oil and water have different refractive indexes. That's a very simple one layer interferece filter with less than optimum dielectric materials.

Properly made interference filters can be better reflectors than the best metal mirrors and can give better transmission than a bare glass window. Anti-reflective coatings are a simple form of an interference fiter. Interference filters don't absorb energy, at least very little. THey either pass a wavelength or reflect it. They can however be combined with absorbtive filters and an absorbtive filter can even be used as the substrate for the interference filter. There are two advantages of interference filters over absorbtion filters. First they can handle very high power levels since the energy can be bounced back toward the source. Second, they can be made to have vary narrow absorbtion or reflection bands to control specific wavelengths. The disadvantage of interference filters is they're expensive to make and they typically "leak" at wavelenghts other than the one you're interested in. The advantage of absorbtion filters is that they are usually cheaper, have very broad characteristics which extend way into the UV and far IR. They are commonly used together. The cost of interference is in the setup and the required equipment. If the filters are made in large quantities they can be inexpensive. If you look at an interference filter it usually looks like a mirror, perhaps a colored one. Looking though it it may have color, be opaque, or transparent depending on what wavelenghts it passes or blocks. It may look like a mirror from one side and opaque or colored on the other if it's bonded to an absorbtion filter.
This website gives a look into how real interference filters are constructed:

http://www.thermo.com/eThermo/CDA/Support/Support_Detail/0,1153,15845-127,00.html?

In my astronomy work I use interference and absorbtion filters to sort out bands of light to help determine the temperatures and other characteriscs of the stars.

So what is a LIF? High power lasers can damage eyes by focusing pulses of energy onto the retina. In the same manner high power laser light focused by the objective of a NV scope can damage an intensifier or a thermal array or whatever the sensor might be. LIF is a generic term for filters which use interference (plus absorbtion) to stop the common laser wavelengths.

What are the common lasers found on a battlefield? For non-nato countries by far the most common is 1064nm from a YAG laser. It's easy to build a very high power (multi megawatt) pulse laser at that wavelength. YAG work great for rangefinders to 30Km or so. They aren't visible to either the human eye or (most) Gen III scopes. They are visible to GEN 0,1,and 2 scopes, though they're not very sensitive that far into the IR. A typical YAG rangefinder, even a handheld one, can put out a 100,000 watt pulse. I's not long duration so the total energy is low, but it's enough to damage a human eye or damage an intensifier with a direct hit.
There are some Gen III systems specifically designed to be able to see light from a YAG laser. Check out:

http://www.littoneos.com/pdf_library/s_pvs-13.pdf

What are other common high power laser wavelengths? The US is converting to erbium lasers for their rangefinders. They have similar characteristics to YAG except for a longer wavelength (about 1.5 microns) They are considered eyesafe (at least a lot safer) but can still damage intensifiers. There are also some old ruby rangefinders around which are visible in the deep red and very bad for eyes and intensifiers as they typically worked at high power than even the YAG's. YAG can be frequency doubled to produce an intense green light at 532nm. Perhaps you've seen the police "blinding" lasers. They are intended to disorient a person without actually producing permanent damage. They only have medium power, a watt or so.
That would be another possible wavelength to block. I don't know which of those, or others, a LIF actully blocks. It would be interesting to test one on a spectrophotometer. I couldn't find a web site with that information.

So do you need a LIF? That depends on who you're going to look at with your NV equipment and what equipment they have. It will not protect your scope from a broadband light source like the sun or a xenon arc or strobe. They will block a little useful light so the image in the scope will be slightly dimmer. I don't know how much.

As I say, I pulled this together from what I read on the web today and my knowledge of interference filters in general. I may have some points about LIF wrong.
