NVPS-10
by

The NVPS-10 is compact and well made. I bought mine used at a gunshow for $450 though it had been dropped and the objective was broken. I replaced it with 25mm f/0.95 and 75mm f/1.3 lenses from Kogaku, both of which give nice results at 1x and 3x respectively.
I'd highly recommend the 75mm Kogaku lens if you're going to use this mounted on a rifle. If you're trying to use it to walk around at night the 25mm is more desirable. Most "C-mount" camera lenses will fit it.

The only problem I see with that setup is you'll need to solidly mount the NVPS-10 to your rifle plus the laser pointer. Trying to hold the monocular to your eye with one hand and shoot with the other isn't going to give very satisfactory results. You might be able to head mount the NVPS-10, I haven't tried. I don't think that would be a very good setup either.

For 200 yards a cheap red laser rifle pointer will produce a brighter spot than you need. Through an intensifier the 635nm units aren't any brighter than the cheaper 650 and 670 nm ones. You only need about 1 milliwatt. You'll probably want about a watt for an illuminator. An "ultrastinger" flaslight with an IR filter makes a good illuminator with the genII to over 200 yards. A plastic filter with a cut wavelength of 800 to 850 nm will do. It will have a red glow, but it won't be noticable beyond 20 yards or so. Some of the Russian LED illuminators are good too. 100mw of IR would be about the low limit I'd want. Using a laser as an illuminator is a waste of money and only makes sense if you truly want no visible light leakage.

The built in IR LED on the NVPS-10 works fine for reading maps or looking around inside a room but it doesn't provide useful illumination at even 50 yards.

Mounting
by

that is about the standard price, I have heard of guys finding them a bit less once in a while, usually if they know someone who is a distributor. The main reason to mount behind the sight is because you run into a sort of parallax error in the line of sight. Your impact point becomes an image on the screen of the NV device, and if this shifts only very slightly, the image impact point and the actual will be off by an amazing distance. There is very little you can do, other than make a mount for the PVS-14 or whatever that absolutely zeros and stays put, which is difficult since it mounts to the unit with only one screw. When it is behind the aimpoint, none of that matters since your impact point is fixed in the optical sight itself.

Glass
by

Speed of the lens is it's ability to collect and transmit light. Several things contribute to this ability, the most important being diameter. An f-stop is the focal ratio. Focal ratio is the focal length divided by it's diameter. Longer focal lengths reduce light unless they have a bigger diameter. A 25mm focal length lens with a focal ratio of 1.3 is a 19mm diameter lens. A 50mm focal length lens with a focal ratio of 1.3 is a 38mm diameter lens. Notice the area of the smaller circle is 283 while the larger is 1133 roughly. These lenses are equally "fast" while one is much larger than the other. Coatings also affect the ability of a lens to pass light by reducing the amount of light reflected in an air to glass surface. By adding a coating an advertiser could call it faster although it doesn't have as much effect as increased diameter. (much cheaper than diameter too)
While focal length is a general inication of power, the size of the format has to be considered too. A 50 mm focal length lens on a 35mm camera is Thought of as unity (normal) In most night vision devices this same focal length is considered about a 2 power telephoto. Smaller format. Focal length combined with format size affect depth of field described by circles of confusion and I will leave that one alone. Hope this helps.

Laser makes gen 1 better than gen II
by

First it depends on the Gen 1 and gen 2 device.
I have an AN/TVS-4 which is a 3 stage gen 1. For sensitivity and resolution (though not image distortion) it stomps on my Gen II AN/PVS-4. Ok, it weighs ten times more and is huge.

As to lasers, a laser is no better than an equivalent power LED or filtered incadescent bulb if the light is spread over the same area as an illuminator. The place lasers shine (pun intended) is when used to obtain a very narrow beam which an led or incandescent light simply cannot do. 90 milliwatts is less total IR power than a mag light flashlight with a filter puts out so it won't be any brighter when spread to the same beam diamter.

Sure, the laser and a gen 1 is a cheap alternative to a gen II or even a gen III, But a gen 1 and a $30 "million candlepower" spot light with a $30 IR filter will do even better. Actually a $30 spotlight without the filter and without the gen 1 using just your eyeball will allow you to see at night even better than that!

NV Book
by franco

I always knew that the ion barrier film is basically a sort of insulator, stretched over the MCP 'tween the MCP itself and the PhotoK.
It is less than 100 atoms thick, and is made of aluminium or magnesium oxide.
It's purpose is that of stopping accelerated ions from impacting the GaAs, chemically "poison" it, thus destroying its effectiveness.
An amazingly little amount of gas is enough for ionization to occur.
But it also "kills" over 50% of all electrons released by the PhotoK, especially the "slow" electrons, so basically the photosensitivity of the PhotoK is reduced to half, since this is the amount of electrons that do get amplified by the MCP.
A filmless tube restores the full photosensitivity of the GaAs, as all the electrons reach the MCP and get amplified.
Also, removing the film reduces drastically the halo.
At the same time, you still have to protect the GaAs; how do you do it?!

I am absolutely NOT sure of any of the above, this is why I posted the original message asking for more info.
