Used them all
by

241K has the best tube performance, PVS-4 has the best optics, reticle etc. New price for new price, 241K is easily the best deal. If you can find a really nice, clean, good working PVS-4($1000-$1300) that is not a bad choice either, however the tubes in those vary greatly, 28lp/mm is the resolution, they are noisy too. The 242 is similar in size and design to the PVS-4, using the same tube, however it is a commercial scope, also discontinued. If there are any left you could get a really good price, however I was not at all impressed with the performance of them. Overall they are ok, again, tubes vary and they do work ok, especially for the close-out price. The 241K will have 36lp/mm and better sig/noise than the other two, also much smaller and lighter. Slightly less magnification, but it does have a built in IR which neither of the others has standard, and they can all use it.

Knightscope Model 007
by

The Knightscope is in fact in production, but all units are going to fill a contract with SOCOM. Civilian sales will probably not happen for some time, and I doubt they will be available in large numbers. I have heard of pricing in the $7K range.

The device should NOT be compared to a PVS-14. The sight was designed to allow use as a pocketscope/viewer or (primarily) to augment a day scope. The unit has a proprietary system that aligns the tube and optics front-to-rear to provide zero POI/POA shift, even mounted off-axis by a few degrees. It also has "infinite" eye relief, in that you don't have to hold your eye right up to the eyepiece.

Like any other NV device, this is a tool, and it was designed with a high degree of user input (That is to say, Operators, not people who post on a discussion board) Some users may opt for this unit, others a Raptor, PVS-10, etc. It depends on the user and his task or mission.

By the way, www.knightsarmament.com is not a website controlled or endorsed by KAC, it belongs to some "internet entrepeneur".

C CS mount lenses
by

Both the CS and C type lens mounts use a 1" x 32 TPI thread. The difference is that the C mount is 0.69"(17.625mm) from the face to the image plane while the CS is 0.4921" (12.5mm).

Unfortunately there is no specification of how the chamber behind the lens (but in front of the image plane) must be shaped. If you screw a CS to C adapter into a CS mount it obviously has to make the chamber smaller in diameter. It's difficult to design a very fast short focus lens for a C mount which does not have a rear protrusion. Many CCD cameras use the CS front flange and ship with a C-CS adapter, and those very short very fast lenses just won't fit.

C mount is an old design which was widely used on 16mm
hi end film cameras (like Bolex) since the 1940's. (earlier perhaps, I don't know). Manufacturers of NV
gear SHOULD be aware of the problem, and there's no reason it should be a problem, but it is.

Fujinon (Fuji?) makes an f0.85 25mm lens which won't fit in a CS adapter. It won't fit my NVPS-10 either. Same for the rare and expensive Fujinon F/0.7 50mm lens. I have a JAI low light camera which is "true C mount" and accepts both lenses nicely.

The things you have to watch when buying a C mount
lens include:

1. Does it have a broad wavelenght range. Most all
lens will pass the full range that NV equipment is
sensitive to, (300 to 1000nm) but they don't necessarily have the same focus or geometry across that
wide of wavelenght range. Lenses designed for film cameras only needed to work from about 400 to 650 nm.
Many CCD cameras have IR blocking filters to give crisper and more natural images in the daytime, but that's the last thing you want for trying to observe in starlight.

2. Does the lens give adequate field coverage and low geometric distortion. Fast short focus lenses often suffer from a "pincushion" effect, or simply have crummy focus off axis. It's hard to tell from the specs
which are rarely complete wheter a lens will be good for NV use. You just have to try them. Looking at the sky is a good test. If the bright stars look like "points" across the field without halos then the wavelength and focus is good. Then look a rectangular buildings and see if the sides and tops look straight. That's the quick test for "pincushion". Sweeping the scope across a scene may show some secondary distortions which aren't noticible when viewing normally, but they can generally be lived with.

3. Is the physical characteristics of the lens. Is it too bulky to use. Is it too easy to accidelty change the focus or f/stop. Does it screw into your NV unit. (the problem which started this discussion). Again, the specs probably won't tell you that.

The discussions about what lenses work well with what equipment on this forum have been good, but they haven't come close to covering all the possible combinations of lenses and NV scopes/cameras.

Underwater night vision glasses (UW NVG): the principle of build-up and range calculation

Making of devices of observation under water represents more complicated problem in comparison with instruments of terrestrial application as character of image generation (forming) in the turbid water and the Earth atmospheric mediums essentially differs.
At optimum system making for observation in water mediums it is necessary to take into account these differences and features to the full measure:
1. As a result of the strong attenuation of radiation by water medium in fundamental due to absorption, the visibility range does not exceed 50 m.
2. Due to selective absorption of radiation the turbid medium essentially changes a spectral content of radiation narrowing it up to 0.4-0.6 microns and cutting off an IR range of spectrum. It determines an optimum range of Night Vision Devices (NVD) operation in water.
3. Reflection ability of a surface of various materials at their dipping in water decreases, especially for dielectrics, approximately by an order.
4. Difference of refractive coefficient of water from 1 in case of application of a normal lens with an illuminator leads to variation of dimensional and aberrational characteristics of the lens. The lens in water has an accessory distortion, which exceeds 15%, the field of view decreases by 25% and a relative aperture by 45%. In water the focusing of lens for the subjects situated at a terminating distance is changed. Therefore special hydro lenses are applied to exclude these distortions.
5. Water represents by itself a scattering medium (hydrosol) and a nonuniform medium with random distribution of refractive index (as in turbulent medium). These features of water medium influence on the picture quality and should be taken into account at preliminary computations through the average empirical formula of a contrast-frequency characteristic of the sea.
6. Aggression of sea medium demands application for a NVD housing of special materials - composites or special protective covers.
7. The optimum scheme of building-up of night vision glasses (NVG) in terrestrial conditions is pseudo-binocular scheme. In water mediums with a view of the divers safety the monocular scheme of the UW NVG building-up is more expedient.
8. An essential problem is ensuring of hermeticity both the instrument and control equipment and also alignment of UW NVG with diving mask. The optimum decision is making a special mask with the built-in it instrument that provides distance of an eyepiece target pupil no more than 25 mm. In case of UW NVG jointing with a regular mask (outside of it) the distance of the target pupil exceeds 50 mm that essentially magnifies an overall dimensions of the eyepiece.
In the a construction of the underwater night complex consisting of a night monocular with magnifying of one-multiple (1x ), an illuminator designed on the basis of a light-emitting diode module, a diving mask such as RTI and a system of fastening of the monocular to the mask is developed. The underwater night monocular includes: the Gidrobizar-2 hydro lens (f'=25 mm, O=1:1.3), an image intensifier tube of GEN 2+ or 3, an eyepiece (f'=25 mm, distance of a target pupil is 55 mm), a built-in illuminator on the basis of a light emitting diode and a built-in unit of power supply (U=3 V).
Calculation of the designed UW NVGs range of working was carried out by the NVD calculating procedure applied in the which is taking into account influence of parameters of water on power (energy) characteristics and image quality of the watched objects. At the same time the geometrical distortions imported by water are not taken into account, as for their compensation in the Gidrobizar-2 lens the special optical elements are included. The carried out calculations of UW NVGs working range of the passive-and-active type have shown a considerable opportunity expansion for observation in water medium at low illumination levels.

Actually
by

I don't know if anyone here makes them, but I believe the US type of gen 1 tube is still manufactured. They work much better for several reasons, of course they were mostly 3 stage being the obvious one. Even 30 year old 3 stage gen 1 high quality intensifiers are an improvement over any of the Russian gen 1 that I have seen. The US type tubes used a fiber optic input and output windows that were curved on the inside so as to minimize peripheral distortion inherent in electrost inversion focused tubes of that type. They had a much more precise method of focus also. They were also not as fragile as the little glass tubes with the "pod" inside on a glass pedestal. Perhaps some of the Russian devices in gen 1 utilize this type of tube now, but I have not seen one. Of course with the 3 stage US units, overall performance is greatly improved, and by using that method, noise is minimized by controlling the light the gain gradually rather than trying to get it all out of one tube. At least that is my understanding of it all. I suppose I could be completey wrong though?
