Objective Lens, Reticle Cell Assembly, Mount assembly, variable range adjustments, Purge, Recticle C
by

1. Objective Lens: Not all lens assemblies have the same quality of glass thickness, adhesive of the glass to the walls of the scope, and if using a catadiptic lens, then the adhesive on the catadioptic. Some lenses are just fine for a .223 round, but will shatter with a 30-06.

2. A mount that is made for a specific scope shooting a specific round. Some mounts just can't handle the weight of the scope and the recoil without bending or shifting.

3. Any ranging "rings", "tubes" or "barrels" in the objective lens could shift or go off track. This goes for f/stop barrels as well!

4. Will the scope body and Objective lens / eyepiece assembly maintain its integrity (seal) during repeated recoil?

5. Finally, and most importantly, our repair department has taken apart countless rifle scopes of all different types. There is a huge variation in reticle cell assembly construction. Some use quality brass washers and feed screws, others use plastic, some use quality springs or other absorbant material, others will use cardboard (!). Very very few reticile cells, are constructed solidly enough (because it is expensive to do so) to withstand repeated blast, and still remain in place and not "walk" while firing. Or, some reticle assemblies will have internals damaged from the recoil and not move exact "clicks". Or (on Cat Lens) the reticile if set too far to the side might break the lens from too much pressure when firing.

Nitrogen purge
by

You basically remove all(99.9% or so)the air from the internal spaces of the goggle. In the case of the 7 that is all the area from lens to lens. This removes any sources of moisture(air) and then you backfill to normal air pressure with dry nitrogen. This eliminates the possibility of air with moisture from getting back in, until someone opens the goggles again. I suggest sending the goggle to a professional for cleaning, since all optics are delicate and have coatings that can be scratched if cleaned improperly. There should be no way for dust or fibers to get into the rear optics unless someone has had the housing apart. On a relatively new goggle like the D, there should be no reason for this unless someone was playing with them or actually built them from parts.
Obviously the front optics just screw off and the tube comes out. That is the most likely spot for dust or debris. This does allow air into the entire unit, and they should be purged. Moist air not only causes fogging, it can damage internal electronics, and can eventually cause image tube problems in rare cases or at least contribute to premature failure.
Any "good" dealer can do this procedure for you. Depending on where you are located, US Night Vision can help. If you are over here on the east I can help you.

Gen 0 resolution.
by

Gen 2 and Gen 3 intensifiers cannot have better resolution than the number of of microchannels per millimeter. Higher resolution requires making more microchannels which is a more expensive and delicate process.

Gen 0 uses a relatively insensitive S1 photocathode with electron focusing optics and Gen 1 uses broad band multialkalai cathode with higher quantum efficiency, higher accelerating voltages, and electron focusing optics. There is no intrisic limit to the spatial resolution of these tubes other than how well the electron focusing optics do their job and the grain size of the phosphor. A single tube cannot be nearly as sensitive as a gen 2 or gen 3 intensifier, but they can definitely have as good or better resolution. Some do, most don't.

There are also gen 0 and gen 1 "proximity focued" tubes which just put the cathode near the phosphor with no focusing optics. These rarely have very good resolution. They can be made very compact but since they don't invert the image. I doubt any are still made because of overalll poor performance.

fogging
by

That's pretty easy to explain. Your goggles still dropped below the air temperature. I would bet it was a clear night with high humidity. Objects will come to equilibrium both from conduction and convection with the air and the the infrared radiation from the ground and sky. Clear sky always appears very cold in the longwave infrared range (5 to 15 microns give or take), and the ground can easily drop below air temperature too. Still air doesn't transfer much heat, and air does not radiate much long wave IR. So in still air objects get colder than the air, dumping their energy into space. The most common example is the fact that car tops and windows can dew and even frost over even when the air temperature is above freezing. If the NV equipment didn't have a significant heat source (your body heat) there would be nothing to prevent it from becomming colder than the air and fogging. The heat from the internal power supply is to miniscule to matter.

I still contend that moisture can't condense on a surface which is above the dew point, and the dew point is always less than (or equal if the humidity is 100%) the air temperature. And the cure is still keeping the equipment warmer than the surrounding air.

Hoffman Tester Capabilities
by

The more expensive ($60,000+) units are capable of giving good accuracy and reliabilty.

These are used for testing the SYSTEM gain and resolution. From that data you can interpolate the tube resolution.

ALL tubes are hand-crafted, and vary as much as people do. Think basket ball players... just because a prospective basketball player is 7 feet tall doesn't mean he is going to be the right person for the team.
(what is his "stats"? Is he blind? Does he have arms? Can he run? etc)
