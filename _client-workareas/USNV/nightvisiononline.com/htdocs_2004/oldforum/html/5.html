you are talking about the AN/SAR-7, this is not same as PAS-7.
the SAR-7 is a part of the Infrared Communication group used by the US Navy to communicat ship to ship with covert visual signals when radio silent is required.
The Infrared Communication Group consists of an Infrared Transmitting Set AN/SAT-2B and a portable Infrared Viewing Set AN/SAR-7 that provide a secure means for signaling at night under darkened-ship conditions.
The AN/SAT-2B transmits signals in the infrared region of the frequency spectrum which are normally invisible to the unaided eye. The AN/SAT-2B uses two transmitters, mounted port and starboard, which may be operated as a steady source for point-of train purposes, or may be flashed in morse code signaling for recognition. A transmitter control device permits selection of either the port, starboard or both transmitters and permits the mode of operation (steady or manual key). Also, a telegraph keyer allows an operator to manually key the transmitter for morse code operations. The AN/SAT-2B transmitters are operated in the same manner as the yardarm blinkers.
The AN/SAR-7 is a portable infrared viewing set, which receives and converts infrared radiation to visible light. The effective visual range between the AN/SAT-2B and AN/SAR-7 is 8000 yds in clear weather; this range will be affected by adverse weather conditions.

Aiming NV equipment at the sun.
by

The large optics found on most NV equipment will focus a lot of energy as it places an image of the sun onto a small spot the photosensitve surface of an genO image converter or any image intensifier. Even silicon CCD's are likely to be damaged by aiming them at the sun through fast optics. It's much worse than just putting the bare tube or CCD in sunlight. The heat can boil off the photocathode, crack the glass, or burn off the conductive traces on a CCD. Being on or off makes esentially no difference in the amount of damage done to the photocathode of an intensifer. With the power off the phosphor viewing screen won't be damaged but the tube will be ruined anyway.

Night driving is a lot like night flying at low altitude
by

Just a couple points of information. The military doesn't allow any single tube nvg for aided night flight. A single tube, whether it feeds one eye or both is still a single tube. Try shutting one eye while daylight driving for a simulation. So try to use a dual tube unit. The second area is loss of area of vision and loss of "secondary vision". Obviously the nvg has a narrower field of view than your normal eyes. What this does is eliminate your pheriphal vision which is what we all use unconsciously for everything from depth perception to gauging our speed of movement. While goggled up, the technique is to have your head on a swivel and constantly look left, straight, right, straight, etc. Your brain will see the view to the left and right, even if you don't pay attention to it with your eyes, and give you lots more information to walk/drive with. Overall you will have a much safer, more enjoyable experience, after you get your neck muscles built up.As to avoiding rocks, ruts, etc., you will have to learn to live in the world of shadows and gauge depth of the rut, height of the rock and so on. 70mph on a good dirt road is a trip but on trails, slower leads to a longer life. For practice, in daylight, put an eyepatch over one eye to simulate a single tube and go outside and do stuff. Also, tape a toilet paper tube to each lens of sunglasses and go walking in the woods. It's good practice too. Once you get acclimated to it, it's easy. One eyed people do it every day.

Recon-2
by

Hi Rick.

We have sold this model for the past 18 months or so.
The Recon is a copy of the 7-B, and so outwardly has a similar appearance, though it is definitely not as robust as the 7-B. Neither is it nitrogen filled - however, the rubber gaskets are well made, and we have not had any complaints about water ingress.

The image quality is very good, using a Katod gen 2+ tube. Manufacturer lists in the spec that the resolution is 30 Lp/mm, but I think they are being a little pessimistic - I would put it at being closer to 35 Lp/mm in the centre, though it may tail off a little around the outer edges of the tube. Intensification is very good, giving nice clear and bright images, and the tubes used are blem free, with no black spots or other artefacts present.

All in all, a good buy - the only slight drawback is that there is no optional higher magnification lens, the standard 1X lens is not changeable.
