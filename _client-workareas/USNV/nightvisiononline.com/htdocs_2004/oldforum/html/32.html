PVS2 needs fixing
by wavelength

I had a similar problem with mine. Under a cap on the top of the unit is a small black plastic hockey puck looking thing. This connects the high voltage generator to the image tube. Mine was open. Just lift the hockey puck off and check with an ohm meter from the contact in the center to the metal ring on the outside. It should be zero Ohms. If it's shows open you simply need a replacement. I bought one from Excalibure some years ago.

Hope this helps.

Spring air rifles and NV
by Sid

Spring powered airguns do have a quite vicious kind of forwards\backwards recoil which can often find the weak link in any optic mounted on them. The cheaper ones with rough actions also vibrate on firing, which can loosen threaded fasteners.

UK specification rifles aren't too bad because the limited power means limited recoil. 'Magnum' spec springers are something else entirely.

The D-141 has a proven track record in the UK for reliability when fitted to a medium powered spring rifle, it's probably the best bet for this application.

Magnification and light transmission
by

seem to have a direct bearing on one another. The higher the magnification of a lens, the lower it's light transmission usually is. Therefore most night vision systems have 6x or less magnification, any highrer requires serious illumination to be effective. Might also have something to do with a reduced field of view in higher magnification as well, i.e., smaller scene equals less area to gather light from.

Magnification does increase system resolution however. It's really a trade off in brightness vs. clarity, finding the optimal balance for your viewing conditions and budget.

Yes, it's critical
by

in night vision to have good light transmission from the lenses. Most lenses I've seen are rated at an f-stop, lower meaning more light transmission. Getting above f2.0 or so starts to be an impediment. I have an f1.3 (1x) lens and an f1.4 (3x) lens and I can see the light transmission difference in both my gen 2 and 3 scopes when I use them, the f1.3 being brighter. The lenses are the same brand lens. Doublers multiply the f-stop too.

I'm not saying you can't get the higher magnification (10x) you want in night vision, even gen 1, but I don't know if a built in illuminator will light that out to 100 yards. It might.

My experience with generation 1 night vision was less than satisfactory over all. I've looked through several at night, and owned a few as well. Distortion, poor image and quality control were often apparent. Of those I owned, one had poor workmanship on the body but excellent image, especially with the built in IR (invisible to dark adapted eye) on and within 30 yards. It had a cracked eyepiece though, so I sent it back. Replacement had a terrible image, IR on or not. Different type of built in illuminator (clear glass and dull red glow visible to the eye) than the first, otherwise same model.

My Retron had a good image in starlight around the city, but died after only seventy or so hours of use (estimated). It was expensive too.

I don't think most night vision is harmful. Some illuminators are, but those are usually IR lasers. Most Russian equipment should be safe, especially if it's in the states. Key word is most here, and these are my opinions.

adjust colluminator
by PAUL

Further down the page anonymous said he adjusted his colluminator and fixed a problem.
The factor way to set (adjust) a colluminator in the 7 goggle is this:
with goggle up facing you and locating tab on the left
place colluminaotr in housing, noting locating tab.
rotate colluminator to the right against tab
tighten screw on the RIGHT side first
then tighten screw on the left,closest to tab, last

The reason for this is due to the rotating action of the screw,in some cases yes even this small amount of turning the screw against the metal on the colluminator can cause ever so slight a shift in the colluminator and cause a blur. Sometimes you dont really notice the shift but for some reason you seem to get eye fatigue or a slight headache after using the goggle for a period of time. If you tighten the screw on the LEFT first you could be rotating the colluminator a very slight amount. In most cases none of this is of concern but this was the factory setting.

Ron....
by

You are correct. You first unscrew the black anodized cap at the middle of your objective lens. Inside, you will find a slotted cap, which actually houses the light bulb for the lit reticle. Unscrew that carefully (standard thread direction). Inside, you will see the back of the reticle housing. There is a small hole in the side of the housing where you can insert a small probe to help you gently slide out the reticle. The reticle should slide out easily. Do not force it. Your new reticle should go back in by reversing the above directions. Just make sure that you line up the reticle orientation key with the slot before you slide in your new reticle.

By the way, a guy by the name of Greg Speckt is looking for a cross-hair reticle if you're going to sell your old one. He posted a message a bit ago asking if anyone had one for sale.
