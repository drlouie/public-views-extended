laser rangefinders and night vision
by

A couple of months ago there was some discussion of using laser rangefinders with night vision equipment. I've been doing some experiments and thought some of you might find the results of interest.

I recently acquired a Leica LRF 1200 rangefinder. This unit is clearly superior to other "hand held" under $500 commercial units as it will reliably give solid reading right up to it's 1221 yard limit off of moderately reflective objects such as rocks and trees. Like other rangefinders it's performance is somewhat reduced in bright sunlight but still gives good daytime performance typically to 1000 yards. I also have a Bushnell 1000. The Leica is clearly more sensitive out to it's limit. The cutoff for the Bushnell is 1500 yards but it takes a very reflective target like a surveying corner cube to reach that range.

The Leica has an LED display which is compensated in brightness using the signal from the IR detector. It is easy to read in daylight or at night unlike the more common LCD displays found on Bushnell, Nikon and similar units. While the monocular is clear and has a fairly large field of view it only marginally usable under a full moon and in not usable in starlight. The Bushnell 1000 isn't usable even with a full moon overhead.

That's where night vision comes in. I experimented with putting the rangefinder in front of a NV monocular and behind it. Neither method is at all satisfactory. However the beam of the laser, while weak and only about 1/4 second in duration, is visible with the intensifer. I measured the wavelength of the laser at 808nm with an optical spectrum analyzer, so it's suitable for use with gen1, II, or III viewers. That wavelength is used both by Leica and Bushnell. I haven't tried other units.

Using either a NVPS-10 with a 75mm F/1.3 lens or an old Varo AN/PVS-4 I was able to (barely) see the laser dot to about 900 yards in starlight only conditions. These are both Gen II scopes. At 400 yards the beam is obvious and "eyecatching" in either instrument. I was able to get satisfactory ranging to around 600 yards by
just holding the rangefinder against the side of either instrument and pressing the ranging button repeatedly until I saw through the NV display that the beam had hit the intended target. The display stays on for 6 seconds which is plenty of time to move one's eye to the rangefinder and read the distance.

Unfortunately with brighter ambient light, such as a partial moon the laser is less visible as it is swamped by the moonlight. The usable range possible using the laser dot to aim with is greatly reduced. I don't think using a Gen III scope would help. I don't see a way to overcome that with the NVPS-10 which has no reticle. With the PVS-4 or other scopes equipped with a reticle it should be practical to make a bracket which will allow holding the rangefinder against the scope with accurate (and adjustable) alignement. If the laser is then pointed to match the reticle it should be no problem to get accurate readings of any object out to 1221 yards at night if the object can be seen in the scope.

Who needs to range to 1221 yards at night? Beats me, but it's very reliable at any shorter range down to 20 yards.

The beam of a rangefinder is narrow, similar to laser pointer but the optics appear to give give better off axis shielding. It's also low power (under 5mw) and very brief, about 1/4 second.

I really doubt there would be a chance of being noticed unless you aimed the rangefinder very close to the person with the NV scope and they were already looking at you.

The chance of your opponent seeing sun glint off the optics may be higher in the day than seeing the laser beam at night.

Focal length and f ratio
by

The f number is the ratio of the effective lens diameter to it's focal length. It is a dimensionless number.

For the same F ratio as you go from 25mm to 50 to 75mm attached to an 18mm image intensifer the following things happen:

1. Apparent image brightness stays very close the same if the f number is the same.

2. The apparent magnification goes from 1x to 2x to 3x.

3. If the weight of the 25mm lens is 1x expect the 50 mm lens to be nearly 8x and the 75mm lens to be about 20x. If the design is simply scaled up the lens elements increase in weight by the CUBE of the focal length. The metal housing parts by a little less because the larger ones aren't much thicker. They go up by more than the square though.

4. Apparent image quality may suffer with the larger lenses. Aberations will be 3x more apparent. Large fast lenses aren't easy to design.

5. Resolution of objects however may go up by 3 times despite the aberations since with a 1x lens the image tube may be the limiting factor and not the lens.

6. Cost will go up with about the square of the focal length. SOme costs such as the housing machining and other mechanical parts are about constant. The cost of lenses is mostly based on the surface aeea of all of the elements.

7. The actual field of view (angle from the scope to the edges of the distant objects in the field) will be reduced as 1, 1/2 , 1/3

8. The area of ground you can see through the scope will reduce as 1, 1/4, 1/9

9. The apparent field of view (the angle the eye sees to the phosphor screen) will remain unchanged.

Longer focus lenses sold with NV equipment usually have a higher F number because otherwise they're too heavy and expensive. That's why large NV scopes like the PVS-4, TVS-4&5, and PVS-8 use mirror/lens combinations instead of only lenses which eliminates a lot of glass used for refraction.

by

I got to try one last weekend and I liked it. High contrast,bright, surprisingly clear images held at arms length takes a little getting used to but I did. The area was a collection of man made lakes in southeast Missouri and it was dark enough to see the Milky Way as a stripe in the sky. A few hours in one location is not enough to evaluate any viewer but that is all I had and so far I like the thing.

C-mount lenses come in...
by RJ

... 1/3", 1/2", 2/3" and 1" exit glass setups. In my experience only the 1" exit glass provides a full image to the NV tube. The smaller sizes work, but you will notice a black portion around the edge of the NV image.

As far as the mounting thread size for c-mount, it is 1" or approx. 25mm.

I have tried about a dozen c-mount lenses... from CCTV lenses, to SLR camera lenses with a c-mount adapter and also movie camera lenses.

The Computar, Navitron 25mm F1.3 and 75mm F1.3 are very good and reasonably priced alternatives.

I have a few other lenses I really like...

4" or 100mm Elgeet F2.7 movie camera lens... this lens has excellent contrast and provides a very good image even under dark conditions.

4" or 100mm Century F1.9 movie camera lens... this is a notch better than the Elgeet, providing a brighter image while still offering good contrast.

1" or 25mm Angenieux F0.95 movie camera lens... this is an excellent 1x lens. The image has is a little cloudy wide open when there is alot of light, like a full moon. Dialing it back to F1.4 works best when it's bright. But in the dark woods, the extra F-stop makes a big difference. This lens also has a custom adjustable back focus, so you can set a perfect infinity focus.

50mm Canon F0.95... Absolutely the prettiest lens I have that works extremely well unsder all light conditions. The 7D goggles fitted with a c-mount adpater and this 50mm lens, produces the brightest image with the most contrast.

And lastly, my new toy for neighborhood watch... a Kowa/Cannon 17mm-102mm zoom F2 lens. This is also a great lens I found in the Cannon version.
