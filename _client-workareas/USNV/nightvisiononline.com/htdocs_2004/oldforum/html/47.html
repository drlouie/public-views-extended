IR spotlights.
by

Maximum viewing range depends on your viewer, the filter cutoff wavelength, and what you're trying to see. I have an LSI spotlight which gives illumination about equivalent to a 1/4 moon at 400 yards which is plenty to see a deer size object when used with a Gen II scope. The problem with this kind of light is that if there is dust, insects, or slight fog in first hundred feet the backscatter ruins the view unless the light is moved a few feet off to the side. With any light shining from near the viewer, images appear to have low contrast because of the lack of shadows compared to moonlight. It's a little worse than using a visible spotlight as color information is absent.

Because of the nature of absorbtion filters, IR pass filters have a sharp cutoff. They have very good blocking of shorter wavelengths and good transparency to IR even with cheap plastic ones. What separates cheap from expensive is the ability to handle power. They have to be able to dissipate the energy they absorb. A typical million CP handheld light is only 75 watts. The high candle power rating comes from the tight focus, not just the power. A similar size and power xenon arc lamp can give ten times the candlepower rating just because the light source is smaller and focuses better. 75 watts of broadband light spread over a 5" diameter can be handled with a plastic filter with just ambient air cooling but that's about the limit. After several seconds of operation the filter gets fairly warm.

The visibility depends on the cutoff wavelength which can be made to almost any value. The one I have gives a faint red glow looking directly at it but it's not noticable even at 10 feet. At that distance your face feels warm from it. It's a WWII surplus glass filter though. I don't know it's exact cutoff wavelength, but it's probably around 800 nm.

... some experimenting I've done ...
by RJ

I have tried a couple of things which have done a good job reducing an IR Spot bulb signature.

Sanding the surface of the quartz lamp... This helps eliminate the filament signature, which is by far the most offending aspect of this type of illumination. One down side, once the quartz bulb is sanded, it will no longer function as a "spot" lamp, rather only as a flood.

A good alternative to sanding the bulb is adding a piece of theater grade diffuser or scrim, on top of the IR Q-beam filter. This material is made to withstand the heat conditions of theater lighting, and can handle the spot with easy.

I've also added a 12 volt dimmer switch to my 2 million CP rechargable spot. The variable dimmer switch and diffuser go a long way in reducing the visual signature of the spot light at various ranges. Also, while a full power IR spot is needed with something like the Sony Night Shot camera, a good GEN 3 device requires very little illumination when fitted with a fast objective lens. However, when using something like a 60-300mm F3.8-5.4 zoom lens, the IR spot helps compensate for the slower lens.

As a side note for those "on device" LED IR illuminators that project a pattern of light... a piece of wax paper filter does a great job of creating a nice even IR wash. This treatment really helps your near field NV image in a dark environment, such as the woods.

The D-241M Cobra
by

is sold by US Night Vision, and uses a new/surplus gen 2 MX9916 image intensifier. New/surplus means they are new, unused tubes, but have been on the shelf and sold off by someone, either the suppliers, or the government, I am not sure, maybe both. The housings do come from Eastern Europe, I don't know of others using the same or similar housing, you would have to ask USNV about that. The reticle is projected in front of the tube, so that makes for a nice accurate design without the parallax problems with the rear reticle designs. They are well built, I personally don't love the mount, but it works ok and works really well on a picatinny style rail.
Yes you could get a PVS-4 for not much more money, and they are a nice sight, but are large and heavy, and only come with AR carry handle mount, and other mounts are expensive. They do not really work much better in gen 2, though they have superb optics which make up for the 25mm gen 2 image tube's mediocre performance. I guess it all comes down to again, the questions you need to ask yourself. What do I plan to do with it? How much can I spend? Am I willing to make a risky buy? What kind of ranges and targets will I be using it for? Exactly what level of performance do I expect or need? Those are some of the things you need to think about before buying, then continue asking questions and checking answers.

Thermal visibility
by

To not be seen by a thermal imager you have to accomplish two things. First, you have to have what the thermal imager sees near the temperature of the surroundings. In this respect it's not unlike any other camoflage problem. A metal umbrella would reflect the surroundings and not be hot but it would still look like an umbrella with the reflected image of the sky (cold) and the ground (warmer). What ever is in the direct line of sight needs to look "natural" whether that be like vegetation as a ghille suit or perhaps a large rock. In a city it might be a retangular box which looks like a power transformer or a parked car.

The second problem is to get rid of body heat in a manner that's invisible. Solid objects radiate IR as a function of only two things. Their temperature, and their emissivity. A perfect mirror mirror has an emissivity of zero and radiates nothing, but it reflects IR perfectly. A perfect absorber has an emissivity of 1 and behaves as a perfect radiator, reflecting nothing. Real objects lie in between, with metal surface at about .1 and "soft" objects like clothing and skin around .8 There are also transparent materials which behave similar to a perfect reflector plus and materials with various states in between, but do not change the direction of the IR radiation. The latter is the best way to remove body heat and fortunately air comes pretty close to being transparent at the wavelengths used by thermal imagers.
If it was't transparent a thermal imager couldn't see through it , yet moving air can do a fine job of carrying away heat energy.

If you can separate yourself from the imager by something which is not "noticable", and remove your heat with air flow which does not heat the insulator you will remain unnoticed. A simple ghilli suit won't quite do the job as your body will heat the surface and it will "glow" at 7 to 10 micron wavelenghts. A loose fitting one with plenty of airflow to remove body heat invisibly could do fairly well. I don't know specifically how to make one which would be comfortable to wear and provide mobility but I think it could be done.

Getting into a cave (or similar) will prevent being seen directly, but a thermal imager is great for finding cave entrances hidden by vegetation, particularly if it's cold outside and slightly warmer air comes out of the cave and warms the bushs and rocks near the entrance.

The other form of camoflage is to blend in with a lot of other heat sources. A thermal imager is not good for picking out specific individuals in a crowd because it does not show normal facial features and distorts the body's outline.

This is an area I know something about. I used to own a laser display company (Hard to make enough money to pay for the equipment.) and I had a variety of VERY powerful lasers from 200mw Solid state to 5w Argon to 20watt Yag.

Lasers of any power level, no matter where in the spectrum they fall, are NOT illegal to possess or use, though use is restricted. (If using any self-manufactured display system, it has to be approved, need to file a variance if using a non-"eyesafe" for public display. etc..)

... interesting setup, but not cheap!
by RJ

The monoloc is designed to screw mount into the 40.5mm threads of your favorite monocular's front objective.

I have put together my own "poor man's" monoloc. I picked up a 1 1/4"- 1 1/4" firm rubber plumbing coupler. It fits over the eye piece of my Night Force scope, and over my monocular 25mm objective. I trimmed the coupler so that the objective lens sits firmly against the back of the eye piece, and this setup provides decent results.

One down side when using a 1x lens in this kind of setup... the scope view does not completely fill the NV field of view. A 50mm objective lens does better, but still comes up a little short. A 75mm does the best, taking the scope view edges just beyond the NV field of view. In this regard, the 75mm F1.3 lens works very well, and the nightforce scope on 2.5x magnification provides alot of light.

Another point to consider, your normal flattop does not have enough rail space to move the scope/NV monocular combo far enough forward, to provide for a normal shooting setup. An extened rail is needed. Then the monocular could be secured to the rail instead of hanging off the back of the scope, which potentially invites some parralax error.

pvs7 plus ACOG for night shooting.
by

I've been following your attempts to use the combination of a pvs7 and an ACOG on an AR. Just from an optics standpoint I don't see how this can possibly be successful.

If you wear the pvs7 and try to look through the ACOG, the first problem is you'd need a neck like a swan to get into position. But besides that the eyepiece of the ACOG (or any other) scope is designed to feed light to a human eye with only about 1/4" entrance pupil. It will not couple well to any normal NV scope. So it's not practical to put an NV scope like the pvs7 between the eyepiece and the eye. If you can see the reticle at least it will still be on target but it's going to be a rather dim image of the target.

If you try to put the pvs7 in front of a riflescope you run into other problems. Again the eyepiece of the NV scope is designed to couple to a human eye, not to the 30-40mm lens of a riflescope. Yes some light will get through but most will be lost and again the image of the target will be dim. But there is a much worse problem. The manufacture of NV tubes and the associated optics doesn't guarantee that the magnification will be >exactly< 1x, nor that there will be zero distortion off axis. In fact there will most likley be a significant angular displacement of the line of sight on axis and it will most likey vary off axis. Even if you are able to see an image through the riflescope at night, the apparent position of the reticle on the object your looking at will no longer be in the same place. That's especially true if you're holding the NV scope. Even if it's firmly attached to the riflescope or the scope rail there will still most likely be a displacement which would require re-zeroing the riflescope. If you simply hold it the accuracy will be horrible even if you can see the target.

So what's the solution to night shooting with an AR equpped with an ACOG while wearing a pvs7? The answer is a laser pointer mounted beside the ACOG and adjusted to the same zero point. You give up the ACOG's magnification to do that, but unless you're shooting prone it probably won't hurt accuracy.

You've already got the another solution in the Raptor though so what's the problem? If you're worried about loosing zero when changing scopes just get another AR-15 and consider it an "accessory" for the Raptor ;-)

I don't have experience specifically with either the ACOG or the pvs7 but the optical principles apply to any similar setup. Yes it is possible to make an intensifier which will mount in front of a riflescope but it has a different layout than a pvs7 and will have adjustments for the angular offset. It will also attach firmly to the scope.. There are also "day/night" riflescopes in which part of the erector and eyepiece assembly can be replaced with an intensifer (Seiler Instrumnt for example).

Laser illuminators
by

I've used a single 880nm 100mw >LED< illuminator. Although it's under 1mm square it still requires too large of collimating lens to be very practical. It does a fair job of simulating moonlight at 400 yards and about fills the field of a 10x NV scope when used with a 90mm f1.0 lens as a collimator. An equal power laser could produce the same results, but it can easily be focused tighter with much smaller optics to produce either a brighter area or give better range.

The advantages of solid state illuminators over incandescents include smaller size, smaller batteries, and most important the better collimated beam which doesn't fog the view as much from near field backscatter. All IR (or monocromatic visible) illuminators placed near the viewer suffer from producing rather low contrast images. With moonlight or sky glow you think you see more because the shadows give high contrast. With the illuminator there are essentially no shadows. The image looks washed out, but the good side is you can see into places that would otherwise be in the shadows and invisible.

zeroing laser.
by

Assuming you intend to shoot the same rifle both day and night you no doubt have metallic sights or a scope on the rifle too. Zero the daytime sights of rifle at your chosen know distance in the day time at a shooting range. Give thought to the optimum zero distance for rifle/ammo/target combination. The same method works fine with red lasers too which are rarely visible at over 50 yards in the daytime.

by

If you look through a scope with an adjustable f stop and switch from f/1.3 to f/1.6 you will notice a difference on an overcast night. In full starlight or brighter you probably wouldn't care.

The difference in brightness is the ratio of the F/ratios squared. so (1.6/1.3)^2 says with the larger opening the image on the tube's cathode will be 1.5 times as bright. At low brightness the phosphor will also be 1.5 times brighter. Since the human brain does not perceive brightness linearly though it won't seem 1.5 times brighter.

radioactive optics
by

Glass made with heavy elements (thorium for example) is used to obtain color dispersion characteristics (index of refraction vs wavelength) not available in "normal" glass. Since intensifier tubes have a much wider range of wavelength than the human eye, making well corrected optics is more difficult than for the visible range only. The use of a radioactive glass could reduce the number of elements and thus reduce manufacturing costs. Fewer elements usually gives better transmission because of a reduced number of surface reflections. The fact that some of those glasses are slightly radioactive is of little concern for military use.

980 nm illuminator
by

980 nm is not a good wavelength for use with NV equipement except the very old gen O with it's S-1 cathode where it's close to ideal. Gen 1 and Gen II (S20 mulitalkali) have some response at that wavelength but it's pretty weak. A GaAs Gen III tube has almost no response beyond 950nm. What little you will see with a gen III tube and that LED will be from the fact that LED's are broad band with 980 just being the center wavelenght. If it were a laser you'd see almost nothing. A 980 nm illuminator will work well with extended IR CCD cameras such as the Sony HAD "ex-view". For a Gen III tube 800 to 900 is probably best for low visibility and good illumination. For gen II something around 785-800 is probably a better tradeoff. Even an 880 nm led is slightly visible to the human eye (but dangerous if you can see it).

silicone grease
by paul

Lanny your right about the silicone oil/grease. Not only will it migrate but it may also swell any o-rings that it comes in contact with. We used a grease called Super O-Lube, and only in very small amounts, use a tip of a tooth pick to apply. Many of these "gimbal" mounts were teflon coated at the factory so very little grease is required. Remember that after a long time in one position even a "good" spring will take a little set so if your sight is off weapon (not zero'd) every now and then exercise the springs, just as you would the shutter on a good camera to keep it from getting sluggish.
