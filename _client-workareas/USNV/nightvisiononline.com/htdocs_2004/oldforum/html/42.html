testing a gen II tube
by

You can tell if a tube is totally dead or not just by applying power in a dark room, but you won't get a good test of it's performance without both a proper objective and eyepiece.

You said you're using a 35mm lens but that just implies it's off a 35mm camera. That's ok, but what's it's focal length and F ratio? A lens slower than about F/2 will give quite unimpressive performance. Most NV viewers use F/1 to F1.3 lenses. It has to be well aligned to give a sharp and centered image on the tube's cathode. It's nearly impossible just to hold a fast lens well enough to get a decent test. A well cut piece of PVC pipe and duct tape is a marginal solution.

If you don't use a proper eyepiece the light coupling from the phosphor to your eye won't be efficient either.

Any light leaks, particularly between the objective and the cathode will hurt the contrast. That will make the image look washed out.

What you've said so far doesn't sound like a bad tube to me. The output should never be "bright" in normal operation. It should just be bright enough so that your color vision is functioning. Excessive gain just makes the signal to noise ratio worse. My PVS-4 gives a noticably poorer image at full gain. With an overcast sky and no moon full gain may be required to get enough light for they eye, but the image is awfully noisy at that point.

lens for 25mm tube.
by

The nicest "lens" you could get for your tube would be a complete AN-PVS-4 body. Contact the guy on this group named Mike from American Surplus. Shoudld be a little under $1k.

I build astronomical instruments as a profession and while an intensifier can be useful as a guider with a CCD on a long focus instrument, visually it will not be very useful. Your choices really boil down to using fast 35mm camera lenses (a 50MM f 1.2 Nikon lens for example). I I bought two used/surplus a few years ago for $80ea. New fast lenses are expensive. Almost all lenses pass IR out to 1000nm, beyond where your tube works, but some don't give very good focus over that wide of range (400-900). Photographic film typically only uses about half that range. That's a likely problem with any fast lens not specifically designed for night vision use. All mirror telescopes don't have a problem with focus vs wavelength, but they're hard to find with f/ratios faster than about 6. One type of big cheap lens which actually gives fair images with NV tubes are the projection lenses for color TV's. Not as good as lenses specifically designed for IR, but a web search will show some F/1.0 85mm FL lens for around $40. If you want bright images cheap that's the way to go. I bought some made by Sony which give quite acceptable performance as long range viewers.

NV + scope
by

Look at it this way. The objective lens of the scope takes in only so much light based on the area of the objective lens. That light gets spread out as the square of the magnification. Unless the objective lens of the riflescope is larger in diameter by the magnification of the rifle scope times the diameter of the objective on the night vision scope you'll get a fainter image looking trough the scope than directly with the NV gear. How much dimmer image you can tollerate is something you have to decide on. But for example if you have a 50mm scope and a 25mm F/1.0 lens on your goggles anything thing over 2X magnification will restrict the light.

The tube diameter of a riflescope can make a difference at very low magnifications but doesn't normally. A 30mm riflescope tube offers strength and greater reticle adjustment range but not more light transmission contrary to the hype of some advertisers.

Unfortunately you probably won't find a 2x rifle scope with a 50mm objective (or a 4 x 100mm one).
Commercial rifle scopes are designed for humans with 1/4" pupils, not for the 1"+ entrance pupils. of NV scopes. 3-9 x 56mm (Weaver 5star tactical) is about the largest exit pupil I've seen on a commercial rifle scope. Thats still only 18mm at the 3x setting so even that will restrict the light if your NV scope has a 25mm objective.

You can use some of the large "dot scopes" but they don't give any magnification and offer little advantage over a laser aiming device. Many of them can't be adjust dim enough to work well either.

drilling millions of microchannel holes
by

I don't know how the production is presently done. Some years ago though I read a discription of the process which went something like this:

A bar is made from a cylinder of glass with a second type of glass inserted as a core. These are fused together in a vacuum so there is no gas trapped between them. Then the bar is heated and drawn into very long fiber (with a lot of technology to keep the fibre round and uniform diameter). To this point the process is simiar to making the fibers used for optical communications cables. The fibers are then wound on a large diameter spool so the fibers are uniformly spaced in a hexagonally packed form. The loop is then cut and straigtened. This bundle would be about 18 or 25mm in diameter for NV tubes. The bundle is again heated and re-fused in a vacuum to remove the gaps between the fibers. At this point it's a solid glass rod with a honeycomb pattern of the two types of glass. It is similar to the image conduit fiber optics rod used to make the fiber faceplates and image inverters for image tubes though they use different types of glass.

To make the microchannel plates thin sections are sliced off of the bar and the surfaces are lapped flat and parallel. Then the disks are etched to chemically remove the glass which started out as the core of the original bar. That leaves what is essentially a very fine honeycomb of hollow cylinders of the glass which was the original single cylinder.

A resistive coating is vacuum deposited onto the inside walls of these microchannels to form the electron muliplier. The ends are lightly metalized to form conductors to carry power to the microchannels. This only requires just femptoamps per channel. The photcathode is mounted very close to one end of the microchannel plate, the phosphor screen very close to the other. These too are lightly metalized. Even though they're conductive they are still mostly transparent. There are four connnections to the high voltage power supply. The cathode is the most negative, then each end of the microchannel plate, and the anode which is coated with the phosphor is the most positive. The biggest part of the volatage is across the microchannel plate. The whole unit is evaculated and sealed.

Lighting phosphors in a vacuum with electricity was known in the late 1800's. Electron multiplication was known in 1940's. Proximity focus imagers in the 1950's. Microchannel plates in the late 70's. GaAs cathodes in the 80's. Since then it's mostly been refinement of the process to give better sensitivity, life and resolution.

Making a higher resolution microchannel is a matter of more but thinner fiber keeping the tolerances and spacings tighter. That's a very simpified version of the process as I understand it. I could be wrong about any of it since I've never worked in the industy which makes them. I'd enjoy being corrected if someone will explain how it's presently done.

tube life
by

The life specs don't mean much when applied to an individual tube. There's no filament boiling off at a predictable rate. A gently treated tube will fail eventially most likely due to gas leakage. If a tube type is rated 10,000 hours MTBF (about a year) you can expect af few percent to fail by 1000 hours but many will still be good after 20 years or more. If you get five year old tube which is still good the chances are about as good for the next five years as if you'd bought a new one.

Abuse takes out a lot of tubes too. Tubes can be get cathode damage from sunlight, phosphor darkening from extended bright illumination, over or reverse voltage power supply damage, corrosion, extreme temperatures stressing the glass/metal seals, and mechanical failure from vibration or impact.

There are a lot of Gen 1 tubes in 1970's military scopes which still work ok. Some which don't too.

battery options for AN-PVS-4
by

You can use a Panasonic CR-2477 "coin" type lithium cell in an AN-PVS-4 (or TVS-5) battery holder with only minor modifications. Pull the spring out of the holder and "stretch it". The CR-2477 is only 7.7 mm thick compared to about 18mm for the original battery. Get some standard 3/4" wide black plastic electrical tape and stick it inside the holder (sticky side out) to reduce the inside diameter of the holder to the size of the battery and to insulate it. It takes about 3 turns of tape. Put the spring back in. You can then drop the battery into the holder (+ side toward the body of the PVS-4). This Panasonic cell has a 1 amp hour rating. Although it's much smaller it has longer life than the mil-spec battery which has a rating of 800 mahr. If you can't find the CR2477 locally you can get them from http://www.digikey.com for about $2.20 each if you buy ten.

Just use one battery at a time (some PVS-4's have two battery holders). One will give about 24 hours of use. It works fine and is lighter and more compact than two AA's. Two AA alkalines give slightly longer life than one CR-2477.

If you don't want to bend the original spring you can get a stainless steel replacement (which is already the right length) from McMaster Carr, part number 1692K28. http://www.mcmaster.com. They come 3 to a pack for $12.59. They're nomially .98" diameter and .75" tall so the outer ring needs to be expanded slightly to fit the holder. You can easly do that with your fingers.

I have a AA adapter but prefer this mod. The advantage of the AA's is you can get them in any local store, but most battery shops have the CR2477.

stacked batteries
by

If you stack two lithium cells (or other batteries for that matter) if they are both the same direction you get twice the voltage. That is called series wiring. If you put them in opposite directions you get zero voltage. To put them in parallel they have to be arranged - to - and + to + which gives the same voltage as a single cell. That can be done in a stacked configuraton with an insulator and jumper strips. (as in the third figure)


______________ ......... -
/ \
|_______________| 3v 6 volts
/ \ 3v
|_______________|........ +

______________....____________ ..... -
/ \ / \ 3 volts
|_______________|.|_____________|.... +


______________.............-
/ \ .
..|_______________| . 3v
. ///////////////... 3v
. / \ 3v
..|_______________|.......... +

The upper diagram is what you get if you stack two cells in one PVS-4 cell holder. The middle diagram is what you get if you put one cell in each holder of a PVS-4. The dots indicate wires or straps. The ////// indicates an insulator.

The unit works best (for total battery utilization) with just one cell in either holder but not both. The CR2450 cell is fine, it just gives less life than a CR2477. A single CR2450 should run a PVS-4 for about 15 hours if you use the reticle sparingly. I don't know for a fact that 6 volts will damage the power supply but I certainly wouldn't try it. It could also damage the reticle bulb or the reticle brightness adjust pot. I would strongly recommend you don't use two lithium cells in series. The AA battery holder does place the two 1.5 volt alkaline batteries in series to give 3 volts which is fine. There's danger there too as at least one company makes a AA size lithum batter which is 3 volts and two would be too much voltage. ITT's specs for image tube power supplies show 3 +/- 0.5 volts.

eye resolution
by

The normal human eye (20/20) has a resolution in the fovea and with daytime light levels of about one arc minute. To compare that to a night vision device, divide the apparent field of view of the instrument (as seen from the eyepiece) by the tube diameter in mm and by the tube resolution in lines per mm.

Using an AN/PVS-7 (1x) as an example, it has 40 degree field of view. thats 40*60 or 2400 arcminutes.

The tube is 18mm diameter and I'll assume it has a 45 line/mm resolution.
So 2400/(18*45) = about 3 arcminutes. That may not sound great compared to your daylight naked eye resolution but it's a whole lot better than the resolution of a dark adapted human eye. If the same image tube and eyepiece is equipped with a 3x lens then you should be able to resolve the same size objects that you could naked eye with decent lighting. Stated another way, wearing a 1X AN/PVS-7 will give you about 20/60 vision. (if you're not worse than that already).

resolution test
by

There's more to being able to read numbers than just resolution. The granularity of the image increases as the light level goes down because of the photon arrival rate (times quantum efficincy) of the cathode. That can be very distracting to the human eye/brain even though the resolution (lines/mm) remains unchanged.
As the light on the human eye decreases the eye's color detecting cones shut down and the rods take over. The rods are much more sparsely distributed than cones in the eye's fovea. This effect is reduced with night vision equipment which keeps the cones in use at lower external light levers, but it's not eliminated. I don't see anything contradictory in your results. Better (higher QE and closer spaced microchannel) tubes give better resolution, but lower light reduces the resolution of the intensifier/eye combination with any tube.
