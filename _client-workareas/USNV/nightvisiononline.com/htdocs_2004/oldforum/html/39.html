D's
by

As far as I know, all of the "D" units are mil spec (through and through, not just the tubes). While there may be some difference within the individual units, they all meet the mil spec minimums. If you buy a 14D from Excalibur, you are getting an ITT manufactured 14D. If you buy a 14D from USNV, you are getting a Litton manufactured 14D.

It seems to me that the mil spec units undergo final assembly by the tube manufacturers (ITT, Litton) and are then just resold by the commercial folks like USNV etc. The commercial folks seem to stick to manufacturing commercial units as it is likely extremely expensive to get your stuff certified mil spec. That's not to say the commercial stuff is not as robust etc, it has just not gone through the mil certification process.

In theory there is nothing to prevent commercial folks from buying a mil spec tube and building it in to a commercial spec unit, but I have not heard of this - folks willing to pay for the mil spec tube, likely want the whole unit mil spec.

Ask Excalibur if thier 14D is a mil spec unit in tube quality only or through and through.

Hope this helps

Comparison
by franco


Russians tend to classify as Gen 1 a very weird technology, similar to Western Gen 0, that relies on
an image tube with a top-quality S-25 photoK (instead of S-1) with an electrostatic lens that concentrates the electron flux from the photoK on a phosphor screen which is much smaller than the photoK.
The gain (300-1500x) is accomplished in a single-stage image tube with no fiberoptic plates.
Apparently, there's no western equivolent to Russian Gen I.
While they classify cascading Gen I (with fiber optics and far more overall gain) as Gen II (i. e. the PN58); it also appears that some scopes using an MCP based tube are rated as having a Gen III (Russian classification) tube!
For this purpose, I will assume you are talking of MCP based Russian Gen II vs. US Gen II tech.
Well, I do have both a 1PN51 and a US- 9916 tube based weapon scope.
Both units are pure MCP based Gen II, with S-25 PhotoK, the Russian unit being a 25mm tube; claimed resolution for the PN51 is 26 lp/mm (I believe it is correct), while Lanny told me most 9916 tubes are just 28 lp/mm.
I believe I dont have a really good tube for my US unit, actually it was a real cheap scope, while maybe I have an outstanding tube in my PN51: the Russian unit has medium to good gain (lots of scintillation, though) but has no halo or blooming and the resolution is excellent, with very little blemishes.
On the other hand, the gain of the US unit is excellent, especially in low light, but it has halo in urban areas and some blooming, plus the resolution and sharpness are mediocre.
This situation is a bit like comparing apples and oranges, but it gives you an idea, since most budget NV nowdays use the 9916.
I forgot the prices: the PN51 cost me 1200$ 3 years ago, while my 9916 based scope was less than 900$.
Comparing the PN-51 vs. a friend's PVS-4: what a difference! Absolutely no match for the PVS-4, three times better in everything.
Made me want to throw the PN51 out the car window.

hot ants
by

A thermal imager works simply by detecting the infrared emission from a surface. Air is fairly transparent in the 6-10 micron range, so the energy which reaches a detector is the combined result of the energy emitted from the surface plus any which may be reflected off the surface from other sources, like the sun.

I don't have any experience with ants, carpenter or other, but it comes down to whether they produce a significant change in the temperature of the surface of the wall. For most materials it still won't let you actually see through the wall.

You can buy a hand held single channel IR thermometer
which has a resolution of about 0.1 degree F for about
$100. Thats not an imager, but it might be sufficient for your needs. The problem it that I suspect ant's do put out a lot of heat and there are other things like air leaks which will affect the temperature of the wall. For $50k you can get a cooled imager which can resolve better than 1/100th degree F and produce images similar to a TV. You can find used industrial imagers for a couple of thousand with a hundred+ lines of resolution and no so heavy one person can't carry the rig and set it up.

You really need to do your homework on how much the critters change the surface temperature of the wall.
In a controlled environment you could problably detect them with IR. On a outside wall I'm skeptical.

Just thinking about it I'd bet you'd have better luck with an acoustic sensor than with IR. Listen for the litte buggers crunching wood with an electronic stethiscope.

Sniperscopes
468. Any person who knowingly buys, sells, receives, disposes of, conceals, or has in his possession a sniperscope shall be guilty of a misdemeanor, punishable by a fine not to exceed one thousand dollars ($1,000) or by imprisonment in the county jail for not more than one year, or by both such fine and imprisonment. It depends...and there are alternatives
by

As with all government regulation, answers can be complicated and no one may ever have the whole picture.

The one thing I know for sure is that US manufacturers are not permitted to produce class IIIb IR laser aiming devices, except when granted a variance by the FDA that requires the manufacturer to restrict sales to military and LE.

I have heard (but do not know for sure) that dealers selling to the public have now caused all such products to be drop shipped from the manufacturer to the end user. Sigh.

While the FDA has no authority outside the US, there could also be import or ownership restrictions I am not aware of (but I have looked).

I don't think this is likely because US manufacturers can legally build, and I can legally buy or import OTHER class IIIB IR laser devices (such as remote temperature sensors, some laser rangefinders, or communications devices). You can buy the actual laser diodes themselces legally too, just not assembled into a laser aiming device. It must make sense to someone :-)

On the bright side, a class III laser device is extremely bright even to the naked eye. Personally I find them (the visable ones at least)way too bright for Gen III night vision equipment and worry about burning my tube. On the dim side, a class I IR laser aiming device is completely eye safe and thus not restricted - and may be bright enough for NV use.

I've found an offshore manufacturer of a class I laser aiming device (similar to the Beamshot units) and am in an (unfortunately long) process of obtaining some to test. If they turn out to work well and some pending approvals occur, I can share the source. If not, maybe demand can convince some US manufacturer to start producing a similar product.

Until then:
a) For pistol use, various holo sights (docter optic, bushnel etc) are as effective and usable as IR lasers when wearing a goggle or monocular. They have an added advantage of being far less detectable by other NV devices that may be looking in your direction.
b) For long guns, unfortunately goggles or moniculars (head mounted at least) do not work as well as lasers. Allthough a high mounted holosight placed far forward (gas port rail) on an AR works pretty well. But you can mount a monicular and a holo sight in-line which works well. Obviously an actual weapon sight works well. You can also use a conventional (visable) laser (with or without some neutral density filters in front to reduce brightness) - after all you would only activate the laser when ready to shoot.
