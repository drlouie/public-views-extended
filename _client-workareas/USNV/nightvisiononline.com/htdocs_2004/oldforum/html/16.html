by

potting is the layman's term for encapsulating the tube and power supply in the plastic(I think they are delron or something like that) housing or sleeve. First you have to get it out, clean it all, check it for proper operation which involves insulating the outer portion of the tube and connections to prevent arcing, then fit it back, and re-fill with the proper silicone compound. It should be put into a vacuum chamber to cure also to prevent bubbles, but some guys don't bother. There are other bits and pieces but that is the basics. It is just removing cleaning and re-encapsulating. Sometimes the tube simply begins to arc between the sections on the outside between the silicone and the tube surface, and that is all it takes to cure it.

Patriot opinion...
by RJ

Duane...

I have a Patriot with the Ultimatte package. I bought it with the an ATN Gen 3 tube, which I think was a 36lp tube. I was not happy with the tube and I negotiated a deal where ATN bought back the tube. I then found an OMNI IV tube, and it made a huge difference in the performance of the Patriot system. IMHO, any system you would buy is only as good as the tube installed in the system.

Tha Patriot hardware is a mixed bag. I agree with Lanny that the Patriot optics are not the best, but then again they are not the worst. Both the 25mm and 90mm lenses provide good brightness, but they do not have as much contrast as other lenses I have tried. The result is an image with a little less detail at brighter light levels, but they hold up a better as you approach total darkness.

The 25mm eye piece and 35mm illuminated reticle eye piece are both good. The 2x 15mm eye piece is not very good, and I would never use it.

The head mount gear and adapter functions as well as other monocular rigs I have tried, even though the adapter appears a little clunky.

The optional IR illuminator also functions well, better than an ITT 190 iluminator, IMHO.

The Weapons mount kit is also a decent option, except in it's standard form it requires you to use the 90mm ATN lens. This set up works good, but I have adpated my rig to use better lenses. I made a custom adapter for my monocular that uses the rear eye piece ring and attackes to the two mounting holes on the monocular body. I used an ARMS #5 MultiBase to make the adapter. This set up does not attach to the front lens, so any lens can be used with the weapon mount set up now. My preference is the 75mm F1.3 lens, which offers excellent contrast and brightness. There are other mounting alternatives I have thought about, which I could share with you if you end up with a Patriot rig.

The Patriot monocular body is a very solid design which provides for easy access to the tube from the front of the monocular. You remove a hex key set screw and the c-mount unscrews from the front end. The tube then drops right out. Very simple.

Bottom line... I would recommend the Patriot if you could control the quality of the tube. The package in it's standard form is OK, but with the addition of a couple of lenses and some tweaks, the system performance is greatly enhanced.

Let me know if you have a specific questions on the Patriot. I wish I knew more about the gen 2 performance, but then again I probably would not be happy with it, since I have lived with a very good Gen 3 for awhile now.

Camcorder hook up...
by RJ

... there are (3) methods on most current camcorders for hooking to a computer...

1) the RCA composite video connection

2) the s-video component video connection

3) the firewire DV digital video connection

Both 1) & 2) require the computer have an analog video input capability, either built in or via a PCI video digitizing card. The firewire or IEE1934 DV digitial video connection is a newer connecton built into every new Apple Mac as well as many PCs.

I have an Apple G4 that has both a Firewire connection as well as a Truevision RTX2000 video digitizing card the can record via RCA composite, s-video component and RGB component.

My video card quality...
by RJ

... well, my business is video production. My Truevision RTX2000 is the same hardware that went into top end AVID editing systems, until the more recent upgrades. It records 720x486 or 640x480 video at full frame rate of 29.97. You can select the data rate so as not to over tax your hard drives. We use Medea video raids, so the card can run full tilt, which comes in at around 12-15MB/sec or 600-750KB/frame. However, I have a hard time noticing any image quality advantages above 300-350KB/frame, so that's were we set our data rates to conserve hard disk space.

My original cost on the card was around $5000, but there are newer products from Pinnacle and others, that offer similar performance for well below $2000.

Most of my work is in the broadcast BetaCam SP format. We are looking to upgrade sometime next year to a DVC Pro HD 720i/1080i 24p system. I am working on a Raytheon project now, that was shot with a Panasonic DVC Pro HD camera at 720i and it is absolutely beautiful. We had the master tapes transfered to betacam SP, and the file edit will them be "conformed" in a HD - High Definition production house.

The best way to go on a small budget is to get a camcorder with a DV-Firewire output/input and then something like an iMac. Having been in the business since the mid-80's, it's amazing the advancements in digital audio & video production tools, especially the cost reductions.
