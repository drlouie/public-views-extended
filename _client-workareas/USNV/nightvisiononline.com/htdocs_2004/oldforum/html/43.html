IR wavelength
by

Both Gen 1 and Gen II use a multialkalai semi-transparent photocathode with a response which is called S20 which peaks around 500 nm and trails off at a fairly even slope out to around 950 nm. Earlier Gen0 units usually used a silver oxide photocathode. This had fair response in the visible, dropped in red then rose to a peak around 900 nm then trailed off to about 1200 nm. It's main problem is that it's also noisy unless very cold and not as good of sensitivity as the S20. Gen III use A film of Gallium Arsinide which is more of a semiconductor than earlier coatings.It has response similar to s20 in the visible but is a few times more sensitive from around 600 to 950 nm where it drops sharply. There are extended IR intensifiers which add indium to the mix which go out to about 1100 nm. They aren't common no the civilian market.

LED illuminators have a broad spectrum The specs are always center wavelength, but say an 880 nm LED will have around 1% of its energy shorter than 800 and longer than 950nm. That's not true of lasers, so it makes a differnce as to whether it's visible. The human eye has some response to nearly 800 nm, but its down about 1/10000 of what it is at 550nm. If you want both low visibility and good response I'd pick an 820nm laser or 880nm led as the best tradeoff for any of the tubes. If you don't care about visibility then a red led or laser will work better with gen I and II. Around 850 nm is ideal for Gen III.

IF you're just looking for a pointer and not an area illuminator 5mw is more more than needed to 500 yards with Gen II or III at any wavelength from 550 (green) out to 900 nm. Illuminators need a lot more than 5mw.

Lenses and Mirrors
by Sid

Generally speaking it's an optical system that combines lenses and mirrors.

In night vision terms the ones I have seen are of a type known as Maksutov.
Basically the light enters through a primary lens and then hits a secondary mirror which reflects it back again towards the primary lens. On the rear surface of the primary lens, in the centre, is yet another mirror which reflects the light back into the scope once again ( still with me here ? :-) ) but this time it passes through a hole in the centre of the secondary mirror and travels onto the tube. Phew.

It's a way of getting a long focal length into a short physical space and has excellent optical characteristics. The two NVD's I have used with Maksutov lenses had simply awesome performance.
There's always a downside and that is the lens\mirror system is heavy and expensive to make.

You can spot a Maksutov easily because there will be a black circle in the centre of the primary lens where the third mirror is formed.

Just poking a little fun, no offense intended. Actually astronomy and astrology weren't really distinguishable befor the time of Kepler. He started his career as an astrologer, but he could also be considered the person who split the two apart as he cast off his astrological training when he proved mathematically that some of the astrological dogma simply didn't match reality. Primarily he proved that planets moved in elliptical orbits while astrology up to that time decreed that planetary motions had to be circular.

A catadioptric system is one which uses both reflection and refraction. This gives the optical designer a lot of flexibilty in producing fast f/ratio compact objectives for night vision equipment. The mirror systems are usually lighter weight than the equivalent lens systems. Most newer weapons scopes are using lenses only, but the ability to do that in a moderate size package is the result of smaller high resolution intensifiers, not that a lens only system produces better or more compact results. Generally aspheric mirors cost more to produce than complex lenses. The tolerances have to be tighter. Others here mentioned Schmidt and Maksutov optics. The Schmidt design uses one aspheric lens and one spherical mirror. It's compact and fast but does not produce a flat field. The popular Celestron and Meade telescopes use an additional mirror to bring the focal plane out behind the primary mirror, but they do not have fast F/ratios useful with image intensifiers. The Maksutov design uses only three spherical surfaces on two elements, one of which functions both as lens and a mirror with a coated spot. It's a brilliant design but it's not suitable for NV use because it doesn't work well at F ratios faster than about F/5. There are hundreds of various catadioptric designs for various purposes using more elements. The AN/PVS-4 has (I believe) five optical elements. The designs used in NV equipment are never the same as the designs used for astronomical equipment because the goal is different.

In Latin it means: "folded path"
by

In optical terms: "folded optical path".

Since the mirror in the back is very "optically fast" (very short focal length), it is EXTREMELY difficult to "figure" a fast mirror into a parabolic reflector (which is absolutely necessary to reflect the light rays back to a symetrical focal point for a clear image), so they use a "corrector plate" which is the front clear "lens". This front lens corrects for the mirror's spherical aberation. A guy named Schmidt invented it around the turn of the 20th century, for use in astrographic camera's. It still is the most economical way (and the only practical form for an axial light path in a night vision device) to make an optically fast instrument.

come on Lou...cat's suck compared to diopt's!
by

Lou, you seem to be informed when it comes to optics as well. I've been fooling around with optics and grinding glass as a hobbyist for 25 years or so (ground, polished, and parabolized a 6" f/8, 8" f/7 newt mirror, and have been working on a 14" f/5 on and off for a few years, but having trouble getting a decent figure...will never use plate glass again!) . I really believe the only reason cat's are used in NV is because of cost and not performance.

There is only one optically transmissive component in a cat, and two reflective. So you really only need one good chunk of clear glass free of flaws. Where with a refractive optical train EVERY piece of glass has to be clear, be polished on BOTH sides, and anti-reflective coated. As you also know a good fast refracting objective requires at least three elements, so that's six optical surfaces that must be perfectly figured, clear, and anti-reflective coated. I don't imagine chromatic abberation is a big problem with color not being a factor in NV, but it could I guess if the red and blue images are greatly out of focus with respect to each other.

Also it is very easy to make a spherical mirror, as you probably already know that the only two surfaces that can completely touch each other are planes and spheres, so when mirrors are ground they naturally want to become spherical. Lastly, there is also probably another reason that the inferior cat's rule in NV scopes, and that is weight. I had the opportunity one time to hold an objective cell out of a 6" f/10 refractor telescope, and I could not believe how heavy it was. Probably due to the flint glass element in the objective. focal length reducer/field flattener
by

Hi Lanny,

That lens that is at the backs of the cat NV objective cell assemblies cuts the effective focal length of the first three optical components (the front corrector plate, primary concave mirror, and secondary convex mirror) approximately in half (eg: f/10 down to an f/5 or so), and it helps flatten out the appearent field of view. That one little lens greatly improves the performance of the cat optical system. Amateur astronomers who like taking wide angle photographs of the night sky use that same type of lens with their telescopes. They usually just make them out of an old small pair of binoculars, by using one of the front objective lens assemblies and putting it before the telescope's eyepiece.

This helps 'speed' up the optics, and reduces magnification thus giving a wider field of view, but still not as fast as what you can get with a totally refractive optical assembly.

Lasers make great pointers for placing an accurately aligned dot at a specific point but that doesn't help much if you can't see the target. They can also make good illuminators but lasers with sufficient power do do that are expensive, at least expensive compared to a halogen incandescent lamp with a IR pass filter if you want a field of view of few degrees. A CCD camera plus an LCD monitor makes a decent IR viewer but unless the camera integrates it's not as sensitive as even a Gen II viewer. And if it does integrate the image will be "jerky", not real time. .

Using an integrating camera with a Sony "Hyper HAD" extended IR ccd (from JAI, under $500) will give better low light images than a Gen III intensifier with integration time over about one second with but that wouldn't be very practical for a hunting scope. Sorting out the wires
by

I don't have knowledge of this piece of equipment. But if you can't find someone with schmatics or interface specs all isn't hopeless. Were this my unit I'd make resistance measurements with a digital multimeter from each wire to each other wire and record the reading for both polarities. Some ohmmeters have a low voltage (under .5 volts) resistance measurement and a higher voltage (typically 3 volt) "diode" test function which displays in volts and allows you to determine what leads have semiconductors attached. It's generally safe to test connectorized leads with such a meter. It's dangerous to assume the color relates to any particular voltage or polarity. (dangerous to the equipment if you apply power incorrectly).

A heater is a possiblity. THere may also be adjustments for focus, gain, or to illuminate a reticle.

Used/Surplus NV equipment - A valid option for most?
by

I have seen many posts on the list regarding buying surplus/used NV equipment. I agree with about 90% of what has been said on the subject. But I feel that alot of surplus/used devices get a very bad rap, sometimes from companies selling new devices, and sometimes from NV owners that have had bad experiences in the past. In no way do I have the experience or where-with-all to tell anyone what is best for him/her.
But I feel that used/surplus NV stuff has received a bad rap, and must write this so that I feel better!
Rememer, that used/surplus device was once a brand new unit also, and maybe not as long ago as you might think!!

But please consider this. Chances are that many of the surplus scopes don't have that much time on them. Canada for instance, with the PVS-502, has NEVER had a war or been to war! So the only time on the scopes is nightime training. Now imagine what it would be like to look thru a weapons scope all night long, with one eye only!....I don't think so! So I figure that they were probably turned on for a few minutes at a time, for maybe 10 years? And definitely not everyday, as they are pretty much an application specific, or specialty item!

I use my scope more than most people, I would guess. It is a weapons scope, however, not a pair of googles! So it is only convenient on a weapon, period. So lets figure out the time I have put on my scope, which is the only "real" time that I know of (since I cannot guess about the time put on the tube in the Canadian military!). I will start to divide my time between what I will call "Summer" and "Winter". I will use "Summer" as May thru August (in my area), and I will call "Winter" Sept thru April. So my Summer useage is 4 months, and my Winter useage is 8 months.

During the Summer, I shoot four days per week with the scope. I turn the scope on after I have set all my stuff up, and am reay to shoot. When I go downrange to check my targets, I turn the scope off (duh!) then when I come back and get ready to shoot again, I turn the scope back on. I shoot for about 2 hours per night (from about 9:30pm until around 11:30pm). I typically shoot six targets, with five-shot groups each. That is a total of 30 rounds. I usually take my time, averaging about two shots per minute. Once I have shot my first target, I go and check it. So I turn the scope off after every 5 shot group. So my scope is only actually on approx. 15 minutes, total time. The rest of the time is spend between targets cleaning, bullsh*tting with the other NV guys shooting, etc.

So we have 15 minutes of scope time per night, times four nights per week. This equates to one hour of scope time per week. I also look at my backyard occasionally, take the scope out camping, etc. So I will add 1 hour of scope time per month, for each Summer month, for misc. veiwing.

Now we have 16 weeks ( 4 months) in the Summer, so at one hour per week of shooting, that would be 16 hours of scope time. For misc. veiwing I will add 1 hour per month, so four more hours. This brings our total viewing time for the four Summer months to 20 hours on the tube.

On to the Winter calculations:
Out of the 8 Winter months, I typically hunt about 4 of them, shooting coyotes, bobcats, and anything else that moves. The other 4 winter months are spent duck and goose hunting, quail, dove, etc. This means that I really only shoot with night vision about 4 months out of the 8 winter months.

I hunt with the night vision probably two nights per month.
My scope is on about approx. 15 minutes per night total, only when I am shooting.
So I use the scope appox. 30 minutes per month, for four Winter months. That equates to 120 minutes, or 2 hours.

For the other four months, I just use it for misc. viewing, about the same amount of time. So 2 more hours.

So during the total Winter months I use the scope for approx. 4 hours of tube-on time.

In summary, I use about a full day, or 24 hours, of actual tube time per YEAR!!

The Gen I tube used in the PVS-502 is supposed to have a life of approx. 2,500 hours, give or take, depending upon how well the scope is taken care of, starage conditions, if it is subjected to bright lights, tube degeneration, etc.

I can't guess as to how much tube time the Canadian military put on the scope, but I cannot think that it would be more than 50%, and that is probably WAY to much to assume!! But for fun, let's look at that.
So that means that the Canadian Military would have put on 1,250 hours on the tube (and I really doubt that, as that would basically be just like leaving the tube on for 24 hours per day, for 52 days straight!). But for grins, lets assume they did use up 50% of the tube life.

So that leaves me with a tube that will die in 1,250 hours. I use 24 hours per year of tube time. 1,250 divided by 24 gives me 52. That means that in theory, if I continue to use the tube at the exact same rate as I do now,24 hours per year, it will die 52 years from now. Just for your info, I am 30 years old. So I WILL have to worry about finding a replacement tube for my 502 when I am 82 years old!!! I don't think so!

Anyway, the point of this whole rant is that if most night vision owners sat down and really, I mean REALLY evaluated the actual time they use there devices, I think you will see that it is a fraction of what everyone believes. Not to say that each device gets used differently. Someone with PVS-7 goggles is probably going to put more time on them per month, or year, than someone with a dedicated weapons scope.
But still, do the math. A gen III would be rated for 10,000 hours plus!! So it still is a basically a wash between the numbers.

My advice to you....buy the best you can afford, use it as often as you can, and most importantly ~ HAVE FUN!! If this means that you can only afford a Gen I, and can only use it once per month, then so be it! You don't neccessarily need a PVS-7 or a Raptor to have some serious fun (at least I don't)!!

Best of luck!

Apples and Apples?
by

Hi Guys:

Personally, I think that the ITT 6015 and the NAIT NVPS-10 are both great pocket scopes. I also think they really shouldn't be asked to go head-to-head.

The ITT 6015 is a civilian knock-off of the PVS-14. As such, it inherits much of its military pedigree. It is robust, sealed, fairly waterproof, simple to operate, and great for things like putting on a headmount or a weapon. As it doesn't accept c-mount lenses and was not originally designed to go on a camera (that duty is supposed to fall to the 6010), I give it about a C- for photography.

The NVPS-10, on the other hand is pretty robust, not at all waterproof, not sealed, and allows the user to change lenses. This ups the user load (you can do thing like cross-thread a lens in the dark). It is not particularly appropriate for mounting to a weapon, and makes an OK at best headmounted unit. On the other hand, it works great for photograpy. You can put many c-mount lenses on it, and if you really want to go crazy, you can pull the eyepiece out and put a relay lens in the back.

So, in my opinion, if you want to blow stuff up, go with the 6015. If you want to take pictures of people blowin' stuff up, go with the NVPS-10.

failure
by

Batteries would be a first, I would assume you checked them carefully and the contacts for cleanliness, but I have been told never to assume, so start there. Also, remember the N-cells are backward from the battery outline on the housing, and you must go by the polarity markings. Again, no insult intended, just covering the basics of troubleshooting. I had one once that a guy had changed the switch on, so I guess those little toggles must fail sometimes. The contacts could be loose or corroded with film. If you know how to pull the intensifier, you can easily check the contacts with a meter for voltage, and you can also use your meter leads to hook up the tube and apply 3vdc to the contacs and see if it lights up. Make sure to cover the photocathode with some black tape with a tiny hole in it, or just do it in a darkened room for a second. I do have one power supply left for that intensifier, and it seems I will never find any more tubes to build another intensifier.

ELCAN is a waste of time for the PVS-2
by

I had a PVS-2 with an ELCAN mount on it. It was worthless. The scope is much too heavy for the mount, and the resolution of the mount itself was terrible.

What I did was to grind the old M-16 mount that fits on the carry handle, down to fit on a removeable carry handle from an AR15.

Then I could mount the PVS-2 on it's original "block" mount that had the fine adjust windage and elevation, and slide it on to the carry handle mount and lock it down. Then I could take the carry handle on and off of the AR15 whenever I wanted.

I basically used the removeable carry handle as the weaver mount. It worked much better than the ELCAN, but was still a 6"+ shooter at 100 yards.

The ELCAN fitted PVS-2's were a good idea on paper, but terrible when actually put to use. It is like using a Band Aid to try to stop a large leak in the Hoover Dam!!

Then I was lucky enough to be able to sell my PVS-2 and upgrade to the PVS-502. Granted, it was made to fit the 7.62 FAL, and does it perfectly. The scope has internal windage and elevation adjustments, the same ones as on the PVS-4, which is MUCH better (the only wat to go, in my humble opinion).
There was supposedly a mount for the carry handle M-16, but I have never seen one.

What I did was to machine an inexpensive $20 riser block (weaver-to-weaver, 1/4" height) to fit the mount on the PVS-502. Now I take the PVS502 off and on my flattop AR15 just like a regular scope. This contributes to it's amazing accuracy, so that I am now shooting 1.5"-2.5" five-shot groups at 100 yards all day (I mean night!) long.
