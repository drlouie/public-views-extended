Airsoft NV
by Edwin

I have a Russian Dipol Gen 2+ D-121M with helmet. It is a very good scope. Sturdy enough to withstand fire from very close quarters. Contrast is good. Cant compare with Gen3 but for the price of $1300 plus tax and shipment, it is a great performer.
Still it is 4-5 times the cost of Gen 1. In house to house search I would recommend Gen 1 like the D-3MK. You need IR anyway and when hit, it will likely be at point blank range. You can afford a new one if the worst happenned. I have seen a player cracked a lens when he pannicked and hit a wall face on. Apparantly it is not easy to see a wall close up and the mind find it difficult to compensate for the extra projection in front of the face. I have seen a PVS-14 with a cracked housing after a game so beware. Personally I would choose a scope with aluminium housing.
In open field, well, Gen 3 if you can get it. If you play sniper a Gen 2+ D141 would be nice. Gen 4 Raptor would be exceptional, but thats financially and technically out of reach for me.
IR is not very usefull with bush and trees around. The reflection from nearby leaves would reduce the contrast and sometimes trigger the over light control in Gen 1.
Apart from IR you may want to try UV too. UV is not visible to Gen 3, but some clothings glow in UV particularly the older generation of US unifrom. Amplified by a Gen 1, its an awesome sight. Problem is I only have UV diodes which is very short ranged. I am looking for a powerful UV flash light now for experiment.
Having said all these, you may want to agree with other players not to use NV killer. Thats 1 million candle spot lights. Once you adversary knows you are using NV he only need to shine the light in your direction and your scope would short down, for good if you are unlucky. The effective range of electric airsoft rifle is between 50m to 80m but spot lights have much much longer reach.
Good huning.

Sorting through some of this confusion
by

There is a whole lot of mis-information and confusion on this forum about night vision..
For the sake of "intellectual integrity" I'd like to share some insights I have learned to help clear some of this confusion.

1. FORGET about TUBE GAIN...at this stage of the end- users needs it is all but irrelevent. If you were an optical engineer designing systems it might be important, but for the vast amount of end users it is SYSTEM GAIN which MIGHT be important if one wanted to know the amount of light amplification of a PARTICULAR system. System Gain can only be measured with a calibrated test device with the specific adapter plates for a given objective lens.
Yes, the higher the number the better. EVERY SINGLE SYSTEM (even consecutive numbered systems!) will vary GREATLY from one to another in recorded SYSTEM GAIN.

HIGHER GAIN does not give you a brighter picture. The "brightness" of the image is set at the factory. Of course, those "dealers" with a small screw driver can take a used tube and turn up the gain to make a brighter picture (to make a quick sale to the unwary) and thereby shorten the tube life! This happens countless times in the industry.

2. SIGNAL TO NOISE RATIO is a very important reading to know how clear the TUBE's image is. Anything over 1500 is wonderful ("THRESHOLD" MILSPEC) of course the higher the ratio the better...But I just looked at about 500 brand new tubes this past week for acceptance...I saw some with very high SN Ratio and with very very low
PHOTOCATHODE RESPONSE. (One would reject those!) Why? Because every single tube is unique, and must be judged on its own unique stats.
Just because it is a Pinnacle tube does not mean it is the best! All that means is that it is thin-filmed with a gated power supply.

3. FORGET about alledged GEN-IV tubes. UNFILMED image tubes degrade from the moment they are made. They are great for a specific mission where street lights may be a factor for that mission, but not too good for
long term investment. The more and brighter the light, the sooner it will die. These alledged GEN-IV tubes are warrantied for SIX MONTHS from the time they were sold.
Guess why that is?

4. ITT and LITTON both make wonderful tubes. There is no difference in quality between a 10130 or 10160 tube (of the SAME GRADE!) between either production house.
This Crap about LITTON system being better than ITT is bogus mis-information. We have had pallet loads of MILSPEC PVS-7D's and PVS-14's from both ITT and LITTON, I have looked though countless systems...believe me, tear the label off and you wouldn't be able to tell
who made it.
AND EACH AN EVERY ONE has a different data record sheet with najor differences in stats...all being over the minimum.

5. PHOTOCATHODE RESPONSE is the ability of a tube to see infrad red light. It has nothing to do with gain.
(1800 is great.)

6. SIGNAL TO NOISE: 23:1 is WONDERFUL on the
RAPTOR.

7. A PROMIS DATA CARD is a valid recording of data. Who started this crazy idea that it wasn't valid?

8. Who also started the crazy idea that Excalibur data sheets were not a certification of the tube/system data? (Unless one is refering to some counterfit ones we have heard about).
NVEC is a GSA contractor. NVEC is Litton's OEM to the Federal Govt.
Excalibur is the law enforcement and retail outlet for NVEC. Excalibur is also the National Distributor for ITT commercial sales.
ITT, LITTON and NVEC are the three major contractors to the US Govt. PERIOD.
Do you really believe the fantasy that we would make bogus data sheets, and lose our contracts? We have hundreds of thousands of dollars in test equipment to measure and certify for our govt contracts. Excalibur retail sales are a very small part of our business, and no we are not going to risk a govt audit on bogus data sheets. The fact is, we are so confidant of the quality of our tubes and systems that we provide the data
sheets...why don't other companies do that?
(and yes...you can still find good quality "new" and rebuilt tubes without data sheets...)
I can guarantee you that EVERY single night vision company out there that buys our tubes or systems, demands a copy of our data record sheets with each and every system. Why do you suppose that is?
I can imagine the nightmare if tomorrow we said we are not going to provide data sheets with our tubes and systems.

8. Just because someone has a wonderful tube, does not mean by putting it in any system it is going to work just as wonderful. Remember, a system must have a fast distortion free objective lens to go with that wonderful tube.
Just because a SN ratio or FOM is real high does not
mean that is a wonderful tube! REREAD THE LAST SENTENCE! I have seen a bunch of wonderful high SN and FOM with real low photocathode response!

Understanding night vision technology is difficult at best. It is much more complicated when a number of people appear to be motivated by sales and not
integrity.

9. The best way to test any system is to go out on a cloudy no moon night (can't see your hands in front of your face) in the middle of nowhere and turn it on, ideally, comparing it with other systems.
The same can be said for a night vision rifle scope, AND, then shoot it in for a about a hundred rounds and see if it still holds zero. Most will not!

10. Looking through a night vision device at a gun show, in a room, etc. only tells you where, if any, are the blemishes. It tells you NOTHING about how well it will work at night in the middle of nowhere.

11. No, the RAPTOR is not made of plastic.
No, it is not the cheapest system available.
No, we will never put an alledged GEN-IV tube in one.
Yes, it is not light weight.
Yes, you can mount it on your M-2 .50 cal Machine Gun and it will still work night after night.
Yes, we sell an awful lot of them to the military.
Yes, it is VERY accurate and VERY reliable.

To all!
Since Newcon Optik is the official distributor of DN 310 product, I think I'm in a good position to shed some light on the issue:
First, the tube question:
DN 310 is using a fairly unique brand new Russian made Gen 2 tube, with 25 mm input and 18 mm output. It's the so called 20/30 configuration. To the best of my knowledge Litton used to make (still does?) a similar product. While a typical Russian Gen 2 tube delivers 30 lp/mm, by compressing 25mm inout down to 18 mm output it increases the resolution to about 45 lp/mm.
Additional advantage of the tube - manual brightness adjustment possibility.

We no longer carry the DN 300; DN 310 is the upgraded version. Main improvements:
- Internal focusing
- Mount moved forward
- Integrated 75 Mwatt I/R (30 mWatt previousely), with focusable lens and 4 position output
- Better plastic lens cap (instead of rubber)
- Accepts a 2x magnifier.

The Numbers
by NightVision Fan

SNR is said to be the single most important measurement, it takes into account *photoresponse*, phosphor efficiency and MCP opertating voltage.

Higher gain does give you a brighter picture, but a brighter picture may not be clearer, you can make a fuzzy image brighter or even drive the tube too hard. The photoresponse numbers are not the same as gain. They PR number measure the efficency of the photocathode, the higher the number the better able to produce a visible image under dark conditions.

I suspect the 3000 gain number listed on your tube sheet is the system gain (not tube gain or photocathode response), taking into account the optics and whole system, more accurate to what the eye sees. It sounds pretty good for a 4x scope, and may be the minimum system gain, not actual. The actual is usually not such a round number. If it is photocathode response (and I'm betting it's not) that is the highest I've ever heard by about 500, 2500 (approx.) is the highest I've heard of, and again the actual numbers are not usually so round. 1800 would probably be the minimum value for those particular tubes on photoresponse, OMNI IV.

23:1 is a pretty good SNR. Those of us with 16.5:1 or so would like to have that. Of course 29:1 sounds even better.

I calculate you tube as having a FOM around 1472+, pretty darn good.

1. FORGET about TUBE GAIN...at this stage of the end users needs it is all but irrelevent. System Gain can only be measured with a calibrated test device with the specific adapter plates for a given objective lens.
Yes, the higher the number the better. EVERY SINGLE SYSTEM (even consecutive numbered systems!) will vary GREATLY from one to another in recorded SYSTEM GAIN.

HIGHER GAIN does not give you a brighter picture. The "brightness" of the image is set at the factory. Hello Everyone, sorry for such a long letter but here it is!!!


We got some 4th/filmless tubes a few days back and went out testing them. The test was conducted with a Patriot body and the Mk.6900/7900/8900 body and the tubes were filmless, OMNI IV Mil.Spec. 64 lp/mm, and an OMNI III (I just wish we had a Pinnacle tube on hand to compare, but I'm working on it). The lenses for the Patriot were the stock 35mm ATN lens, 3x Computar lens, and 90mm ATN lens (these are all C-mount, but the Computar does not fit flush with the body of the Patriot, but that's just cosmetics and I'll be checking with ATN to see if the can do some adapters to flush-mount different lenses).

Problems, sorry
by Sid

I should have engaged my brain properly Ian, there are a few reasons why your mount has to be factory fitted.

1) The retaining screws for the mount don't go into 'blind' holes but actually pass into the body of the scope, around the area of the reticle assembly. The screws are coated with a threadlock compound and if they are unscrewed then the debris from the threadlock will be floating around inside the unit. This is especially a problem with the D-300 because if ( or rather when ) these particles settle on the etched glass reticle they will cause black spots in your field of view.

2) Removing the retaining screws will de-gas the scope
letting the nitrogen filler out. This isn't a
problem in my experience of repairing day scopes, as long as the unit is re-assembled in a warm dry enviroment but would certainly.....

3) Void the warranty :-(

The nitrogen purge removes air from the inside of an optical device, because it's possible that moisture in the air will condense under certain climatic conditions and fog internal lenses. I have never found this to be a problem with devices that have lost their nitrogen, but then I have never used optics outside the UK climate.
Fogging won't cause any damage as far as I know, just temporary inconvenience.
