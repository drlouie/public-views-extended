Emission Point
A steady or fluctuating pinpoint of bright light in the image area that does not go away when all light is blocked from the objective lens. The position of an emission point within the field of view will not move. If an emission point dissappears or is only faintly visible when viewing under brighter nighttime conditions, it is not indicative of a problem. If the emission point remains bright under all lighting conditions, the system needs to be repaired. Do not confuse an emission point with a point light source in the scene being viewed

by RJ

If it is just some dust, or a few specs, do not worry. IMHO, it's only when you have serious fungus building up that causes a cloudy image, is it worth the effort.

Also, based on your reported observations of your Gen 3 PVS-4, it sounds like you would be wasting your $$$ having the unit cleaned.

If you are seeing what looks like dirty on the tube images, that dirt is either on glass of the front or back of the tube. And usually dirt on the back/output end produces the most crud in your viewing image.

In this case, removing the eye piece and blowing out the backend with compressed air will fix you right up.

Try cleaning off the back end of the front lens, the front and back of the tube, and the inside glass of the eye piece. That should do the trick.

f/1.4 vs f/1.3
by

Assuming they're the same focal length and of similar quality you could proabably tell a difference in performance with a "side by side" comparison. The about of light which get's passed is the reciprocal of the square of the f numbers so an f/1.3 lens should pass 16% more light. That's not dramatic but not insignificant either. Since the human eye has "automatic brightness control" it won't appear much brighter but you should see an improvement in the signal to noise ratio in low light conditions.

There are other important factors which could make one lens better than the other. Not all C mount lenses are desingned to give sharp images over the 18mm image area of a typical intensifier. The new lens could be better or worse. There could also be more geometric distortion in one of the lenses. You really won't know unless you compare them. Even with new lenses the "specs" rarely give enough information.

Nitrogen purging
by

Look at this website on finding nitrogen. It's a site about food preservation, but if you think about it it's about the same process, getting rid of oxygen and moisture.

http://waltonfeed.com/grain/faqs/ivb2.html

Air is about 70% nitrogen, so it can only hurt you by displacing oxygen or just from being under high pressure should the cylinder or valve get damaged. The only likely hazard is to the NV equipment if you damage the seals when opening or closing it, or contaminating it from handling.

I've purged optical equipment (not NV equipment) by putting the equipment in a large plastic bag and letting nitrogen flow through. This method also keeps dust out if you do the whole operation in the bag. Its best to have an input and output tube with the bag tied closely around them so moisture can't migrate back in. Gas (including water vapor) can migrate surprisingly fast, even against a low velocity gas flow. This method is a bit wasteful but doesn't require much equipment. You can put all the tools you need to open, clean (if necessary) and reseal the instrument in the bag and work them from the outside.
A glove box is better but more complex.
Select a reasonable thickness bag to allow handling the tools without tearing it.

Purging does no good if you can't seal the instrument to keep moist air out afterwards. It has to be a good enough seal to at least stand pressure swings resulting from heat, barometric changes, and elevation. Old NV equipment may have brittle seals which opening will ruin. It's best to have new seals (gaskets, o-rings, lubes and sealants) already "in the bag". Some equipment has silicone lube on O-rings and other parts and care is required not to get it on the optics. It can make a mess that's hard to clean.

Dry air is not quite as good as nitrogen because oxygen will interact some with most metals, but it's better than having moist air. Argon is as good as nitrogen for purging and may be easier to find because it's commonly used for welding though it usually costs more. Do not use helium for purging. Although it's inert it can migrate into vacuum tubes through the seals more easily than any other gas. It is likely to degrade an intensifier.

purging without a vacuum
by

If the unit is opened such that nitrogen or other dry gas flows inside moisture will be removed by simple volumetric dilution plus evaporation of condensed liquid. That could be as small of opening as a single screw hole if a probe is used to insert the dry gas. If the moisture is not condensed as droplets this can be done rapidly (tens of minutes) without a vacuum. If there are droplets it takes a long time (hours) for them to evaporate but still gets dried out. Even a good vacuum takes some time to remove dropplets. It happens as fast as the vacuum can be drawn if there is no liquid water in the instrument. For production work a vacuum pump is needed to make the job economic from a man-hour standpoint, but it's not necessary to accomplish a good purge.

Perhaps you define just purging as using a vacuum, but that has nothing to do with the end results. The question was about doing the job at home. Vacuum equipment is expensive enough to make it impractical if you're only going to do your own equipment and don't have other uses for the pump. You can argue that getting a gas cylinder and valves is too. I'm not arguing that a vacuum pump won't do the job quicker. It will.

It is not necessary that 100% of the moisture is removed to prevent condensation. It's only necesssay to drop the dew point of the enclosed gas below the lowest temperature the instrument will be exposed to.

purging
by

I agree leak testing is useful to give confidence the purge will last. It can be done with vacuum or with pressure. I haven't seen the ITT purging kit but I have used the small hand pumps with gauges which sell for around $60. They're available by themselves from Cole Palmer and other sources They work ok. You can also find surplus electric pumps which would do a good job, often for less. Be careful not to use ones which use oil, or at least use a trap between the pump and the instrument if you do.


Something which hasn't been mentioned so far is what are the indicators that a unit needs to be purged?

1. the unit has been opened.
2. new corrosion is visible inside
3. there are spider webs inside (don't laugh, I've seen this in instruments which seemed to be well sealed).
4. seals appear cracked or damaged.
5. (most important) moisture condenses on the inside of the lenses when the instrument is cold.

Without any of those symptoms it's probably better to not to mess with it.

Moisture condensing on the outside surfaces of the optics of course has nothing to to the the state of the gas inside.

polish out scratches in a lens?
by

The short answer is no. If scratches are just in the anti-reflective coatings then it is possible to get a lens recoated but for a "one up" job it's expensive. You have to know the specifications of the coating too. A coating that's designed for visual use can make things worse in the IR.

Generally the best thing to do with small pits and scratches is to ignore them. If it's a big spot like a chip in the glass which is scattering light you can cover the chip with flat black paint. If you try to polish a lens the first thing that goes is the coating, and just removing that will do more harm than having a few scratches. You might just be able to purchase a single lens element (with coatings) from the manufacturer. Realistcally though your choices are usually to live with the defects or buy a whole new lens assembly. You can't "spot polish" out defects as that changes the overall lens shape. Repolishing a lens is not something you can do without specialized equipment and test facilities. If you can't make a complete lens to specs you won't be able to repolish one either.

Scratches and chips don't hurt as much as you might think. Hold black tread in front of your NV scope at night. It's unlikely you can detect it at all. It's the average condition of the lens which is most important. A single scratch won't hurt the image nearly as bad as rubbing away the coatings while trying to "polish" it.

Gen III tube damage from muzzle flash
by

The light from muzzle flash will illuminate a large area of the intensifier's screen since it's not a point source and very out of focus at a foot or so in front of the scope. It will not have the effect of burning the phosphor like the continuous bright point from a laser aiming device or running the reticle on your PVS-4 too bright will.

Any light source slowly degrades the phosphor screen, but it's the product (multiplication) of time and electron bombardment. Muzzle flash is very short in duration, just a couple of milliseconds, and duty cycle even with a full auto weapon is low.

The power supply shuts down based on the total current draw and when illumination is spread over a large area of the tube damage is unlikely. Very bright light (like focused sunlight) will damage the photocathode and that damage can be nearly instantanious, but muzzle flash isn't very bright. It's usually invisible to the eye in the daytime.

I don't know what you mean by "constant exposure", but I'd be more worried about replacing rifle barrels than intensifier tubes.
