Tony,

You may notice a little less gain going from an F1.2, but I;m sure you would see a difference.

Objective lens
by

Given that both lenses are of high quality and produce good images the f/0.95 lens will put (1.2/.95)^2 or slightly over 50% more light on the tubes cathode while giving the same image scale and field of view. The faster lens will give a noticable though not dramatic improvement in brightness. There is no free lunch. The faster lens will cost more, be larger and heavier, and give a little less depth of field though that's only a problem at short distances. You have to decide if the tradeoff is what you want.

My choice for $500 / Gen I...PVS-502 read and see why I am in love!!
by

Nothing in the Gen I compares to the PVS-502 for clarity, brightness AND most importantly...usable reticle, in my humble opinion. I have owned numerous Russian NV scopes, and a US AN.PVS-2. No comparison.

Since you are hunting foxes, i will assume that you are an avid shooter, like me.

When using a NV scope, it REALLY pays to have a lit reticle, and a reticle that is completely adjustable for brightness. Let me explain. Let's say that you are socuting for foxes. yo leave the reticle turned off on the PVS-502, and use it just like a monocular (a VERY clear monocular!). You then spot a fox walking at around 100 yards. You then take your support hand and gently turn the rheostat up, and the reticle magically appears in your scope. I like to turn the reticle up and down slowly, at the same time I am breathing, which gives a nice, unobstructed view of the target (fox). As you slowly squeeze the trigger, you increase the brightness of the reticle just enough to super-impose it over the fox. BAM! Dead fox. Try to shoot the same thing with the PVS-2, and you will really see the difference! I find that shooting with the reticle just barely on, and adjusting my rheostat as I breath, makes for the most accurate shot I can get. this is just my technique, so it might be different for you.
But i have found that I never need to adjust the brightness of my reticle over about 1/3 of the low range (it is REALLY bright if yo turn it up!). This allows me to see my target clearly, at the same time i can see my reticle clearly as well, and the brightness of the reticle doesn't interfere with my view of the vitals of the target. i can shoot using this natural (for me) technique, groups of around 1 1/4" to around 2 3/8" all day long, night after night. My average group size this summer, on a partly cloudy night with 1/4 moon and a few stars was around 1 5/8" overall. So I could reliably hit a cotton tail rabit sized target at 100 yards every time. However, I also don't shoot offhand with the scope. I utilize some sort of rest everytime. whether this is a cement shooting bench, the hood of my pickup, or a partially rolled up window (with my jacket as a pad). hey, you might as well capitalize on anything you can find to steady your gun! After a while, it is pretty natural. I have also shot the PVS-502 at 200 and three hundred yards on full moon night, with groups averaging around 3 1/4" to 4" at 200 yards (12" target) and around 6-7" at 300 yards (12 inch target). I had absolutely no problem seeing the target at all, as a matter of fact, it was more fun to shoot at cans set up at 300 yards than it was to shoot at 100 yards. Since there is no recoil on my Bushmaster V-Match .223 (it weighs 13lb), I could see the cans fall one by one!

Anyway, this is just my $.02 worth, and I am not an expert marksman by any means. But I do practice with my PVS-502 and AR15 alot, just as I do with my daytime setup (Tikka 595 sporter, .223, with Tasco Super Sniper 10x42). Practice makes you shoot better, get your breathing down, etc., and practice at night REALLY makes you improve. I practice reloading my magazines at night, as well, but I always have my rifle pointed downrange, just like the daytime. i try to do everything in the dark, just as you would if you had to do it in real life, it makes you much more proficient with both your NV scope, and your rifle. But remember to practice safe range rules and gun safety!! Before putting you weapon away, turn some lights on and make sure your gun is unloaded and your chamber is clear!! No excuses for proper gun safety.

Feel free to email me and I will send you some pics of the target that works the best for me (it is a very simple target, nothing fancy, but with good contrast - that's the key to shooting at night with a Gen I scope!). I can also send you some pictures of the groups I have shot with my PVS-502 at 100 yards (I save every one!) This should give yo an idea of what the PVS-502 is capable of in the hands of an average shooter like myself. If this sounds like a plug for the PVS-502...it is!!! I love this scope so much it makes me cry with joy every time I take her out of her military case!!!

Where this comes from...
by

I tracked down some of the FDA rules/memos on the subject. Optical laser sights are not regulated if they are low power, not beyond required warning labels. The idea is that they are visible and less of a risk to eyesight. Similar lasers outside the visible range, regardless of power, are required to have optical lights and warning sounds when in operation. This is of course stupid and annoying for an IR laser sight, so manufacturers wanting to sell such devices must request an exemption. The exemptions are routinely granted with the provision sales are restricted to military and law enforcement who will get the proper "training" to use them safely without the warning lights/sounds.

So, it isn't illegal to make one yourself (if you don't plan on selling it), or even own one. It isn't illegal to sell the IR diodes as they are safe unfocussed and don't meet the FDA requirements. Actual IR laser sights manufactured for commercial sale do come under the restrictions.

I'm not saying it makes complete sense or is fair or reasonable, just reporting the facts.

IR laser
by

I know the one in the old HP Laserjet series II works well. Probably most others. I've bought printers which looked ugly but were working as printers for as little as $20. If the unit hasn't been opened there's a good chance the laser is ok even if it won't print. Most laser printer failures are mechanical. I posted here earler about how to calculate the resistor. That was for LED's but the concept is the same. These lasers have about a 2 volt forward drop. Buy a "high brightness" red led at radio shack to experiment with. When you get it to light up you can put the laser in the circuit instead. You can do all the testing with just a volt/ohm meter and use your NV equipment to tell when it's working. Most of the difficulty is making an appropriate adjustable mount and battery holder. The thing which will kill a laser is electrostatic discharge. Work on a grounded workbench and discharge yourself and tools before doing any soldering or lead attachment. Use a small grounded soldering iron.

I suggest you read and (and contemplate) this webpage:
http://cord.org/cm/leot/course04_mod06/mod04_06.htm
and particularly figure 18. That's the minimum to do the job but it works. Do not "remote" the switch in that diagram as the circuit offers no electrostatic protection. All the components should be inside a metal enclosure, including the battery. The more complex circuits shown give better intensity stability and protection for the laser, but for a pointer it's not needed.

These lasers are not truly "eye safe" in that they could do damage if you stare at one, but in practice they aren't much of a hazard.
