Re: Where can I learn??
by

John, there is a wealth of information on this forum, a lot of posts to sort through, I know. The U.S. Night Vision site has a FAQ and other info. You could also check the FAQ at Excalibur (www.nightvis.com), and a web search for info will turn up lots of sites that list a glossary and other explanations. LAMSA.com has some good info on their site, I think it's called night vision primer.

FOM is a new way to classify tubes, the old way was very complex. It refers to the lp/mm (tube resolution) and signal to noise ration, which takes into (indirectly) account many other tube performance factors. You multiply lp/mm resolution times the signal to noise, and that is Figure of Merit. There is a post answer on it down farther on this page or maybe page 2, the answer from Rich is very concise and I found it easy to understand.

F9800 is a tube type, and the numbers at the end denote various performance levels aspired to.

Asking here is an excellent way to go, as this is the most active place for night vision info I know, and I look frequently for other sites, just to be thorough. Many pros and amateurs here, with many diverse experiences and opinions. I'm just an enthusiast.

Generally the cheaper black and white CCD cameras can see 1000nm or even the 1064nm output of a ND:YAG fairly well. I say cheaper cameras because high quality cameras usually have a blocking filter specifically to get rid of the IR component. In the daytime the IR components make the images look odd. Trees look lighter than they should. For a sensitive camera with good IR response look for ones with the Sony HAD EXview monochrome CCD chip. Several industrial and survailence cameras use this chip though they aren't all that cheap, usually over $100. Many of the <$50 board cameras do ok too.

Very best NV rifle scope.
by

My vote would be for a Leupold Mark4 16x rifle scope with an AN-VSS-() illuminator using the visible spot mode. You should be able to shoot sub-moa at 1000 yards with a good rifle and tuned ammo from bags. It's better than shooting in daylight since there's usually less wind and no mirage. It's not very covert but neither is muzzle flash and blast. With the light on, muzzle flash won't be visible from the target area. In fact nothing will be visible in your direction from the target area except for a dazzling ball that looks like the noon day sun. It only needs to be on for a few seconds. The VSS lamps have a shutter so you can preheat the bulb and not have a glow during the warmup/cooldown time.

The Mark 4 scope is about $950. Surplus factory rebuilt VSS-1's are selling for about $600. Optically this combination will far outperform any intensifier or thermal weapons scope for image quality and definition and give sharp natural color images. The VSS-1 weighs about 250 lbs and draws 100+ amps @ 24 volts. Bring a truck. You can get a miniature civilian unit called the MaxiBeam. It's only 10 million candlepower compared to 100 million for the VSS1 but it's only a few pounds. They sell for about $1800 and don't have a shutter. With either lamp locate it at least 50' to the side of the shooting position or the nearfield backscatter will fog the image. Nightforce makes riflescopes in the same class but I don't see an advantage and they cost more. .
