by

You won't get a very good intensifier for $200. Some of the Russian viewers are OK to over 50 yards if they have an infrared LED illuminator. Without the illuminator you'll see more with a pair of 7x50 binoculars. It won't be as bright but you'll see more detail if there's even a 1/4 moon out.

I have pool of water a couple of feet behind my house which I put in for the local critters to drink from. I use a cheap CCD video camera with a built in LED illuminator strapped to a tree about 15 feet from the pool. The video feeds on standard TV coax cable (RG-59) to the VCR and TV in my living room. Watching the deer, javalina, skunks and coatimundi is at least as much fun as anything on commercial TV and it doesn't spook the animals at all. I have about $200 in the setup (not counting the VCR and TV). It gives a much sharper and clearer view of the animals than I get with my $1300 Gen 2 intensifier looking from the house. That, plus opening the door of the house even 200 feet away spooks the animals. Of course you can't scan the area with that method but the animals come to the water.

57mm
by

As Litton/ITT use smaller and smaller fiber optic, the resolution gets larger. 57mm, was a size used briefly, though currently, (almost) all GEN III 18mm tubes are made with fiber optic generating a 64lp.

I would never be able able to tell the difference between 51 and 57, 57 and 64....

MCP intensifiers
by

Microchannel plates work like most digital detectors, that they have a limited reservoir of electrons that can be liberated to show an image. Over time, the response decays, preferentially where the electrons get depleted (i.e., where the bright light hits). If your scope always sees a bright dot in one location it will "burn in" because of this depletion.

Keeping the dot on night visions settings will reduce this problem significantly, but you still don't want to have long continuous exposures.

Dimming a diode laser.
by


Another way to dim a diode laser is to pulse it with a reduced duty cycle at a rate faster than the eye's flicker rate. A variable pulse width can give a very wide range of apparent brightness yet leave the full power available if it's needed. 100 hz is a reasonable pulse rate. This method will give better battery life than using an optical filter. Reducing the current with a resistor like can be done with an LED doesn't work well with a diode laser since they have a threshold current below which they quit.

The parts are cheap enough and the circuit can be as little as three components. The downside is that it requires a bit of surgery on the laser housing to install it.

One of my peves about commercial dot scopes and scopes with illuminated reticles is that they can't be adjusted dim enough for night use. A pulse driver can cure that too.

Try this to determine fault.....
by

It should be easy to determine if the fault is in the optics or the image intensifier by a simple process of elimination.

I have to first admit that I don't own a pair of PVS-7's (and probably never will as I don't like "bi-ocular" 2D vision), I own ANVIS 6 and every series of PVS-5's made. So Lanny or Chris who are very familiar with this gear will have to put their 2 cents in here if this will work.

You should be able to tell if it is a flaw in the objective lens (front lens), by focusing the objective in and out, if it is in that component, the line image artifact you describe would also change in sharpness (focus).

You have already ruled out the eyepieces by saying it is visible in both eyes.

Since the prism image splitting optics are not in the same focal plane as the output window is on the image intensifier, if the flaw is in these optics it should appear always out of focus just like a person's eyelashes do when you squint.

More than likely it is a flaw within the image intensifier itself. Kind of like the defect described in gen 2 image intensifiers called "fixed pattern noise", as this type of defect most often occurred when the tube was exposed to high light levels. Or you maybe lucky and it turns out to be debris on either side of the image intensifier's 'windows' or other optical part.

Get us posted 'Friend' as to how you make out,

Comparisons
by

Outside at night, it will be difficult for most people to tell the difference between 51 and 64 lp of resolution. Especially as most people do not know how to properly focus their night vision goggles (and refocus them for change in range!)

Where one can see a big difference from one system to another system, outside, ON A DARK NIGHT, is in Photocathode Response, and in System Gain.
Keep in mind, while all tubes are hand-crafted, and with unique performance characteristics, occasionally you might find a 51lp tube system with photocathode response and System Gain approaching that of a 64 lp system or even a Delta grage system.

If there is a moon out, or other light sources, you probably won't be able to tell the difference between 51 and 64 and 64DELTA systems.

I have seen some wonderful/gorgeous 51 lp tube systems...I have also seen some DELTA systems that had spots that commercially would be difficult to sell (but the military would take!).

When buying the higher grade 64lp and 64lpDELTA systems, you are paying a premium price for a system that can "see" in much darker environments.

Please note: Even a DELTA grade system will suffer in a forest or other "under canopy" environment.
