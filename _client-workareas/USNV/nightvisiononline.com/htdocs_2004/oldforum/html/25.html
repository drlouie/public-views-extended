lens to make 1x monocular with 25mm tube
by

Most intensifers (including the MX-9644) produce a 1:1 image scale from their photocathode to the phosphor screen, so to get a 1x viewer you need an objective lens with a focal length equal to the eyepiece focal length. I don't know the exact number, but the focal length of an AN-PVS-4 eyepiece is around 40mm, and therefore a 40mm objective would be most suitable. Fast lenses in 40mm focal length aren't very common, so I'd suggest experimenting with a 50mm F/1.2 to F/1.4 35mm camera lens as the objective. Those can be had from used camera shops for around $50-$75. If you also need an eyepiece see if you can find a surplus "erfle" eyepiece with a 40-50mm focal length. (with 18mm tubes 25mm eyepieces and objectives are normally used for 1x viewers.

FOM tube classification
by

1. Figure of Merit is the new way to calculate the quality of an image tube. (Resolution) x (Signal to Noise Ratio) = FOM

2. There are three separate levels of tubes: Under 1250, 1250 to 1650, and over 1650. The higher the FOM number the better the tube under this classification!

3. The old way of tube classification (MILSPEC, Commercial, "D" grade which, by the way, does not mean DELTA grade, OMNI III, OMNI IV, etc...was so damn confusing to everyone, especially US State Department, so that decided on this simple formula.
And yes...the old way is still used...and the FOM classification will become more and more the standard.

4. Notice that Photocathode Response , TUBE GAIN and EBI are not (directly) recorded to determine the tube quality under this new classification! (Indirectly they are as part of the formula for SN Ratio).

5. The PROMIS Card or the Data Record Sheet will have the FOM number on it.

thermal vs nightvision.
by

"night vision" usually refers to image intensifiers.
but it could be argued that it includes thermal imagers too since they can work day or night.

The primary difference between "thermal" and "near IR" which includes intensifers, image converters, and ccd cameras is the wavelength at which the work and the source of the infrared energy. Thermal imagers work from around 5 microns (5 millionths of a meter wavelength) to about 15 microns. Objects at normal temperatures actually are emitting energy at that wavelength and NO external illumination is required. You can see a person in a cave using a thermal imager simply because they'll be warmer than the surroundings.

Most intensifers and CCD's work in the 0.4 to 1 micron wavlength region. Most common objects aren't hot enough to see, but at the exhaust manifold of a running engine with a Gen II or old Gen0 viewer is usually visible. Normally intensifiers use the naturally available IR light from skyglow at night, or they can see artificial IR from filtered lamps, led's or lasers. The IR from the sky is stronger than the visible light at night which gives a big boost to infrared sensitive viewers, plus they really are more sensitive than the human eye. That comes from a faster lens, more sensitive and lower noise detector, and and more gain.

Glass lenses work with shortwave IR. Thermal imagers do not use glass as it is opaque at 10 microns. Thermal imagers generally resemble a TV camera more than an intensifier, as they normall have an array of detectors which are scanned and converted to a video image. Thermal imagers normally cost 10 times more than intensifers simply because they're more difficult to manufacture.

Image Intensifier and Infrared Systems
by Denis

There are several differences between Night Vision Systems (image intensifier or CCD based) and thermal sensor (cooled or uncooled). The major difference is which part of the electro-magnetic spectrum they are using. Night Vision device works by amplifying whatever electro-magnetic energy (light) between 625 nanometer and 950 nanometer (or 0.625 to 0.95 micron). Thermal imager will detect wavelenght between 3 to 5 micron for the cooled sensor, and 8 to 12 micron for the uncooled one. Because of the nature of the electro-magnetic waves (above 3 micron), thermal systems detects emitted energy as opposed to reflected energy for the Night Vision systems. Depending on the application, one might chose Night Vision versus Thermal Imager.

Coating on Objective Lens
by Denis

The mirror finish is more than likely a class A or B filter used in the military aviation world. Its purpose is to cut off the lower part of the electro-magnetic response in the lower part of the spectrum making it available for cockpit lighting. The problem is that if the aircraft lighting is not compatible with the Night Vision Goggle (NVG), the light will interfere with the NVG. Class A and B refers to which wavelenght the filter is set. I might be wrong but I believe that the minus blue question refers to the needs for the pilot to still see what is displayed in his head-up display (HUD). Because the spectral response of the phosporus used in the HUD is below the cut-off of the class B filter, a bit of energy at a wavelength corresponding the the HUD is allowed to "leak" and be amplified by the image intensifier tube of the NVG, this is referred to as a "leaky green" filter.

More on color and it's making me dizzy...
by

I've seen so much data on phosphor screens and types my head is going to explode. A lot of this information conflicts slightly or diametricly, such as what colors P-20 and P-43 are. Some info says they are both yellow or yellowish-green, some says P-20 is yellow or yellowish-green and that P-43 is green. At least one says P-20 is green. Many gave the color by spectrum wave, and some info charts it in color and wave.

The best answer I can find directly contrasts with what some have said here about color, including what I said before.

"For the first time with AN/AVS-9, P-43 phosphor is being used on the output screen of the I2 tube. Past night vision systems have used P-20 or P-22 phosphors, which are broad-band and produce the standard green night vision image. illuminater vs pointer
by

There are four common types of IR light sources
Incandescent -tungsten filament, includes halogen's
Arc - ususally xenon but also carbon, cesium, other
Light emitting diode - semiconductor
Laser - usually semiconductor for these purposes

Lasers are the only source which works as a pointer, ie, can produce a beam under 5 minutes of arc with reasonable size optics. Since the illumnnated area for a pointer is very small they don't have to be high power. 5 milliwatts is sufficient to over 1000 yards viewed with genII or genIII viewers.

The power needed from an illuminator depends on the distance and area which needs to be illuminated. Lasers have two advantages over the other sources. They are nearly monochromatic and therefore wavelengths can be selected which can be detected by intensifiers but not by the human eye. The discrimination is significantly better than for the other sources. The second is the small emitting area allows beams narrower than 0.5 degrees with modest size optics. IR lasers are available over a wide power range, accompanied by high prices for high power. A laser as an illuminator makes sense for a high magnification long range viewers (like riflescopes) where only a relatively small area needs to be illuminated but at a long distance. To go from a 1" spot at 100 yards to a 10" spot takes 100 times the power and to go to an 8' spot at the same distance requires about 10,000 times the power of a pointer for equal brightness.

If neither a small beam or maximum "invisibility" is a requirement the for a lot less money per watt you can get an LED which is probably the best choice up to one watt. From one watt to 100 watts incandescents are usuually the choice, and from 100 to 1000 watts xenon arc lamps are usually best. (there is some overlap) Lasers and arc lamps are expensive, LED's and incandescents are relatively cheap. Xenon lamps and incandescents require a visible blocking filter. LED's and lasers normally do not.
