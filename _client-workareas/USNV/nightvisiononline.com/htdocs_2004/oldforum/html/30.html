Raytheon NightSight 200... My take on thermal...
by

Hi there Anomymous,

I would strongly recommend AGAINST buying the nightsight 200 for sale on Ebay. I think that it's been badly abused. The seller states that the Germanium "window" was broken and replaced with a plastic one. That Germanium "Window" happens to be the front lens for the system and is very important in the fuctioning of the unit as well as giving it the 3X magnification. Germanium is the only material that will pass the 10 um wavelength that the unit measures. I have to imagine that the plastic window they put in badly attenuates the signal. When I was working with CO2 lasers, which emit on the 10 um wavelength, the only thing that we had to use for eye protection was regular plastic safety glasses, which cut out 99% of the beam.

If you're looking for a fairly reasonable mobile thermal vision system in the same ballpark as what that guy wants for the NightSight, look at buying one of the NightSight 4000 systems. Much lighter and it has image processing software built into it and you can get a brand new system for around $4,500.

As far as thermal imagery versus regular night vision. They really are two different animals. I have a NightSight 200 and I really enjoy playing with it. The advantage of the thermal over the regualr NV is that, with thermal equipment, you can see somebody that isn't moving or hidden behind camo. We were plaing with it in the desert on my last camping trip, and there was no moon or stars out with a cloud cover. My buddy walked out about 300 yards and just stood still with desert camos on. I couldn't find him with either my PVS-7 Delta or my PVS-4 Gen 3, but the thermal system picked up on him with ease. The nice thing about it was, that as soon as I knew where he was with the thermal, I could then track him with the NV.

The other thing that's cool about thermal systems (excuse the pun) is that they work as good during the day as at night. It uses the temperature differential of objects rather than just gathering available light and amplifying it.

Anyway, just my thoughts. E-mail me if you need any other information.

7A/7B
by

The 7A is an older version, but still available and the Navy contracts for the C version of the A, which is submersible. I really like the A/C goggle as it is smaller, more compact and has very nice optics. It has a slightly longer eye relief also. The accessories are much more expensive and basically you have to buy them from NGLitton as they are not common like the 7B stuff. The headmount fixtures for the 7A/C are very expensive, but there are several types including flip ups, which is a nice feature. The goggles themselves are very expensive to buy new.
The 7D is also a very nice goggle, the current issue military, and super performance. 7A/C and B/D are all available to you, though the A/C would normally require a wait for delivery as I don't think any distributors stock them. I could be wrong about that, but since they are made only by Northrup Grumman Litton for the military, they are not a shelf item. Litton and ITT both make the 7B/D, while the A/C is exclusive to Litton. In all honesty, performance wise, the 7D next to the 7C or A with the same spec tube is hard to tell the difference. The A/C has an auto on/off/IR setting for use with the headgear that allows removal of the device while on, and it switches off automatically then back on again when re-mounted. The 7B/D simply shuts off and requires re-setting to on. This is a nice feature, as sometimes you do drop the goggle off on the neck cord, and it is nice to know it is off, and you can simply mount it again and it is on wihtout messing with the switch. All the goggles use 2 AA bats,or the military type lithium cell. The 7B/D has an additional feature which is a high light shutoff separate from the image tube. The A/C has only the built in cutoff in the tube. They all use the same type image tube since the first 2 types. A would not fit B, original B would not work in A, then C/D/E tubes fit them both.

USNV D-143 preliminary review
by

Hopefully I will get time to do some shooting with it on the 10/22 and see what it can do. But for starters, it is very small, light, and compact. Controls are simple, rubber covered push button, neat and clean, one for on/off, one for IR. IR is built in, under the objective, and hits right on at 30yds, clear and bright. This being a gen 1 single stage, let's not pretend you can do a lot without the IR. On a 1/2 moon night I could see ok with it, tonight was cloudy, no moon or stars, so not much of anything coming through. With the IR on, the tube is amazingly crisp and clear, excellent resolution, I would easily compare it to a 51lp or better-that is with the IR of course. I was targeting a 1 3/4" tinkertoy wheel, the yellow one kids. At 30 yards with the IR, crisp and clear, not a problem shot. (if I had it mounted on a rifle that is) I was comparing it to the gen 2 25mm D-242 for a customers needs comparison. The 242 could see the thing for a shot with or without IR, but with the IR the 143 was so much crisper. Of course without the IR I could not even see the target. All that being said, the gen 2 worked good in the darkness without the IR, and of course has 3.7x compared to the 143 @ 2.2X. But for closer work and if you don't mind using the IR(which works really well), the the 143 might be the lightweight sight you have been waiting for.
Just a little totally unbiased review for anyone interested. Of course neither of them was a 6X raptor or a PVS-17, but neither of them costs $7K either!
