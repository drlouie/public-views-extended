Depends on what you mean by "real"
by

Any visible light that works from heating an element produces plenty of IR light. IR filters "filter" out the visible light. In practice, they often look a little red because either they have visible light leaks or are simply bright enough that your eye can still pick them up (your eye can see IR light at a very low efficiency, so a super bright light at 800 nm can be seen as a faint red light under dark conditions). With a very bright illuminator, eg. a high power LED array, at 940 nm (the same as most TV remotes) there is still some residual light although from any distance (more than a few feet) it is effectively invisible.

The only "pure" IR light sources are lasers and they have some small range of frequencies they emit light at. I think most gen 1 night vision with the "visible" red LED illuminators operate at 780 nm (+/- 40nm or so). Most are very hard to see at any distance. Gen 3 will still work at 940 nm, although its sensitivity is dropping by that wavelength, so that's where to go for a "real" IR light that is effectively invisble without night vision.

Most of the filters I've seen have a faint red glow similar to the 780 IR LEDs, so I imagine they're leaking light in the 700-800 nm range but I have not seen a transmission curve. Usually 700 nm is considered IR and we're not supposed to see at that wavelength -- but it is an analog effect, not digital.

14's
by

Yes there are many variations, and of the military version would be the most complete and best, also the most expensive. I have seen some real nice NQ/PVS-14's as far as performance. The Litton and ITT AN/PVS-14D incorporate an auto shut off feature for head/helmet mounting. The commercial ones do not. The military ones have a purge fitting, the commercial ones do not. Litton uses Fuji optics, the others are AVIMO. The Litton housings that I have seen are made from a different material, smoother and better finish at the seams than the NQ units. The tubes could be similar, or not. Commercial units do not come with image tube sheets, sometimes a promis card which gives some of the specs. Littons AN/PVS-14D comes with the factory spec sheet. I believe the headstraps are the same for all, however the little adapter is either commercial or mil-spec because it is wired for the auto off feature.
NQ stuff has camera adapter capability, Litton's does not as the optics are slightly different. The ITT adapter could be modified to work in a machine shop, or one could be made.
So after all that, if you are looking less expense and good to excellent performance, and don't care about the other stuff, The 14U is probably for you . Oh, the 14's all have manual gain, the 6015 does not.

One last theory on the PVS-4 problem. See what you think!
by

I have not been able to sort out the focus problem yet. I am going to probably send the scope to John (Doc) and Lanny, and see what they think. Also, I have to slightly re-focus the unit every 1-2 shots.

I do have another theory though. On the PVS-4 tubes, there is an indexing slot that is milled into the plastic housing of the tube. The metal stud on the inside of the housing (the silicon dot you see on the outside of the PVS-4 is where you adjust this stud for depth) rides in this to keep the tube from turning when focusing. It should be perfctly flat, and the index slot should be perfectly flat as well. The metal stud is flat, except for a small "tit" that was left when the stud was machined (basically a tiny remnant of the cut-off tool left when the stud's end was machined to be perfectly flat.

In this milled slot on the tube, which the stud rides in to keep the tube from spining, someone (probably the Israeli's) has scribed a serial nuber into the plastic, and this has a "raised grain" since it displaced the plastic on the surface of this slot when the number was written with a vibrating engraving pencil.

So this metal stud is haveing to ride over all this raised hills and valleys, when it should ride smoothly over a nice, smooth, machined flat surface. Plus the metal stud has this little tit that digs in to the plastic, and is not smooth either.

So I am going to cover both ends of the tube for protection, and try to smooth out the index slot with a bit of sandpaper (and a vacuum to catch the dust).
Then I am going to get rid of the little tit in the middle of the index stud. This way the index stud can slide smoothly in the index slot. then I will adjust the stud depth so everything slides smoothly again.

I think that the problem righ now is that when you focus the objective ring, the index stud and its "tit" have to bump and slide over all this rough engraving, and catch on it (I can see the linear mark the tit makes in the engraving!), just shy of being nicely centered in its travel pathcausing th slightest binding/tilting of the tube.
