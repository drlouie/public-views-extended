343 has its...
by

windage and elevation controls (and reticle, of course) BEHIND the image tube. The PVS-4 has its reticle and windage/elevation controls in front of the image tube.

The 343 is not as accurate as the PVS-4, and has alot of parallax. The PVS-4 has almost no parallax.

I prefer my reticle and elevation controls IN FRONT of the image tube, for various reasons....as does the military!

That is why these commercial scopes all have "red on green" reticles, they can have any colro they want, since the reticle is superimposed over the green image.

You cannot do this on the PVS-4 (and other mil weaponscopes) because the lit reticle is in front of the image tube, and whatever the image tube sees is green. that is why the reticle on the PVS-4 is white.

Handle both scopes, no comparison in my opinion.
The 343 will have a nice Gen III tube, and the Gen III tube is very expensive for the PVS-4 (and still only 45lp/mm, but nice!) The 343 can have a wide variety of tube resolutions, whatever you wish to pay for.
That is the positive side.

But when you compare the quality, accuracy, optics (PVS-4 has the best NV optics of any scope anywhere, period), reliability, etc. there is no comparison, the PVS-4 will win hands down.

I had a 343 and took it to the range. My PVS-4 in Gen II was more accurate than the 343 in Gen III!!

Proof is in the shooting..

The manual on the AN/VSS-1
by

specifically states not to point it at people closer than
100 yards. That's a Vietnam era light. The IR mode was used to illuminate an area (typically a mile away) on command of snipers calling the requests by radio. It worked well with the GenO through GenII scopes available then. The term was "pink light". It could also be used in the visible mode thhough the sniper had to be more careful of his concealment when the light was requested, but he could use a regular optical scope. His chance of detection was still low, and the light prevented the enemy from seeing his muzzle flash.

Having a light that bright close to an observer doesn't work well because there's too much backscatter from aerosols. That's a problem with any light more than a about a million candlepower, including laser illuminators of 100milliwatts if you're trying to view over a few hundred yards. Candle power is a function of how well the beam is focused, not just the total power.

by

the pins are numbered 4 3 2 1 and have the following connections: Pin 4 goes to ground and/or the negative pole of the 3 volt battery and a 517,000 ohm resister. The other side of this resister goes to a 55,000 ohm "pot" of which the other leg of the pot goes to a 2 ohm resister of which the other side of this resister goes to Pin 3. The "wiper" of the pot, which is the connection that changes resistance when the pot is turned, goes to Pin 2. Pin 1 goes to the positive terminal of the battery via a (optional) switch. A little more clarification, but in a LOOOONNNNG post
by

Paul's info is helpful. The accelerometer data on .50 BMG rifles (not the Barret M82) that I've seen did have peaks around 500 Gs. Lots of dangerous game rifles have higher peaks, but much less duration than the .50 BMG. Those few I've shot personally (nothing more severe than a .458 Win M70 that weighed maybe 8 1/2 pounds and of course had no muzzle brake) had much more severe perceived recoil than either of my .50 BMG single shots (both in the 25 lb general class).

These rifles without the muzzle brakes though don't seem to be especially hard on scopes, and I would not expect them to be especially hard on night vision equipment. The difference with the .50s (all except the M82 family) is just as the rearward acceleration is tailing off and everything is loaded as far forward in its mounting arrangments as it can get, an essentially equally severe (in peak acceleration, it is shorter in duration) forward acceleration pulse arrives as the bullet clears the end of the barrel and the gasses are diverted by the brake.

Thus any clearance created by compression or springiness (as in resilient mounts for lens cells or intensifier tubes!) results in impact shocks when the forces are quickly reversed. This is very similar to another well known phenomena: spring-type air rifles destroying very high quality scopes that cheerfully tolerate life on a .458 Win Mag---the spring piston charging forward causes recoil to the rear, which instantly reverses direction when the piston runs into the cylinder end wall buffer.

In other words, it isn't just the peak G's, it's the double pulse in opposing directions rattling them to death. Even though the impact shocks of small bits in a rifle scope or NV device will have very much less energy, the peak loads can actually be much higher than 500 G's. Things like lens cells, switches, anything with a spring in it, get pounded to pieces quickly.

The reason the Barrett M82 gives it's optics a much easier ride is simply that the optics are mounted on the main housing, and the barrel/barrel extension/bolt/carrier etc recoil separately within that housing, coupled only by the recoil springs and the buffers that stop the motion. The forward acceleration pulse from the muzzle brake arrives while the barrel is still moving to the rear and the scope sees literally none of it---just the recoil spring-coupled aftward acceleration being reduced as the barrel mass slows.

Sorry for the treatise, but if you're going to try your NV equipment on one of these, it may be useful to have some appreciation of what's going on with them. Hope it helps a bit.

Common bottled gasses.
corrosive leaks cost
nitrogen - low ok low
oxygen - high ok low
helium - no bad higher
argon - no ok higher

helium - will ruin an intensifer tube


Air is roughly 70% nitrogen, 25% oxygen,

I didn't include any flamable gasses. There are some

bright lights
by

the life of the phosphor display is the product of time and intensity of the electrons comming from the microchannel plate. Looking at small bright sources for extended time is more likely to produce damage than using the intensifier with moderately bright overall illumination as the power supply shuts down on total current. A small spot can burn a spot on the phosphor.

Looking at any bright light, be it a street light, your friend's IR illuminator, or even a bright planet isn't good for the tube, but if the image of the object is moving around on the phosphor it's not as bad as looking at a fixed spot such as from a laser sight where the spot is always present and always in the same place. Just ask your friend not to aim his illuminator at you and don't look at him with the scope.
