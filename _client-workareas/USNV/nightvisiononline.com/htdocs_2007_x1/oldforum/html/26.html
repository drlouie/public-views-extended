purge
by

the ability to evacuate the device, removing all air(containing moisture) then backfill with dry nitrogen to replace the air, and re-seal. Manufacturers do this with all good optical systems with nitrogen or sometimes other inert gasses to prevent condensation inside from temp. changes. Also helps protect the electronics in NV. I don't know if or how they can do the commercial variations of the 14 unless they loosen one of the lenses and use a vacuum chamber to do the job, but that would allow some time for air to re-enter while removing the device and re-installing the lens or whatever. In most cases this does not make any difference anyway, and most of the NV sold used, or even built in someone's shop is not purged anyway. Only if you go from hot to cold, or into extreme cold would you get the condensation internally in most cases. It is a recommended procedure though.

Advice, whatever it's worth
by

I've wondered how well these things perform for quite some time. I'm hoping you attract some actual end users to tell about that.

The objective lens on that scope seems small, diameter wise. What is the f stop rating of it? The lower the better, and with night vision you definitely want the most light to enter the system possible. This usually (not always) requires a good wide lens, and the one on Night Patriot seems smaller than the eyepiece, unless I've got it backwards. When adding magnification, does the lens come off and another go in it's place, or are you adding glass to glass by putting one lens on another? Glass to glass has the advantage of better water and dust/debris resistance as well as holding a nitrogen purge and changing lenses in a dirty envrionment like out in the field, but is not as good for image quality as interchangeable (removable) lenses.

Also magnification reduces light transmission as well, if you plan on using the twelve times configuration, you will need lots of light such as full moonlight, city glow or an IR illuminator, more so with gen 2 than gen 3, but still for both. Do the lenses have an iris? This helps set contrast to optimal conditions in different light environments, and can help with tube life (darker environment it works in the longer it will last and reduce chance of burns, etc.) but is by no means required. What type of mount are the lenses? Is it a type that will allow you to use other lenses than the ones offered by ATN?

The price on these and their kits (ultimate, etc.) does seem attractive. Almost too much so. I wonder about the extra optics, magnifier eye pieces, bi-ocular, etc. How good are they? The red on green eyepiece sure seems a nice plus though. If you plan to shoot with it, ask them what calibers it can handle. I'm not saying these are bad units, I just wonder what kind of quality you get, since in the NV world it's pretty much true you get what you pay for.

Multi-purpose devices are flexible, but do not perform every task equally. For instance, a dedicated rifle scope will work better for shooting than a multi-purpose device, all else (optics, tube gen and grade, etc) being equal. That seems to be true no matter what the cost and quality of the unit. Then again, you can't wear a rifle scope like a google (not most anyway). There is always a trade off in dedicated vs. multi-task. To put it another, non-night vision way, Magic Johnson was a better guard and forward then center, but he could play all positions to some degree.
This may not really apply to what you plan to do with it, but it could.

For most of us that can't afford dedicated scopes for every task (and I'm one of those who can't and has a multi-purpose), modular versatility is the way to go. I know how it is to have a price limit! Review how you plan to use night vision and where, and then decide if it makes more sense to get something that does only one or two things, but does them better than a do-all. If you don't plan to shoot but want the versatility on the chance you might, is it worth it to sacrifice headmount quality or camera adaption quality for something you may never, or rarely do if you plan to headmount or photograph more than anything else? And just because it headmounts doesn't mean it will do so as well as PVS-7's that are headmounted. Scopes designed for photography should do better at making pictures or video than, say, a rifle scope even though most rifle scopes have a photo adapter available. Etc, etc, etc.

If you plan on using the device in dark conditions such as rural no moon, or in the woods, gen 3 might be worth the extra money. Around or in a city the ambient light is usually sufficent for gen 2, particulary when the cloud cover reflects street lights and such.

Also, see how weather and waterproof the device is. Find out about trial and return conditions, as well as the warranty. Ask if the tubes are new or used, and where they were made.

I hope this has helped you some, and not caused more confusion. You may have received these answers in your half hour discussion already. Please post on how well the thing works at various tasks if you get it. I've looked at these for quite a while now...
