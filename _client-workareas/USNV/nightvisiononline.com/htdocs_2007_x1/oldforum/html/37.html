ref:changing a commercial visible laser to infrared
by wavelength

Tom,
In theory it should be possible to do providing you can find an IR laser that will fit. Most of the IR lasers are designed for CD-RW and sensing apps, the reds for DVD and laser pointers, hence the packages may not match. The optics should be close enough to accomidate the new longer wavelength. IR laser diodes will have performance equal to or better than the red ones (they have been made since the early 70's). IR types are more efficent, more light per mA provided, so it will be "brighter" than the red one. Like all semiconductor opto devices they are current driven once the threshold voltage is reached (1.3Volt for IR, 1.8V for red). You should be able to simply replace the diode with no changes to the circuit board since the laser power supply board regulates current not voltage, both devices will operate in the 30 to 40mA drive range. The IR diode will have 3 terminals consisting of: the laser diode, the feedback detector and either a common anode or cathode for the two devices in the package. The red diode will likely have only two terminals (anode and cathode, no feedback diode) so you will only need to properly hook up two leads of the IR diode (the feedback detector terminal is for regulating the output in sensitive applications like CD-RWs). Checking the IR diode manfuacturers data sheet will give you the pin out, a multimeter will show you how the red diode is hooked up (the Anode is the arrow, the Cathode is the bar, positive "travels" in the direction of the arrow). Here's some of the manufacturers of laser diodes:

Japanese: ROHM, Sharp, Hitachi, Panasonic, Matshu****a, Nichia, etc.

www.xxxxx.co.jp (Japanese web site, many are in english for Singapore, etc)
www.xxxxx.com (US web site)

European: Philips, Telefunken
Taiwan: Kingbright, Lite on, Arima

US: Optodiode, Hewlett Packard (HP)

It's highly possible that your red laser was sourced out of China and might be one of a kind. Keep in mind that your eyes retna are very easy to damage with IR (write once, read many) since the pupil won't close, can't see the IR to react and you won't know if you created a IR searchlight or a candle. Also keep in mind that you don't what to use your expensive NV to align the new unit (most IR lasers are prefocused since they are made for very specific applications). If you don't have one of the IR viewers (like Edmund Scientific sells) then you may want to get a phoshper target (Edmund sells them, in the old days electronic parts houses sold credit card sized units (made with tiny square of material from Kodak) to check remote controls, ask to check under the dust on the shelves behind the counter, a couple of bucks). All laser diode devices are highly sensitive to static electricity, with damage levels as low as 5 Volts (vs. the 10 Kilovolt lightning bolts you generate walking on carpeting). You need to use a wrist ground strap (like the ones in the computer repair tool kits, under a buck). These straps use a 1 Meg Ohm resistor in series to prevent you from getting electrocuted, so don't ground yourself directly.

So if you find a diode that fits, don't blow it up and blind yourself you did good. If not you never heard of me...

Image Stabilizing Optics
by Ken Doe

Pinnacle tubes ARE filmed although I didn't know they used Indium Alloy GaAs for the photo cathode.
I do agree they have wild photoresponse claims. The signal to noise ratio is more then double of any "conventional" image intensifier!

Although Image Stabilizing Optics *seem* to be magic and voodoo (the Canons *almost* are voodo) they're not breaking any laws of physics. There are 3 types that I know of:

1. The Fujinon Stabiscope which mount the errecting prisms on a gimbled frame along with a high-speed gyroscope. When the gyro is spun-up, they provide ~5 degrees of stabilization. They have interchangable oculars and can be fitted with night vision eye-pieces. The 2nd generation version of these binos are waterproof and standard issue on the space shuttle! Fraser-Volpe also make a pair. Be careful you don't die from price sticker shock, they cost anywhere from $5k-$15k a pair depending on options!

2. The Fujinon Techno-Stabi do not have interchangable night vision oculars and uses polycabonate to reduce weight. They employ accelerometers to measure the pitch & yaw and by way of a microprocessor, provide a correction to direct drive motors which adjust the angle of the errecting prisms. Hence, no gyro. These binos also provide ~5 degrees of stabilization and can usually be bought for less then $1k

3. Canon IS: These use what Canon calls a "vari-angle prism" which is in fact a lens consisting of two pieces of glass which sandwich some "martini" juice. A bellow around the outside edge of the glass allows it to be squeezed which changes the defraction angle of the light as it passes through. There are a couple of interesting things about this. The technique was actually developed by a Hollywood cameraman in the 1950's who created some movie camera lens that worked using this principal decades before the stedi-cam became popular. I suspect that when the patents expired, Canon decided to use the technique.

The Canon 10x30 IS binos are light weight and compact and can be had for ~$400. The image is amazingly flat and the 1 degree of stabilization is enough to remove hand tremors quite well.
If you have a very good eye, you can sometimes see the image stabilzation working as the image appears to very slightly shimmer in and out of focus. You really have to be looking to notice it at all. You can also *hear* it "buzz" as its working if your in a very quite room.

Once you've used image stabilized optics its very very hard to go back to using regular binos. I can't stand to look through my 7x50 M-51 Fujinons' anymore :-( Image Stabilizing Optics are amazing to use.
