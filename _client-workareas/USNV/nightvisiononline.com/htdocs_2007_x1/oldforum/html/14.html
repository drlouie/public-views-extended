Two things...
by RJ

... will tell you that your're getting an F9810P OMNI IV tube.

1) The Price...

2) The ITT Factory Data Sheet for the tube. The data sheet will give you all of the specifics of the tube. You might even want to pick from the available systems to get the one that has the best tube, but you might have to pay a little extra.

IMHO, some sites don't specify what tube they're providing because then you would realize they are over charging you for a "fallout" tube or a tube that has been repaired or refurbished.

Are these bad systems as compared to a new PVS-7B with an OMNI IV tube? Not at all, but you have to know a little something about the seller and what he is selling.

You should also note, there are only a few places that sell a new PVS-7B with factory papers. Anyone else that sells them, is either buying them from these few dealers in the main distribution channel, or they are to be scrutinized for what they are selling.

Based on your question, maybe you are trying to make a comparison between the systems from Excalibur and the host of this forum, US Night Vision.

Both companies probably mean the same thing when they are talking about a "Delta" grade system, which is the cream of the crop and cost the most cabbage.

When you start trying to compare Excalibur's "Prime" and US NightVision's "Ultra", again you are probably talking about the same thing, although Excalibur devides the category by the number and size of blemishes on the tube.

Then you have the "Choice" and "Standard" categories... which again should be similar.

Honestly, either company is offering you a nice range of choices at typical prices. You would do well with either company, although it's always nice to send the business to our host. ;-)

If you want to put together a system that rivals the Prime and Ultra systems for under $3000, then you should talk to Lanny to see what he can work out. I think he even has a "new" PVS-7B with an ITT Pinnacle tube in that price range.

Can you spend even less? Sure, but you take greater risks on the quality of the tube.

Good luck with your shopping

I had a PVS-2 with both the Elcan mount, as well as the M16 carry handle mount (which is a side mount).

The Elcan mount is a terrible bandaid for the PVS-2, first of all. It was designed to hold an optical sight that weighed about 1/2lb or less, not the 4lb PVS-2!!! Also, the Elcan does not have fine enough windage and elevation adjustments for precise shooting.

That being said, it IS a weaver mount. You basically unscrew both of the plastic thumb screws, set the unit on the weaver base (being sure that the recoil lugs/crossbars on the bottom of the Elcan mount fit into the weaver slots), and tighten both thumbscrews.
Then the windage and elevation are adjusted by turning the large wheel, and side wheel/slotted adjustment.

If you have a metal mount, that looks like a solid metal rectangle, with two knobs that protrude (one from the bottom of the rectangle, one from the top side), and a slide rail on the side, that is not the Elcan mount. That is the M16 carry handle mount, and that will NOT mount to any weaver rail, only to its mating piece, which fits on the M16 carry handle and has two locking flip levers.

Also, there should be a machined block that adapts the Elcan mount to the bottom mating surface of the PVS-2, as the Elcan was not designed for the PVS-2. It should be bright natural aluminum and be sandwiched between the Elcan and the bottom of the PVS-2.

Send me you pictures and I can walk you through any step that you like.

My guess is that you have the M16 mount, not the Elcan mount, since you said that the mount looks like it fits on sideways (the Elcan does not, and is a very apparent Weaver mount!)

You are actually better off with the M16 carry handle mount, if accuracy is your goal.

p.s. the carry handle mount WILL NOT fit a detachabe flattop carry handle (without a TON of machining/filing/cutting), since the hole in the detachable flatttop carry handle is too small.

Fat ANVIS
by Ed

The Gen III image intensifers that show up in the AN/PVS-5s are a non-standard product, that is to say they are not manufactured by any of the major manufacturers. What is done to make this product, aka Fat ANVIS, is; an MX-10160/UV is torn done and its Module (vaccum tube) is removed, clean and prep'ed then mated to an MX-10130A,B,or C power supply and potted in the MX-9916/UV's Noryl boot, then installed into the MX-9916/UV's metal shell. The the AN/PVS-5C or AN/PVS-7 objective lenses are modified to move the focal plane back 1/4 inch and installed on the new image intensifer's metal shell's objective lens mount.

COLLIMATOR INSTALLATION
by PAUL

Here is the procedure taken directly from the manual:


Insure that the collimator does not move while tightening the two screws.

Secure collimator with two screws and two washers. Do not over tighten screws. With thumb and fore finger, rotate collimator counterclockwise against stop. While holding collimator, tighten screw nearest the tube contact pins first; then tighten oppositre screw.

Of course the collimator has to be placed over the stop tab to start.
The reason for this procedure is the the simple act of tightening the screw will cause the collimator to rotate in the direction of the turning screw thread.

red light, green light
by

It's not that a red or green light is better for night use, it's that have different uses.

When the eye is not dark adapted it's sensitivity to green light (LED about 530nm) and red light (LED about 640nm) are roughly the same. However the dark adapted eye is approximately 200 times more sensitive to green than red light. Only the rods change chemically to produce "dark adaptation". It requires far less green light to see at low levels once the eye is dark adapted. Power savings though isn't the reason to use a green light for walking at night though it is a benefit.

The penalty that's paid is that the cones in the fovea which are much denser there than rods aren't doing much at very low levels so the eye's resolution is about ten times worse at light levels where the rods are fully dark adapted. This is still fine for walking and judging footing, but not adequate if you're trying to find pea sized or smaller objects on the ground while you walk. The benefit is that you can use a light several times the brightness of starlight in front of you to make walking much easier and safer but still see at a distance since your dark adaptation isn't harmed.

If you need to see something in detail it's best to use a red light if you don't want to mess up your night vision much. The eye recovers it's night vision more quickly since the rods aren't any more sensitive to red light when dark adapted than the cones. You do need a lot more red energy (like 200 times more) to "turn on" the cones but you can use this if you need to read a map or document which requires "daylight" resolution and get back to the dark adapted condition quickly. Walking with a red light takes a lot more power from the light and doesn't allow you to see distant objects as well though it gives more detail on the ground. It is certainly more visible from a distance with NV equipment.

You can use a brighter green light get to the level where the cones in the fovea are working to see the same detail as with a red light but that will screw up your dark adaptation much worse. Since a white light is a mix of all colors you can use it at very low levels like you can a green light, but the non green light is just wasted since you can't see it anyway.

References for this include MIL-HDBK-141 "Optical Design" and "Experimental Sensory Psychology" by Bertram Scharf.

PalmIR Pro
by

Your PalmIR Pro works in the far infrared wavelength range of 7 to 14 microns. (7000 to 14000 nanometers). Visible red pointers are in the range of 0.63 to 0.67 microns (630 to 670nm). Image intensifiers are generally sensitive from 0.4 to 0.95 microns (400 to 950nm) so they "see" laser pointers easily. IR pointers as used by the military are usually at 0.83 microns (830nm).

Some lasers, such as CO2 lasers at 10.6 microns would be very visible to you viewer, but not any common diode laser. Your unit probably can't even see the hot filament of a flashlight very well since the glass lens and bulb won't pass 7 to 14 micron IR.

Be happy, there's nothing wrong with your unit.

Laser power control with resistor.
by

The problem is that below a certain threshold current lasers quit oscillating and just act like weak LED's.
They don't put out a focusable beam in that mode. Most low power diode lasers have between 4:1 and 10:1 adjustment range just by adjusting the current. I'd suggest adding a 1K potentiometer in series with the fixed resistor to adjust a low power laser. The threshold current will vary from unit to unit. I'm assuming the current is 20ma which allows for battery voltage variation and the ultra simple one resistor driver. Other diodes may be more or less. To get a wider adjustment range a diode laser needs to either be pulse width modulated or use an optical filter.

Greetings....I just bought a viper and am relatively pleased with it, but...

The viper really does have a narrow FOV and the optics are not as hoped for. I really do not expect miracles from gen 1 stuff, but the optics do produce a strong "fish eye" effect, more so than other gen1 stuff I have had. I attribute this to the fast optics, and there also seems to be some distinct coma on light points just outside the center of the image.

Another gripe that I have is the IR LED. Not only is it not very effective, but it is always on needlessly draining batteries. The included IR is VERY weak and as such it severly hamstrung this unit. I carry an IR LED flashlight and it solves this problem.

The headgear is antoher issue. Although it doesn't appear as sturdy or of the same quality as the rigel's headmount, I find it adequate and *relatively* comfortable. I have used it about 8 hours at a time before and although sometimes annoying, it was never really uncomfortable. It can sometimes take several adjustments to find the perfect configuration.

All in all the viper is pretty good as far as gen1 goes, especially for the price. The rigel unit does appear to be a superior unit, but at nearly 100 bucks more. I am also pleased that the viper utilized cmount lenses and am anxious to try out different combos. You really cannot beat it for the money.
