You get what you pay for
by

My understanding is that the difference between a mil spec tube and a commercial spec tube it that when the tube was graded, it either passed ALL military specifications (and thus becomes a mil spec tube) or it fails ONE OR MORE of the military apecifications and thus becomes a commercial grade tube.

There is quite a premium usually placed on the mil spec systems - often near the $1000 dollar range for high end systems. Sometimes the price difference includes additional features or toys in the mil spec unit so the price difference between the tubes is actually less.

Is there a quality difference between the commercial tube and military tube? Sure. Is it worth the premium? You'd have to look at the units (or at least compare spec sheets) to decide if the failure area(s) were important to you. For instance, would you really care if the tube resolution was 63lpmm or 64lpmm? Likely not, but you'd likely want to know where a commercial spec tube failed and by how much.

I reciently purchased a set of AN/PVS7D's and while they were more expensive than the B's, I found the quality difference worth the price. The 7D's were cosmetically perfect and all specs were in the high end of the mil spec. If the D's had been in the low end of the mil spec, I might have chosen some 7B Ultra's at considerable savings.

The trick is to actually look through the units at a stocking dealer or be able to compare spec sheets and know the meaning of the differences.

resolution.... ctx
by franco

actually, that's not bad, I have come across Ist gen claiming resolution of 100 lp/mm!
But, sometimes these numbers are correct, well, sort of; anyway we are always talking of the very center of the phosphor screen, an area less than 40% than the total viewable area.
There has been a thread not long ago explaining why, posted by Lou Boyd (thanks Lou!):


"Gen 2 and Gen 3 intensifiers cannot have better resolution than the number of microchannels per millimeter. Higher resolution requires making more microchannels which is a more expensive and delicate process.

"Gen 0 uses a relatively insensitive S1 photocathode with electron focusing optics and Gen 1 uses broad band multialkalai cathode with higher quantum efficiency, higher accelerating voltages, and electron focusing optics. There is no intrisic limit to the spatial resolution of these tubes other than how well the electron focusing optics do their job and the grain size of the phosphor. A single tube cannot be nearly as sensitive as a gen 2 or gen 3 intensifier, but they can definitely have as good or better resolution. Some do, most don't."

Rotty,
Here's my bottom line on Gen 1; some of it can be pretty nice and yes, some examples may equal or exceed Gen 2 and 3 in resolution. None of it comes close to the light gain, though. Through my eyes, trade offs in light gain and resolition are highly subjective, personal preference issues. Whatever else you do, personally inspect any imported Gen 1 products before you part with your cash. Quality control is sometimes all over the board and I've seen side-by-side identical models with significantly different image quality.

Generations
by franco

This is what I figured out up to now about the generations actually used from the 1940's to present, including strange hybrids.
Please correct me!!!!! if wrong, as I am trying to have a complete "picture"...

Gen 0: just "translates" IR into visible by means of 1:1 electronic conversion. Uses S-1 photocathode with terrible sensitivity, but provides some gain.

Gen 0+: Essentially, a Gen 0 with S-20 photoK and added electrostatic acceleration (and inversion), but no other features of real Gen 1; gives 150-1500 x gain, this is the so called Gen 1 single stage tube that EVERY Russian built scope uses.

Gen 1: S-20 PhotoK, electrostatic acceleration, fiber optic input/output windows, shaped PhotoK and phosphor to reduce distortion. Expensive to build.
Normally used in multiple tube configuration.

Gen 1+: There actually may be a Russian Gen 1+, from what I have been told is essentially a "real" Gen1 with electrostatic inversion and fiber optic windows but with S-25 PhotoK (better gain and sensibility).
Still no match for a multiple tube gen I.
Probably super expensive Dedal gen 1+ riflescopes use this stuff.

Gen 2: S-25 PhotoK, MCP, fiber optic windows/inversion (some hybrid, like the 9644).
Now resolution is dependent on the MCP and it's spacing from the phosphor;

Gen 2+: not clear whether this is an otherwise "normal" tube with a special power supply.
I do know that the spacing between the MCP and phosphor in "offical" Gen II+ had been reduced, so the image quality is better.

Gen II HD: still a S-25 but with ion barrier film (10K hours use; essentially almost a Gen III), smaller and more holes in MCP, MUCH better resolution, etc.
This is also what Photonis and DEP Supergen stuff is about (?).
Is this stuff available somewhere?

Gen III Hey we know everything bout this one do we?

Gen 4 or "unfilmed" gen, the real "edge" (but I dont like the price tag and the fact that it is again a 2500 hour tube); also similar to DEP's Hypergen except that they do not use GaAs as PhotoK.

I have concentrated on the first gens 'cause I believe that's where most of the confusion is, especially because most of the "budget" riflescopes (300-600$) are built on that stuff.

The example you cited, Maxa Beam, is one of several product types that hold the same risk of overexposure. THE book defining these optical hazards was written/edited by Dr. David Sliney, the Army's expert at Aberdeen Proving Grounds. The laser hazard classes (1, 2, 3a, 3b, 4) are defined in such a way that only class 1 is considered truly eye-safe. There simply isn't enough emitted to hurt you even if you stare into it for 8 hours. Your cd player or computer are considered class 1 systems because no light gets out. Inside there, though, is a laser diode (infrared in almost all cases) that could be a few milliwatts (usually less than 5). Unfortunately, that type is not particularly well suited to aiming lasers.
Class 2 is limited to visible lasers up to 1 milliwatt. No ir at all in class 2.
Class 3a can hurt you bad if you stare into it, but is considered reasonbly managable for most applications. Class 3 b is considered a real hazard if viewed through combining optics (e.g. binos or spotting scopes).
Class 4.... well, one of the defining hazards is possible ignition of materials.

LED's, Halogens, lasers, and Short arcs.
by

To sum it up:

LED's - 0 to 100? milliwatts for a single diode.
Cheap. 100 mw and up with arrays. Emitts little
visible light without a filter. A single diode
requires large optics to focus to a tight beam
though better than halogen. Much poorer than a
laser for obtaining a small beam.
A good option for low power moderate area illuminator
Continuously adjustable brightness with current or
pulse modulation.

Halogen bulbs - <1 to 100 watts cheap and
available, even in handheld package.
Cannot focus tighter than a couple of degrees
because of the size of the filament.
Requires a filter for IR only.
Continuously adjustable brightness with current
or pulse modulation.
Lowest cost per / watt.

Lasers -
Up to about 1 watt for a single diode, but very
expensive over a few mw. A bad choice for an area
illuminator in most cases. The only option for
pointers and aiming devices. No filter required
except possibly to reduce the power for short range
use. Limited power adjustment range (50%) but can
be pulse modulated

Short arc lamp
Very high power. 50 to 1000+ watts Tube is
expensive with an expensive power supply.
(Requires 10,000+ volts to start). Similar beam
size to a single LED for the same size optics but
vastly more power. Requires a filter which can
handle power. Normally no power adjustment possible.
Pulse modulation not practical. The $1700 "MaxiBeam"
is 70 watts. It's 6 million CP compared to 1 million
for a similar size halogen costing $50. It doesn't
put out that much more IR, it just focuses it
to a beam about 1/3 as big.

All of these devices have similar efficiency for
converting electrical energy into useful IR light. (10-30% after filtering).
