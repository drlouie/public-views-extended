2. The PVS-14 with one of the NV-compatible Aimpoints really does work quite well, and uless I'm indoors in a room with no windows and no lights, I've never needed the IR illuminator. It's there, I've used it a few times just to be sure it works, but not otherwise.

3. The PVS-14 actually works surprisingly well behind a conventional scope. laser for pointing
by

I'm a bit surprised by the statement:
"b) you'll be giving up much of the long-range capability of that FAL with that approach, the lasers just bloom too much beyond a few hundred yards for any real utility. There are also conditions in which to see and ID your target you'll have the PVS cranked up in gain and the laser will bloom badly at short ranges"

A 5mw laser at 635nm (red) is plenty bright to be seen at 1000 yards naked eye at night and that's not an optimum wavelength for the humnan eye. With 780 to 850 nm laser through a Gen III scope (near it's peak sensitivity) with an F1.3 objective at even 1 milliwatt is much brighter than desirable at 500 yards. To use a laser for precision aiming it needs to be adjustable in brightness over a wide range. Lasers don't respond will to just lowering the current. Filters can reduce the brightness but then you have pieces to add and remove. A pulse width modulator can switch the laser on and off at a 1 khz rate with a duration of under a microsecond to full on giving a over 1000:1 range of effective brightness.

If the image "blooms" it's way too bright. It should just be visible on the target. At that level it won't attract attention unless the viewer is looking directly at the beam with NV equipment (assuming it's built with some internal baffling).

A pointer is nothing like an illuminator. It takes over 100,000 times more power to light up a 10 yard diameter spot as it does to light a 1" spot to the same brightness.

How is the "square inverse law" applicable
by

to a laser pointer? With good optics on the laser the spot size at 500 yards can (should) be smaller than at the source. The amount of light received back from the reflected beam will reduce by 1/d^2 where d is the distance, but the apparent surface brightness remains constant as long as the spot is resolved (except for atmospheric attenuation which is usually negligable). It's easier to aim at a 1 MOA spot compared to a 4 MOA spot of equal surface brightness even though the latter returns 16 times more energy. As previous posters mentioned, too much energy causes blooming and obscures the target. With NV equipment and laser pointers too bright of spot is generally more a problem than too weak.

Like I said, TANSTAAFL, (There ain't no such thing as a free lunch)
by

NVD monoculars in a head mount and a laser aiming system on the weapon do work, and can work well for close-in targets. I've never tried one that didn't give a spot size at 400 yards or up that wasn't much larger than I (like to think) I can hold. Plus the blooming I mentioned earlier, when it's a problem, takes place right on the spot you're interested in hitting. A bit of shiny metal or glass on the target makes it much worse.

The comments (from Lou, I think) about the lasers typically not adjusting to low enough power for many situations is right-on in my experience. Been tempted to fit some flip-up neutral-density filters to one but just never got around to it.

Re the inverse-square law, doesn't apply strictly on the way out, the light is coherent (more or less) after all, and with good optics should not diverge much at all. Over a long distance though (I live in the desert) there will often be enough scattering from dust/haze/moisture to give much the same effect: you'll get a return from a larger zone around the beam than you'd ever think should be there. You can often see the return when the laser is not pointing at anything but air. And with most real-world surfaces, the return is quite divergent and the inverse-square law does apply (well, comes closer anyway) from the target back to you.

Mounting the NVD along with an aiming system of whatever type you like on the weapon does away with that problem, but now you have to wave the weapon around to scan for whatever it is you think may need a hole in it. Not only rude in the extreme in many situations, but likely to cause excitement and also just plain doesn't work that well for scanning an area---you end up turning your whole body or at least torso instead of just your head.

Neither solution is perfect. That's why I like to have both ready to hand. To make quick switches of a PVS-14 from weapon to headmount or vice/versa does require using the GGG mount for the PVS (which I'm not wild about), but it is convenient and quick.

Just my views, take em for what they're worth,

reticle brightness and burn
by

A reticle burn on a front projection reticle indicates either abuse or an awful lot of hours on the tube. The normally adjusted reticle should be just bright enough to resolve clearly. If it's brigher than the target scene it will just hurt your aiming ability. This is also true for laser pointing devices.

The rear (red) reticles don't shine any light on the image tube. Not even on the phosphor. They produce a virtual image which just appears to be in the same plane as the phosphor. Being too bright won't hurt the tube but will make aiming more difficult.

resolution
by

lp/mm stands for line pairs per millimeter. There are standard test charts (commonly the Air Force resolution chard) consisting of arrays of black and white stripes at various spacings and orientations which when focused on an intensifer tube's photocathode give a corresponding image on the phosphor. The intensity also has to be specified for the test to be meaningful.

See:
http://cord.org/cm/leot/course10_Mod08/Module10-8.htm
for a picture of the chart and some general information about resolution testing. The chart is used for other things besides NV equipment.

and:

http://www.lprc.phy.uab.edu/optical.html
for what an image tube might look like near the limit of resolution.

Beware of what the spec are describing. In some cases it may just be the capability of the tube under ideal conditions. Or it might the the limit of the tube under some prescribed low light condition, or it might be of the overall system including objective lens provided with the NV device. In any case it's the lines per millimeter on the face of the tube's phosphor, so a larger tube can look better than a smaller tube with a "better" number of line pairs. For example an 18mm tube with 45lp/mm should resolve (theoretically) 810 lines across the little screen. That's better than a normal television. A 25mm tube with only 36lp/mm would be a slightly better image with 900 lines resolution. Of course lp/mm is only one test of a tubes quality. It says little about gain, noise.
or geometric distortion.

Gen 1 tubes are quite different from gen II or III tubes. Gen1 tubes can change their resolution by changes in the drive voltage since they use electron optics for focus. Gen 1 tubes can have supurb resolution if they're well made, but often don't because they aren't well made or correctly driven. A gen 1 tube can have severe geometric distortion and still have high resolution at the center (or elsewhere).

On gen II and gen III tubes the resolution can't be better than what's set by the spacing of the multiplier microchannels. That resolution is set at the time of manufacture and is unlikely to change over the life of the tube.

All things being equal higher resolution gives better ability to recognize smaller objects at a given range. There will be a "weakest link" in the overall system. It could be the objective optics, the tube, or your eye. It's usually the tube but not necessarily.

choices
by

If you go with a high quality gen 3, there is not much worry about it being outdated for a while. Gen 4 is seemingly kind of a non-issue right now. It was here, then it was no longer classified gen 4, the data is there, my guess is it will be a while before anything is available to us. At any rate, unless you are hunting in the streets of Kansas City, the gen 4 would not make all that much difference. It is basically a high quality gen 3 without the ion barrier film, and the main advantage is better high light resolution and no halo. The current run of Delta tubes I have seen specs on are so good, I don't think they will be beat for a while in performance. Of course with technology, you just never can be sure what is around the corner.
As for 441 compared to PVS-7 and PVS-14, you just need to decide exactly what the boundries of your usage are. The 441 is a nice weapon sight, and can be tripod mounted, but the 14 is very much more versatile, though not a weapon sight by itself. The 7's are a goggle/binocular, that is about it. The Falcon is a nice multi use device also, perhaps not as good a weapon sight as the 441, but then it is very versatile. There are a lot of things to think about before you want to shell out even $2500, so you are not dissappointed with your purchase a week later.

Juiceman, with regard to your four questions, they're all
by

about the same. It's in the other features that the big differences are found. With the government no longer dithering the unclassified signal, they'll all get you to within 5 to 7 yards in x and y, z maybe 10. All the consumer GPS units are quite portable, some of the newer ones fitting very comfortably in a shirt pocket. And none are much more convenient to operate than another.

About this last, when you first pick one up and start trying to use it the controls seem like something out of the earliest days of DOS, but it doesn't take long at all before they're easy.

About tree cover (and other terrain masking), if they can't 'see' at least four satellites, they don't work. There are actually some small differences in sensitivity that do seem to show up in light tree cover, but they're not large. It doesn't really take very heavy foliage to reduce the signal to unuseability with the small antennae built into most of them.

Some of the better ones do have a jack for an external antenna that can be a good bit larger and thus less impacted by foliage. I find that's also a handy feature for using mine in a car. Put the antenna up forward on the windshield and the unit on the speedometer.

The other differences? The big ones are whether and what kinds of maps they'll display, color or monochrome displays, size of display, batteries used/life. For city use, nearly all of the map loading flavor are adequate. It's when you want to load topographic maps into it that some really annoying differences pop up. Some will only take their particular mapping format that you have to buy from them. Some though are very versatile and will take maps downloaded from many sources.

I'd suggest doing a search on GPS using google.com. If you don't find a half-dozen GPS forums I'll be surprised.
