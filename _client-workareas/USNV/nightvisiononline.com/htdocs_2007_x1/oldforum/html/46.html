PVS-2
by

The PVS-2 does use a 3 stage cascading arrangement of 25mm gen 1 tubes. What you end up with is an inverted image at the last screen. I don't know the dimensions of the image intensifier assy, it may be close in diameter to the 9644 PVS-4 tube, probably slightly larger in diameter. It is very long, about 7 or so inches I believe. You would have to cut the body down in order to use one. You could make a single inverting tube like the 10160 work, but it would require major cutting and reforming of the housing, then you are still left with a scope that needs the adjustable base to aim. The reticle is fixed in them. If you cut it down enough, it might be light enough to use with an ELCAN base, that would require some testing or more knowledge of the ELCAN's weight limits than I have. I don't believe the original mount is useful on much other than specific military weapons. As for staging some gen 1 Russian tubes, it might be a fun project, but without some serious electronics knowledge or a power supply from a PVS-2 that may or may not operate the Russian tubes, it would be an improbable project. I have heard the optics are nice on the PVS-2, I have a PVS-3 objective, which is similar and very nice.

Depth of Field
by

As a photographer, let me offer a tip which might help some of you -- especially those with NV designed to accept c-mount lenses or Pentax screw mount "universal" mounts.
Most of you have noticed that the view through an NV pinhole cover provides incredible focus in a very dim image. This is because the smaller aperture increases the "depth of field" or amount in focus of a given lens. Camera lenses have adjustable apertures, or "f stops" which allow you to increase and decrease the the aperture. Open the lens up and you get more light but shallower depth of field. "Stop" it down, less light but increased DOF.
With my new (well, new to me anyway) Gen 3 monocular and headmount rig, I find that by closing the 25 mm lens down a few stops, I gain enough DOF to retain a very good handgun sight picture with good infinity focus and still -- usually -- have adequate light gain. If you own NV which will accept variable aperture lenses, this could be a valuable -- and easy -- modification.

NV hunting
by

There has been quite a discusson of hunting in the last 100 or so postings here. It's worth going back and reading them. Check the legality issues whereever you live. The use of any kind of lights or firing at game at night are the most common issues.

To sum up, the options appear to be:

1. Use a NV weapon scope (with reticle) mounted on the rifle. This is the most covert and gives magnification when shooting. The downside is having to use the rifle as a viewer or carring two pieces of NV equipment. Gen III is desirable to get sufficient magnification without monster optics. An option is to use Gen0 through Gen III equipment with and IR area illuminator. Those are specifically illegal in some places (California for example). Also there are thermal weapons sights. They are bulky and very expensive.

2. Use a head mounted viewer plus a low power laser aiming device on the rifle. Most are brighter than needed. The aiming device can be red (cheap and available) or IR. The downside is lack of magnification. The viewer could have mangnification but it would be hard to control it and the rifle at the same time. Target identification is a bigger problem than aiming. There are some government/manufacturing/sales issues related to IR weapons pointers. Check legality in your location.

3. Use a NV viewer to locate the target. Then use a conventional scope with a white light illuminator on the rifle. The light is only on a couple of seconds for the shot. This is will give best accuracy and target identification ability up to about 400 yards.
Not very covert and has legal restrictions for hunting in many locations.

Trying to use a head mounted NV viewer through a riflescope is generally unsatisfactory because of mounting position, alignment difficulty, and poor optical coupling. A reflex type dot scope could be used mounted on a rifle with a head mounted vewer. I don't know of one which is suitably built though as the dot is far too bright on most units even at it's lowest setting. It would have no magnification. An unusual forward mounting position would also be required.

Night binoculars
by

Optical theory prevents any optical binocular or telescope from giving an image with greater image surface brightness (per unit of apparent angular area) than the unaided eye. So it boils down to binoculars which have quality optics which includes good coatings and good internal baffeling. Most quality brands have that. IF you look at the objective end and can see your reflected image the optics aren't well coated. All the light should go through. Better units pass 95% or more of the available light. To see the differency look at a clear sky with your naked eye and through the binoculars. Perfect optics would be exactly equally bright and it can't do better.

Optics for night use must have an exit pupil equal or larger than the eye's pupil. Generally than means the objective lens diameter divide by the magnification must be equal or larger than the eye's pupil. The typical young human eye is about 7mm. That get a little smaller with age. 50mm/7 = 7.14 so that's consider a "night" binocular. So are:
6x42
8x56
10x70
12x85
15x105
etc
but binoculars of 10x70 and larger are getting pretty bulky and difficult to hold by hand. The dark adapted eye does not have very good resolution because of the sparse distribution of the low light "rods" in the eye and higher magnification does help quite a bit. You can buy 20x140 Russian survailence binocualars for about $4000. They give spectacular nighttime views, but weigh over 100 lbs with their tripod mounts.

As to brand, you can't go wrong with Leica or Zeiss. Big bucks though. There are some quite good Japanese and American units. Read the reviews. There is nothing magic about any brand so don't believe too much hype. Steiner makes good binoculars but their advertising hype is truly rediculous. A lot of what makes good (and expensive) binoculars is their mechanical ruggedness.

Another point, having good eyecups which seal out even starlight allows you to make maximum use of your eyes dark adaptive capability. It definitely make a difference to block ALL light not comming through the optics. My favorite night binoculars are a pair of 10x70's from Orion. They're Japanese. They're not very expensive but you definitely see more detail than with a 7x50 set. 7x50 is probably the best overall compromise for wide field, stable handheld viewing, and moderate weight.

I tend to agree. If you got your night vision system...
by Doc

from a respectable dealer, then the image tube surfaces should have been pristine and the only dust/dirt should be on the "user" (outside) lens surfaces.

To clean the outside lens surfaces, ALWAYS blow them off first with a gentle lens blow off system, available at any camera store. NEVER wipe off a lens surface that has any type of dust or grit on it as you will ruin any coating on the lens. Then, get yourself some lens paper (same camera shop) and gently, and I mean GENTLY, wipe the lense. If you have fingerprints on the surface, then clean alcohol (not the kind you drink) applied to the lens paper will help.

If you suspect that the tube has any contamination on its surfaces, then I highly recommend that you send it to a qualified dealer to have them clean it.

Have fun.
