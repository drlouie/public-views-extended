Review of Gen III Omni IV tube...last night.
by

Night before last, I installed the F9860B Omni IV Gen III tube (MX-11620/UV) in my PVS-4.

It replaced a Litton MX-11620/U Gen III tube, which was the same basic tube as ITT's "enhanced Gen III" tube, the F9860C. The Litton tube was also new in the package, and only has 1 hour on it so far! It has a few minor blemishes, but is a true mil spec tube, with mil stickers, documents, mil packaging, desicant, etc. I still have all the packaging it came out of (it was only taken out of the brand new pack about 2 weeks ago, by me!! It is as close to new as you can get!) I am tempted to sell it, since I have a newer ITT tube now. If interested, let me know! It is basically brand new, used once. This is NOT a surplus tube, it is a military issue tube. Warranty expired in 2000 0r 2001, I can't remember.

Anyway, on with the review of the new ITT tube:

I took the scope, along with my Bushmaster Flattop .223, to the rifle range to see how it performed. It was extremely cloudy, with no moon whatsoever. I was able to easily see berm behind the 500 yard target pits, and actually alot farther than that, probably 700 yards or more. From one side of our range, to the trap/skeet tower area, it is approx 1,100 yards. I could see the door to the tower and the outline of the warning sign on the tower, no problem!
This tube performs about 100% better than the basic Gen III Litton tube did. You can really see the difference between the signal-to-noise ratio (15.5 versus 17.5), and the image is much less grainy/flashy. I would estimate resolution at around 50 lp/mm. Actually, the 64lp/mm 18mm tube that I saw in the D-343 I had earlier this year was only slightly better in resolution (but a much smaller FOV). So maybe it is higher than 51 lp/mm. MR, who is the F9860 series tube engineer at ITT said that most of the B version tubes he was seeing coming out of production were around 56-62 lp/mm, with the Omni IV Mil spec calling for a 45 lp/mmm MINIMUM. The field of view thru this 25mm tube is absolutely wonderful.

I shot two, five-shot groups at 100 yards, and then a single shot at a 10" 200 yards plate. The 100 yard groups were 0.562" and 0.625", extreme spread! The 200 yard single shot struck the 10" plate almost dead center, with the shot being only 1"low/1"right. I was using Coyotee Varmint Duster, 55gr. Hornady V-Max, made by 3-D Ammo (who is now owned by Hornady). So this was not match/hanloaded ammo, but much better than my IMI made FMJ surplus ammo!

This is one accurate scope! And now, with the ability to see anything at pretty much any yardage, my night life is perfect!

I left the range, locked the gate, and decided to see how well the scope performed in the darker tree lines that were totally black shadows. To my suprise, I could see about 10 feet into the treeline, from about 200 yards away! As I was scanning the tree line, and adjacent soy bean field, I had a wonderful suprise! There were 14 deer (I counted them!) bedded down for the night, all over the bean field! There were a few solitary animals, and then two groups with about 4-5 deer per group. The closest one was about 100-150 yards away, and the farthest was around 350. I watched them for aobut 5 minutes, then started to whistle and clap my hands. They wouldn't budge, they would just lift their heads up and look in my direction. They seemed to not notice me (or take notice of me) at all. They did not spook like the deer you see driving down the gravel road. They didn't seem to mind. If it was deer season (and if it was legal to hunt at night), I would have filled my tag on the spot! Anyway, it was a wonderful end to a perfect night.
The new Omni IV tube has much better resolution than the plain Gen III tube. Actually, I cannot think of a shooting situation that would require any more resolution than this tube provides.

I am sure the Raptor is an upgrade to my PVS-4, but it will have to be MUCH better to warrant the price difference. And then again, how much better do I need?
I would feel completely comfortable making 400 and 500 yard shots with the PVS-4, even longer if it was mounted on a larger caliber (like .308) than my .223.
You can see that well with the scope!

If anyone is interested in pictures of the tube, packaging, sticker, etc. let me know and I will take some pics of it.

If you want to see my targets, I have pictures of them also, send me an email!

If you want pictures of the deer, as seen thru my scope, I can't help you there. I did not have a camera or camera setup! :-( BUT if you know how to adapt a camera to the PVS-4, please email me!!!

I would like to start taking pictures of the stuff I look at through the PVS-4 at night, I think a nice deer with my M16 reticle over his/her heart, at night, in a soybean field, would make a beautiful Christmas card for our Family this year!!

Lens question
by

The lens stop is simply the focal length divided by the effective entrance pupil diameter.
To use your example I'd expect a 17-105mm F/2 lens (which may exist) to be F/2 at least at the 17mm setting. It would only need a entrance pupil of 8.5mm. To be f/2 at 105mm it would be a fairly large lens, 55mm in diameter. Many zoom lenses do maintain their F ratio across the zoom setting. Others just rely on the camera's automatic gain. Generally if the entrance lens is as large as the focal length divided by the F number than the lens will maintian a constant F ratio as it's zoomed.

As to whether all lenses with the same numbers are equal in brightness that's pretty much true except for really junky lenses which may have poor coatings or on zoom lens which may not have constant F ratios over the entire range. THe trouble is there are quite a number of specifications which usually aren't mentioned. There are several distortions which can be a problem for use of a lens with a night vision scope.

The most obvious specifications include image scale which is just the focal length and the brightness which is mostly given by the F number on a fixed lens. On zoom lenses just assume the F number is for the shortest focal length unless other information is given. Those are almost always given, But there are other spec's which are rarely listed. One is pincushion. This is the effect that if you look at a square object (like a building) the sides, top, and bottom will not appear straight. They may either bulge out at the corners or the midde of the sides. Lenses which give less than 1x magnification usually show at least a littl pincushion but up to 2x it's not uncommon. Another problem is that some lenses aren't corrected to give the same focal length in visible and IR light. This means that with a scope which can use both you'll never get sharp images even though the lens might be fine with film which isn't IR sensitive. Some lenses also have internal reflections in the IR. This can give "halo's" around bright objects.

It's been my experience that price doesn't guarantee a good lens for night vision use. A lens may be great for film or with a color video camera but be rather poor for night vision. It also depends to some extent on the NV scope. Both the wavlength range and diameter of photocathode may be different for different scopes.
It's also true that some oriental lenses are blatant copies (optically) of high quality lenses designed by others and the price can be less than half. Some fast TV lenses are made for 4-5mm CCD's and perform very poorly with an 18mm intensifer.

I don't have a good solution other than to try different lenses and see what works for you. SOme lenses have been discussed on this group, but not too many with side-by-side comparisons.

Two that I've been happy with are the 75mm F/1.3 lens and the 25mm F/0.95 lens both made by Universe Kogaku.
The shorter lens shows a bit of pincushion but not objectionable. Both have good vis/IR focus. I've not used any zoom lens which I though was suitable for use with an intensifier. In my opinion anything slower than about f/1.5 is a poor choice for use with an intensifer. Lenses I've used faster than F/0.95 all have had some objectional property, either halo or pincussion, though they do give bright images.

Corsak vs PAQ-4C
by


Asking that question might be like asking why the HK UTL is a better flashlight than a Tac-Star or Sure-Fire

The main reason the PAQ-4C costs so much more than the Corsak (real world prices are $600 and $160, the rest is market driven) is that the PAQ-4C is made by a US defense contractor that has customers who are willing to pay top dollar for the product. As for the quality being better, yes it is, but when you start reaching into street prices on the two which are $3,000 and $350 respectively, then what you are buying on the difference is status. In both cases, the end user is feeding a food chain of suppliers that work to get the items from a restricted market to a more or less open market.

Compare these

Bulk/weight - Winner is the Corsak. Lighter and smaller.

Waterproof - Winner is PAQ-4C - submersible while the Corsak is only water resistant. Note that a Corsak may not be permanently damaged from freshwater entering the unit, but saltwater will corrode the internals.

Durability - Toss-up. Both units have heavy duty housings and mounts. The corsak has a replaceable lens system and other components can be more easily repaired or replaced, but availability can be an issue. Corsaks use a metal alloy housing that is very tough, PAQ-4C uses a heavy duty plastic.

Warranty - Depends on who you are. You can get warranty service on Corsaks if you buy them from some vendors like savvysurvivor.com. Government types can get warranty service on PAQ-4s but it takes a while and LEO status does not make one eligible for warranty repairs - they only deal with departments and organizations. Parts are not readily available but mounts are. Worst case scenario if you are not a .gov organization, you can send your $3,000 PAQ-4C in for repairs and on top of never seeing it again, you will be threatened with jail time.

Fit& finish - PAQ-4C is the hands down winner. They come in a nice pelican box with top shelf quality finish and materials you would expect from a high dollar camera. The Corsaks are unceremoneously epoxy painted and packed in a small nylon bag.

Laser dot quality - PAQ-4C is the winner. It uses a higher quality and more powerful diode than the Corsak. The Corsak dots can have some "splash". "Halo" or "Ghost dot" depending on the power setting and variations in the exit lenses of the diodes. In theory, a PAQ-4 can be used to engage targets at longer range, but given target recognition distances with even the best NV equipment are limited to around 100 yards which is within the abilities of both lasers, the sharper dot of the PAQ-4C is hardly worth the premium cost.

Adjustability - Toss-up. The Corsak uses a Gimball ball and socket type adjustment while the PAQ-4C is more or less conventional opposing screws. People have their preferences on these. Generally, the PAQ-4C is easier to adjust, but the Corsak holds its setting better.

Mounting options - Corsak is the big time winner here. Most Corsaks sold in the US include a compact and simple Picatinny compatible mount. This allows the Corsak to be adapted to nearly anything that can be adapted to a Picatinny rail and the fairly compact size of the Corsak. The PAQ-4C on the other hand, requires a costly series of dedicated adapters and the size of the unit itself seriously limits mounting applications and makes practical handgun mounting options virtually impossible.

Availability - Winner is Corsak. Since they originate outside the US, they are available for delivery to most countries without US export restrictions, unlike the PAQ-4C which is restricted. The Corsak had been engineered with safety regulations of the civilian market in mind (5MW max output) so it is less restricted than the PAQ-4. Being that the Corsaks never have been a USGI item, none of the samples on the market are or can be claimed to be stolen GI property. There are no commercial imports since the FDA had not formally approved them as a weapon sight, but they have been allowed under "other" IR laser devices and are adapted for weapon use through the use of various seperate mounts. Hence, many Corsaks that are available to the US market are marketed as "laser pointers with optional mounts" and are brought in small numbers from Europe by private parties.

The PAQ-4C is powerful enough to be compatible as a spotter unit for laser guided bombs and whatnot, but again, the average shooter, LEO, or survivalist is not going to be spotting laser guided bombs very often.

red/green reticle, parallax
by

You're customer does sound like a whiner. However,
two color reticles do open the possiblity of introducing parallax error as the image which counts is the one on a human's retina. It is not just a function of the scope's optics, but also that of the eye and perhaps glasses or contacts. Human eyes are not achromatic, neither are glasses. A scope can be properly adjusted for one persons's eyes and not be right for another person IF the light from the image and the reticle are well separated in the spectrum. Normally a human eye fuses the color images in the brain, but with a reticle superimposed over an image there is nothing for the brain to use for alignment since the images are comming from separate sensors within the eye.

Given the choice I wouldn't use a red reticle with a green or blue phosphor screen. It's just asking for an unnecessary source of error. How big of error will be depenent on the individual using it.

A front projection reticle like on the AN/PVS-4 cannot have a parallax problem, at least not with respect to eye position. A rear retical the same color as the image can give a parallax error, but it will be the same for everyone.

Your choices seem to be:
1. refund is money and don't sell to him again.
2. tell him how to adjust the reticle to match his
eyes but assure him it will void any warranty.
3. Have him bring the scope to you and adjust it to
his satisfaction.
4. replace the reticle's red LED with a green one and
readjust it.
5. Politely tell him to get lost.

2 and 3 are only reasonable choices if the reticle position is adjustable. 4 may be difficult depending on the design. Probably 1 and 5 are your only reasonable choices.
