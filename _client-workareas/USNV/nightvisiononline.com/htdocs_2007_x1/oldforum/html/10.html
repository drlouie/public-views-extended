Laset filter
by Jimbo

I'm not sure about a light filter, but the laser filter blocks specific frequencies of light. It is meant to protect the NV user from laser damage where lasers of sufficient power are likely to be encountered.

In realistic terms, if you are looking at folks through your NV that you expect to be pointing powerful lasers back at you (like in military combat) a filter is a good idea to prevent damage to your eyes. Unfortunately filters only work on very narrow bands (if you still want to see anything through them) so you have to know what frequencies you need to protect yourself from and there is a lesser degradition of light on other frequencies that may impact your NV effectivness.

FDA Regulations on lasers 21CFR 1040.10 and 21CFR 1040.11
by

FDA Registration

All laser products entering the USA must be registered with the FDA to legally enter the country.

* In the USA laser safety is dealt with by regulations which are part of federal law (as opposed to Europe where manufacturers self certify to various product
standards). CDRH, which is part of FDA, has the task of enforcing the regulations. The laser product regulation is known as 21 CFR 1040.10.

* To legally enter the USA a laser product must have an accession number which needs to be quoted on the 'importers declaration' . To obtain the accession number a 'laser product report' must be submitted to the FDA. The FDA do not make any charge for receiving the report or issuing the accession number.

* Anyone can submit the 'laser product report'. However, compilation of the report is extremely laborious, time consuming and confusing for those who are not used to it. In addition, unless you are familiar with the regulation it is quite likely that the report will contain inaccuracies which may cause a problem with the FDA.

What you're calling "ghosting"
by

is, I believe, more commonly called persistance. It's a function of how long light is emitted by the phosphor after electron bombardment ceases. Older designs of intensifiers usually had longer persistance as it tends to reduce the "sparkle" at low light levels. With GenIII tubes phosphors were selected with faster decay. Phosphors are available (to the design engineers) with persistance from microseconds to minutes. The selection of what to use depends on the application.

The existance of persistance is not a sign of a tube wearing out. It changes very little over the life of a given tube.
