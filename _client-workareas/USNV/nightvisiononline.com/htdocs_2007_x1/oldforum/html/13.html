wavelenght range
by

No, The wavelength range has not really been broadened, at least not for image intensifiers.

Gen 0 (activer IR) Ag0 cathode. Had some response from about 350nm (uv) to 1600nm. However, they have a lot of thermal emission so they cannot be used with microchannel plates or by stacking tubes. Their noise at "room temperature" is greater than the emission from starlight. They could give good performance with cryogenic cooling to liquid nitrogen temperatures but that's not very practical. These are still handly for lab work for viewing lasers. They were used with liquid nitrogen for astronomical research in the 1950's .
Gen 1 Used Multialkalai cathodes. These have fairly low noise. THeir response starts around 350nm (limited by the glass window), peak in the green, then falls off smoothly to zero at about 1000nm. These tubes don't have electron multipliers so they had to be stacked to give good gain. They use electron optics which usually has some geometric distortion though they can have excellent resolution. (many don't)

Gen II uses essentially the same cathode used in Gen 1 coupled to a microchannel plate which is hundreds of thousands of parallel electron multipliers. This gives about the same gain as stacking three gen1 tubes in a much smaller and lower power package. It also has significantly less geometric distortion. Resolution is limited by the number of microchannels. Technology keeps increasing the number of microchannels.

Gen III uses a Galium Arsinide cathode. GaAs has significanly better quantum efficiency in the range of 600 to 950nm than multialkai with similar noise performance. The fact that the night sky is much brigher in that range gives them their main performance acvantage. Their response falls off sharply to zero around 1000nm too. Neither gen1,genII, or gen III tubes can see comonly used yag lasers at 1064nm. Gen 0's can. While GaAs intrinsicly has response to 350nm like the others, these tubes often have the visible portion of the spectrum supressed with a filter coating built into the tube. It helps reject scattered light in the atmosphere which improves contrast and improves long distance viewing range.

So with the various generations the wavelength range has actually been reduced. THe wavelength range has been taylored however to give optimum nighttime performace. Sensitivity has been greatly improved and
noise has been minimized without resorting to cooling.

There are some intensifiers made with Indium doped GaAs photocahodes. These allow seeing the beams of YAG lasers and are helpful in certain military target designation rolls. I've never seen one on the civilian market.

Silicon CCD's give response from UV to beyond 1100nm (either end or middle of that range can be enhanced with the manufacturing). Cooling and extended exposures are required to achive "night vision" results although they work fine with active illumination like the Gen0's. Actually all of the generations will work with active IR illumination.

Higher performance can be achived with cryogenic cooling of various solid state detectors. That has to be coupled with low noise readout techniques as there is no solid state equivalent to a microchannel plate. Solid state InGaAs detectors can see out to 1700 nm where sky brightness is greater still and air transparency is even better. The cost of these viewer however is as much or more than long wave thermal viewers and not presently suitable for handheld viewers or goggles. Longwave viewers are usually solid state microbolomometers which are sensitive to all radiation from down to about 20000nm. They are then filtered to give the desired bandpass. They require complex readout electronics.

ccd vs intensifier
by

Both CCD's and intensifiers have their places for night vision. Intensifiers win if you want light weight, low power consumption, and real time motion observing. The best performing units (Gen III) work primarily in the near infrared from 600-950nm. The microchannel plate amplifiers in intensifiers really don't have an all sold state equivalent.

CCD's can give higher quality (resolution and noise) images but generally require longer exposures to get down to "starlight" levels and long exposures require cooling the CCD. CCD's do work fine with infrared illuminators which gives both good motion viewing and low cost but limited viewing distance. CCD's are great for remote viewing. (mount the camera outside, view from your living room). Cooled CCD's can provide color viewing in starlight but it requires exposures of several seconds, so motion will be blurred.

CCD images are easy to record.

There is of course the capability of putting a ccd after an intensifier. The best ones have the intensifiers fiber bundle bonded directly to the CCD, but they're quite expensive. You can get decent results by just mounting a CCD camera with normal lens after the eyepiece of an intensifier.

It's not a mater of which is better, it's a matter of what application you have. For a "starlight" portable viewer, there is nothing on the market to my knowlege using a CCD which compares favorably to a Gen III viewer if you want it to be reasonably portable.

by

Generation IV night vision (it's not even called that anymore, it's called gated/unfilmed) is NOT the only (or first) night vision that has/had 64lp resolution. The OMNI IV contract called for 64lp resolution, and that's an old contract that was for gen III. They are now on OMNI VI! Those are usually three year contracts lately (I believe the OMNI I might have been five year, and another OMNI was two year with an option for a third, I think). lens for use with intensifier.
by

First you must consider what kind of nv tubes you have.
What generation tube are they?
Do they have flat cathodes? (some are curved)
How accessable is the cathode (how far behind the face of the tube?
What is the diameter of the active area of the cathode?

THen you have to consider the application. Do you wnat to make a hand held viewer with te ability to focus close? Goggle or monocular for walking around? A long range surveillance device, a weapons scope?

Fast lenses in the range of F/0.8 to f/1.3 ususally give the best low light performace. Faster is better for brightness but distortions and weight make lens only designs faster than about f/0.95 a problem. Some of the catadiopric (mirrors+lens) systems are as fast as F/0.5 They have to be designed to work with the shape of the cathode. For example old lenses from WWII era NV equipment are often designed to work with a curved cathode and are very poor with a flat intensifer. Fast lenses are very unforgiving when even slightly out of focus. Then the image has to cover the field of tube. Lenses for 35mm film camras are usually ok with 25mm tubes. Lenses for 16mm movie cameras are usually ok with 18mm cameras. Some lenses for TV use are made for 1/2" or samller CCD's and dont give sharp images near the edges of an 18mm tube.

Other consideration include whether the lens gives the same focal plane in the visible and infrared. TO use with a genII tube the optics have to focus the same for both or it will never be in sharp focus. Some GenIII tubes are a bit more forgiving as they have the visible response suppressed on purpose. Camera (film and CCD) lenses designs for color use generally don't care what the focus is like in the IR.

For a weapons scope camera lenses are generally unsuitable as all the focus mechanism is too loose to hold zero. THey aren't designed to hold up to recoil either (though some better ones are fairly rugged). You also need to consider the size and weight of the lens if you intend to carry it around. A AN/TVS-4 has great optics but it requires a strong tripod just to hold it. There are occasionally AN/TVS-2 optical assemblies on the surplus market but they usually won't work with a GenIII tube because they don't have adequate back focus to reach inside the tube.

If you state the kind of tube and your desired use it might be practical to suggest some suitable lenses.

Typically, NV magnification will be calculated as following:
(Objective lens focal length/eye pece focal length) x tube internal magnification.
Some Gen 1 tubes have 0.4-.6 magnification, some Gen 2 tubes have 1.5x magnification.

(Objective lens focal length/eye pece focal length)

Differences...
by RJ

Although 19 and 21 SNR levels are very close, you can tell the difference, particularly in very low light levels. The difference provides a slightly clear image due to less apparent noise and scintilation.

Move up to the 25-26 SNR and the image quality improves even more.

You should note, a higher SNR does not provide a brighter image, just better clarity. The better clarity make make you think the image is brighter even though the system gain is identical to a system with lower SNR.

One other thing others have mentioned. Alot goes into making a system with a higher SNR. In general, a system with higher SNR also benefits from better specs in other areas. So, with all things being equal or nearly equal, shopping for a tube based on SNR is not a bad idea.

Optical scopes at night
by

It's not that it's made by ATN, (though that's nothing to recommend it). It's simply the laws of physics. An optical scope cannot produce a brighter image (more photons per square mm on your retina) than you can see naked eye. It can produce a larger image with does improve your ability to see detail in low light.

The exit pupil size is the diameter of the objective divided by the magnification (optical scopes only).
If a scope has a large enough objective to produce an exit pupil as large as the pupil of the human eye using it having a larger objective will not make the image any brighter. The "normal" human eye of a young adult is about 7mm. With age this will be about 6mm around 50 and 5mm around 70. So if your young an 80mm objective will give give an image equally bright compared to naked eye up to 80/7 = 11.4 power.

In bright moonlight such a riflescope will work fairly well. The color receptors in the human eye are quite dense but not very sensitive. The rod sensors in the eye are the detectors which "dark adapt" but they are only about 100:1 the density of color receptors which make up the fovea. So you get about a 10:1 loss of resolution if it's not bright enough to see color.

So, the ATN-4-12x80 will proabably work about as well as a Raptr with a full moon overhead. The image won't be as bright, but you would be able to ame the
rifle as well. It won't even come close in starlight.
Image intensifiers "cheat" by using three factors.
1. they allow very fast optics. They human eye cannot use optics directly faster than about f/3. Night vision scope are generarlly f/0.5 to F/1.3.
2. They make use of the fact that the starlit sky is birghter in the near infrared, and all night vision intensifiers use near IR. The genIII's as found in a Raptor are particularly good for this.
3. THey use enough gain that the image from the phosphor is bright enough to allow the color receptors in the fovea to to work. That give back the factor of 10 resolution lost with the use of optical only scopes.

Save your money for a good genIII scope. An 80mm scope will be awfully bulky on a rifle anyway.

Big eyepiece for use with intensifier
by

Using this eyepiece isn't quite like looking at a 4" screen. It's a lot more like looking at a 1" screen though a 4" diameter magnifying glass. It does not give the same off axis view that just looking at a large screen would. The virtual image is not at the surface of the lens or even at the position of the intensifiers phosphor. It appears to be well in front of the entire scope.

This sort of a lens was an option on the Vietnam Era AN/TVS-4. It does allow a person to view with both eyes which makes viewing more comfortable for most people. Two people can look at once if they press their heads together and each person only uses one eye. Three or more normal humans could not use it simultaniously.

As a point of interest this type of eyepiece can only work with a luminous screen which emits light over a wide angle. If you tried to put one on a spotting scope or similar it would not allow even one person to view with both eyes.

If you haven't noticeed night vision scopes (with intensifiers or video screens) have huge exit pupils compared to binoculars or telescopes. Unlike optical scopes their exit pupil is not determined by the size of the objective divided by the magnification.
