There is no gen 4(officially) and Pinnacle is
by

not readily available commercially. Pinnacle is designated "RG", the "L" is omni-5 spec tube. "Gen 4" is filmless, unlike the thin film Pinnacle tube. They are similar in performance however. Both are being sold to the military but neither is easily available commercially. There are some tubes being sold as gen 4 which I assume are filmless/auto-gated tubes. I did see one of these and it was quite nice especially in high light performance with almost no halo. I have also used some Pinnacle tubes and they are quite nice too, however both add quite a bit to the price of a normal gen 3 device. You would have to question whether the high light performance is worth the extra $$$. Full mil-spec Pinnacle of course be very super, but I am afraid that would not be a reality at this time. I am not sure what the full spec filmless tube would be like, but they are very high performance. There was no omni-contract regarding those that I know of, however there used to be some spec sheets available on them a couple years ago and they were pretty nice. The usual low light specs do not radically exceed the normal omni-4 and 5 tubes in those characteristics though, so again, the high light performance is the big thing on those, and you have to really need that to justify the extra bucks(in my thinking anyway)

But, please note that the latest Hi-Perf Euro tubes are not any more strictly "gen II"; because, even if they do not use GaAs for the PC, it is still a semiconductor mix that is being used (it's exact composition is classified), and PR can be in excess of 850 uA/lm; being essentialy "filmless", these tubes will perform as filmed Gen III tubes with a PR of 1600-1800.
Resolution is well over 70 lp/mm, with amazing S/N ratios (over 28, measured by US standards) and at least one of these tubes is autogated, allowing use even in high light conditions (Dep's XR5).
These tubes CAN very well compare to current mainstream US production.

Some European tubes can reach a FoM of 2000, well over the US Gov't exportability limit.

SN and PR
by

Signal-to-Noise Ratio (SNR) - A measure of the light signal reaching the eye divided by the perceived noise as seen by the eye. A tubes SNR determines the low-light-resolution of the image tube; therefore, the higher the SNR, the better the ability of the tube to resolve objects with good contrast under low-light conditions. Because SNR is directly related to the photocathodes sensitivity and also accounts for phosphor efficiency and MCP operating voltage, it is the best single indicator of an image intensifiers performance.

In short, the Photocathode Response is indirectly a factor in the computation for SN.

D310 Tube
by

The DN 310 tube is a so called 20/30 arrangement. I believe a similar one was produced at one time or another by Litton. It has a 25 mm input, and 18 mm output. 2 main advantages of this arrangement:
a) Increases the resolution on the output by about 50% compared to a similar 25/25 tube. The DN310 tube is rated at about 40-45 lp/mm
b) The tube has an internal magnification (1.55x)compared with most other Gen 2/3 tubes which are 1x. This allows achieving relatively high magnification in a compact weapon scope, using small lenses.
The tube is built with 4 outputs, 2 of them allowing variable gain (real one).
It's a fairly large size (about 65 mm long).
Is it a Gen 2 or 2+ ? There is no sharp definition. One of the criterias are tube life:
The newest ones are rated at over 5000 hours, although we have 1 pc back at the factory which has ranked over 7000 hours and works well.
The tube is not as good as latest/greatest Gen 3 equipment. But for the cost, as far as new equipment goes, I think it is a good value.
