Ion Barrier Film
by Anonymous

I will explain this as best I can. I don't remember much chemistry, so feel free to correct me if I mix up the details. The Ion Barrier Film is the primary reason for the significantly greater tube life of Gen III versus Gen II image tubes. The photocathode releases a whole bunch of free electrons when light hits it, and in the presence of oxygen or water, the photocathode will quickly oxidize, effectively burning up the cathode. So image tubes are usually purged with a non-reactive gas or constructed in a high vacuum state to remove the contaminants, and sealed to prevent its future entry. Still, contaminants will slowly migrate into the tube over time, so eventually the photocathode will degrade. Gen III tubes use an extremely thin (just a few atoms thick, I believe) layer of alumnium (I think that's right) to prevent this from happening as quickly. The drawback is that the quantum efficiency (basically the amount of electrons actually getting through) is reduced by nearly 50%. This is compensated in part by higher sensitivity to IR light, which is abundantly produced by stars and a lot of artificial lights. Thin-filmed and filmless-autogated tubes deal with the problem, in the first case by making the ion barrier film thinner, and in the second case (which I don't understand completely) by removing the film and using some kind of dynamically controlled power supply to reduce the amount of electricity going through the tube.

I hope this is a fairly accurate description, and I hope you find it helpful.

Re: NVPS-10 2-3 GEN or D-121M
by

In terms of the quality image you see (especially in no moon conditions), it should be NVPS-10 in gen3 (make sure it has a PVS-5 eyepiece), then the D121M, followed by the NVPS-10 gen2.

I own two NVPS-10's, gen 3 with PVS-5 eyepiece, gen 2 with standard eyepiece. The gen 3 45lp is much better than the gen2 tube. It should outperform the 36lp gen2+ tube in the D121M also. It'll probably cost a little more too. You might check directly with NAIT on a price for one of those 45lp NVPS-10s. Other tubes may be available (51lp) and some places even sell them with 64lp blem tubes. I would not go with a gen3 36lp tube though.

As far as ruggedness for mounting on a weapon, I don't know. Both are versatile systems, headmountable, camere adaptable, etc. The NVPS-10 accepts C mount lenses, giving you a good variety and even the option to go with (expensive though) C mount version of the PVS14 and PVS7 military 1x 27mm lens. I believe you can also use relay lenses with them for video too. Don't know about the 121M, but they have been given good reviews by users here.

I think both are nice systems, but would always choose gen 3 (45lp or better) over a gen2+ system if price is not a huge limiting factor. The D121M should have a better built in illuminator, though I almost never use the one on my gen 3 NVPS-10. Again, check with NAIT, see what they want for them now. You might be pleasantly surprised at the price, I was last time they sent me a price on them!

These are my opinions, hope they help you some. You should get more D121M comments also from others here. You might also try asking a company that sells both of them for an opinion. D-121 stripdown
by

Screw off the eyepiece assembly and you'll see a threaded ring which locates the tube. Using a small screwdriver inserted into one of the holes in the ring rotate counter clockwise to remove it.
Under the ring will be a circular plastic mounting plate for the fibre optic 'power' and 'illuminator' indicators. Be careful as you move this out of the way, the soldering of the wires connecting it to the switch isn't always 100% and the wires themselves are fragile.
There may also be rubber and\or metal shims present, if so label them 'rear' and put them to one side.
Carefully move all the wiring out of the path of the tube and shake it out, gently.
The power connections to the tube will depend on what tube you have inside :-) the 121 can accept a wide range of intensifiers...
There will be metal\rubber shims at the front of the tube, remove and label these as well.

I always check that the battery is removed before working on an NVD, to prevent an accidental power up :-)

Hope this helps. I have modded D-121's to take various tubes, if you are doing a swap then shout up again for help - getting the focus correct can be a pain but I think that I have encountered and overcome most of the potential problems now. Touch wood :-)

Lif's
by

A LIF is a narrow band optical filter that will not pass light in a particular frequency range. It is designed to protect both the NV device and its user.

As is blocks almost all light in a particular frequency range and blocks a small amount of light in all frequencies (more optical surfaces) having one on your NV device will make it slightly less effective and is only protected against a norrow bend of frequencies.

The trick is, you have to know what frequencies you need protection from in a particular situation, and unless you use multiple filters or have an extremely expensive miltiband filter, you have to choose one narrow bend to protect against.

Great if your special opps, but for most of us, they don't have a lot of value.
