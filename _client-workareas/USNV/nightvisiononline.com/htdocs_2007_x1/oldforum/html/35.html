If you have
by

the tubes potted in the plastic sleeve with red and black wire sticking out, and extra metal housings, you are ok. Just pull the lenses off the old ones and set those aside, make sure you have the o-rings for the objectives. When you slide the potted tube into the housing take care with the wires, they obviously have to go out through the little hole. The tubes are secured with a type of RTV at the front of the housing on that little flange. Just a small amount will do. I have to look up the number, I think it is 2600 or something like that. The stuff I have is a DOW product and makes a nice thing film and is very adhesive. The tube has to be held in place so the wires don't get cut. Originally the wires are sealed with some sort of epoxy, but you could use the same silicone stuff to seal and secure them in the hole. Let it set, then reassemble optics and done, other than perhaps adjusting the focus infinity stop for the new tubes spacing.

Lanny,

I think I can answer that.
It is not the PC photoresponse that counts, it's the amount of PC-released electrons that are actually delivered to the MCP that count: the typical photoresponse of latest Gen III tubes is about 1800 uA/lm (Indium in the mix), while XD-4 is 600/700 uA/lm.
But Gen III is "filmed" technology, and about half of those electrons simply do not pass the ion barrier, so that leaves Gen III with an effective sensitivity of 700/800 uA/lm, while XD-4 has no loss.
I believe that's what Gen 4 is all about: the full 1800 uA/lm are delivered to the MCP for amplification.
I have no idea what DEP uses for PC, on their website there is no info (other than "XD-4 photokathode").

harry
by franco

as Lanny said, the response of a good Omni V mil spec GaAs photocathode is now around 2000 uA/lm.
Again, that is only the response of the PC, and it does NOT take in account any subsequent loss due to the ion barrier (or the MCP).

To my (limited) knowledge, PC response is always measured in isolation with NO amplification stage and ion barrier (i.e. the PR testing is not carried on an actual tube), and data sheets only carry this "raw" figure for the PC (remember, the ion barrier is not on the PC, it's on the MCP).

As a matter of fact, the data sheets for Gen 4 tubes have the same PR values!

How can Gen 4 be be so "hot" then, if the PR is the same?
Because removing the ion barrier and allowing all electrons released from the PC to reach the amplification stage yields greater S/N ratio and lowers the EBI, with amazing performance in extremely low light conditions, and reduces dramatically "hazing" in low light and halo, glare effect with bright lights; also, the contrast level is excellent.
In regards to this technology, I really found very limited literature (and would like to know a bit more), so I may be wrong- I'll be happy if anybody can correct me.

Back to the original post: XD-4 tubes are great tubes, with extremely high image quality (the resolution is 64+ lp/mm) and both comparable S/N and EBI levels to Omni V tubes, while halo and contrast level is most probably similar to Gen 4 (unfilmed) technology.

I also heard of skyhigh price tags... lots higher than any Gen III tube, and that's probably why there are'nt many around in the US...
Hope this helps.

PS There is a definite electron loss at the input of the MCP too, but this is another story.

A NV viewer is handy when working around automated telesopes at night without disturbing them. It's also very handy for checking the optical limit switch which use IR LED's and handy when troubleshooting fiber optic communications equipment. Its a Safety Issue
by Gary R.

Lasers are divided into various "classes" based solely on their potential hazard to users/operators, etc. All lasers that operate in the spectrum that is not visible are "classed" in a higher hazard class, regardless of how little power they transmit. This is goofy as a .001mW IR laser is therefore classed as more "hazardous" than a .5mW red-dot laser. The thought is that you can't tell whether or not an invisible laser is "on" or not. So you might "look" into an IR laser, not realizing that you are cooking your retina. Of course, once the power goes up, the hazard classes of all lasers goes up.
