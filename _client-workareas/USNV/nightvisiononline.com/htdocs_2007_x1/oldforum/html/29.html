truth about pinnacle
by

as a leading expert in the night vision arena I can give some light on the pinnacle topic.

First the military is leaning towards callling the pinnacle gen IV, even though the gen IV has also been used to describe systems in progress that combine I2 and IR into one device.

The pinnacle tube gives the user a drastic improvement in certain environments and not as much in others. The biggest gain is when using the pinnacle in an an/avs-6 or an/avs-9 system. When flying an aircraft or helicopter at night over a lighted area such as a city or harbor, the improved power supply (gated) drastically reduces the halo effect giving a clear more distinct picture. There is a good picture set of this in the May 2001 issue of the Armada defence magazine.

The other improvements in the tube are supposedly a 40% increase in range over the omni IV tube, personally I would say about 20% but it all depends on the perticular test that they set up to rate the performance. The s/n signal-to-noise is now at 26.0 with the use of a filmless micro-channel plate.

Also to answer a question the estimated MTBF or life of the tube is rated at 10,000 hours not 2,000 hours.

As a expert with U.S. Navy and Marine systems I look forward to see the Gen IV tubes put into AN/PVS-7D goggles the AN/AVS-9 and AN/PVS-15 Binoculars, the AN/PVS-18 and AN/PVS-14 Monoculars and the AN/PVS-12 ISWS.

If there are ever any questions for U.S. Military systems, especially Navy and Marine systems please e-mail.

The bottom line is if you want the best you have to pay the price and the pinnacle is the best available today.

Rail grabber Mount review...on my PVS-4.
by

I just received one of Keith's rail grabber mounts and installed it in place of my current ARMS #20.

It works fantastic, much better than the ARMS version.

It sits up slightly higher than the ARMS Mount, which helps with proper alignment of the scope with your eye.

On a flattop, the ARMS mount sit way too low for proper eye relief.

It holds zero perfectly, and is easier to install. it also features a much bigger recoil lung than the ARMS mount.

It has a larger grabbing rail, steel, guided by two pins with a larger surface for much more grip.

It absolutely gets an A+ in my book!

And it is $50 less than the ARMS!

The only drawback I could see was:

#1) the adapter bar is made out of aluminum. I would prefer steel, but since it is only the adapter bar, it really doesn't make any difference. Many of the PVS-4 mounts (carry handle, etc.) are made form aluminum. I am just an "all steel" guy, and don't mind sacrificing a little extra weight for increased peace of mind!

#2) I bought the ARMS mount first, so I lost $50 on the deal!! But I am much happier with this mount, so I don't mind!

Diopter correction
by

I wear glasses, too, and it can be a pain with night vision but not a big one usually. Look for systems with a "diopter correction" which is present on all the systems I've used. Basically you get to use the nightvision as your glasses. If you don't have an extreme prescription or extreme astigmatism or something, you'll be fine. The painful part comes when you have to switch back and forth between glasses/night vision.

I would not start with gen 1 and plan to upgrade. It's easier and better to get some good advice about systems and sellers and get gen 2 or gen 3 (it is more expensive, of course). Gen 1 is a step toward seeing in the dark, but gen 2 is a leap. I've owned two gen 1 systems and didn't keep either for more than a couple of weeks.

Knowing your application would be helpful as well.

Really need optics or Laser
by

I have experimented quite a bit in this area. The large f stop in all NV gear has the side effect of a very shallow depth of field. That means you require a focus adjustment for focusing near or far.

With a handgun you have to focus so close to see the sights, that you are effectivly "blind" futher out. Depth of field is so shallow with PVS7D's that even if you focus on the front or rear sight, the other sight is blurred. Nightsights help a bit but I would not choose that route.

Laser's work fine if the power output and or frequency is matched to your NV equipment. Most lasers are way too bright for gen 3 NV. Halo's, dimming of the rest of your image (due to ABC) or even system shutdown (due to ABP) may occur.

I experimented at Thunder Ranch a while back with a Dr Optic holosight (like a Optima 2000) and it worked great. Easy to pick up, appeared in focus, good brightness. A variable output laser is even better but most of those are a little more bulky.

I keep a glock with a Dr Optic sight on it (replaces the normal rear sight) for use with my NV gear.

On long guns, a laser is the only real way to go for use with goggles.
