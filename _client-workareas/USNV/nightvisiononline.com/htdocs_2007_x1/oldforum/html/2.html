Yep, it is a wonderful device, the monoloc....
by

I absolutely love mine. What is so neat about it is the versatility. And the PVS-14 is the KING of versatility, so they are a natural pair together.

For an avid NV shooter like myself, being able to shoot as consistently as possible (from day to night) is very important. The monoloc allows me ZERO change in impact from day to night.

So the accuracy of your rifle and scope setup when shot in the daytime is the exact same accuracy as the nightime setup, since your daytime rifle and scope never change! You merely lock the PVS-14 to the ocular of the daytime scope (using the monoloc) and commence firing! A wonderful device!

You can also lock it to anything you want!
I have even locked it to a Leica rangefinder and a K-Mart 20x spotting scope! and even the eyepiece on one side of a pair of 8x40 IOR/Valdada regular binoculars!

Another neat feature of the monoloc versus the quick detach ring mount is that the area and lenses of both scopes are sealed water/dirt tight! So if it is raining/dusty, your PVS-14 lens and your scope eyepiece are completely clean. Try that with the regular mounting system of the PVS-14 and quick lock rings, no way, as there is a big space for dirt, debris, rain, etc. to get in between the scopes and block your view.

Just my 2 cents, I am 110% satisfied with my monoloc.

High magnification night vision
by

NV magnification is the ratio of Objective lens focal length devided by eye piece focal length (multiplied by tube's magnification, which is usually 1x.
As example, 75mm objective lens coupled to 25mm eye piece will provide 75/25=3x magnification.
Therefore, as you can see, one can get high magnification in 2 ways:
a) Decreasing eye piece focal length. I'm not an optical engineer, but apparantly this is not practical - and hopefully someone on the forum can explain why.

b) Increasing focal length of the objective lens. However, if you keep the same objective lend diameter, this is accordingly reducing the light transmission. Meaning you'll get the required magnification, but the image will be too dark. Alternatively, you can start increasing proportionately the lens diameter. But than you'll get into big size and very expensive optics.

Bottom line - if high magnification night vision mandatory, you'll either shell out much $ for a large objective lens (Our NVS 8 system as example), or put a cheaper longer focal length objective lens, and use a powerfull I/R illuminatpr to compensate for the losses in light transmission.
