Image Intensifier Tube Inverter
by Denis

Depending on the type of eyepiece you have, you need or don't need an inverter in your image intensifier tube. It is just a question of the optical design of your particular NVG. The inverter is a fiber optic bloc that is twisted 180 degree in order to present the intensified image correctly to the eyepiece. Early GenIII had problem with this fiber optic twisted bloc as the ouside of the block would "rotate" faster than the core of the bloc during the "twist" process making for a distorted image. The fiber optic bloc is attached to the image intensifier and have very stringent tolerance.

As far as what type of lens surface you need this is again totally linked to the optical design of your NVG. The sales representative you are talking to should be able to point you to the correct tubes if you provide him with the model of NVG you plan to install those NVG tubes in.

"Cycles per Milliradian (cy/mr)
Units used to measure system resolution. A milliradian is the angle created by 1 yard at a distance of 1,000 yards. This means that a device that can detect two 1/2-yard objects separated by 1/2 yard at 1,000 yards has a resolution of 1.0 cy/mr. Do not confuse cy/mr with line pair per millimeter. For example, a system can have a 3X lens attached and increase the system resolution by a factor of 3, yet the image intensifier's resolution (measured in lp/mm) has not increased."

You've probably already seen this definition, but I included it anyway. That and the statement ITT used to make to the effect of "our competitors put magnification in their systems to give the impression of good resolution" (paraphrased to the best of my memory) leads me to believe it will increase the apparent resolution to the end user, just as system gain is more applicable to the user than tube gain. Contrast
by

By doubling the size of the image, you will obviously see it better, but not clearer. The image will NOT be sharper, you will think it is because it is so large you will see it better, but not sharper. The limiting resolution of the actual image by the tube will not change, but the size of the "pieces" of the image will change. Like when you get a large screen TV, the picture is huge, but grainy unless you are back away from it. This is not very scientific, but by increasing system resolution you do not increase image resolution exactly. Then I could be wrong again also.

Not the same
by

With a bigger tv screen the same information is spread over a bigger area. With a longer lens you are taking a closer look. Light is spread over a bigger area though with a longer lens unless the diameter of the lens is increased. (Focal ratio) Tv's can't change the input but only spread out the lines. Digital cameras have both digital and optical zoom. Once the limit of optical is reached the digital crops the edge pixels, spreads out the middle, replaces the gaps with the cropped pixels and interpolates the edge values to fill in. The owners manual warns that resolution is lost to a degree in digital mode. Not so in optical. With a TV your brain does the interpolation as you back away. With a long lens you kind of rob the edge but pay the middle. Resolution by it's own definition is increased and passed on to the tube which now has a head start.

Nite Shot
by

See Lou Boyd's post on video imaging on page 4. It is a neat idea. Video cameras use ccd's. Ccd's are IR sensitive. All light focuses differently and daylight cameras are corrected for rgb. IR is very different and unnecessary so a cut filter is used. On a 35mm slr there is a red dot on the lens barrel for a focus correction if you use IR film.
Nite Shot: Drop the cut filter. Add Ir illumination. Range is determined by the amount of light (lumens) and concentration (candlepower)of the illuminator. There is no light amplification so they are inexpensive and the image isn't noisy (sparkles). Add some green to make it look cool like the movies.
Great marketing.

the D
by

was originally the upgraded version of the B, at least in the 7's. When the B was 45 or 51lp, there was a high performance tube available with 64lp and much higher specs, and the unit was designated the D. They also used the suffix on the military image tubes after the tube type, ie. MX10130D-UV. The other tubes were MX10130A, B, and C. Then the unfilmed E came out, and I don't know what the thin film is yet. It is not really an abstract term, but defines the unit as an omni-4 or 5 spec device. At one point I was told that the 7D's went through some extra overall performance tests prior to shipment. I don't know if that is the case. All the D's I ever had were from Litton, and were labelled AN/PVS-7D. As for the 14's I had a set of Littons for a while and have seen plenty, but did not remember if they were labelled such. Apparently the tube sheets on them are also slightly different fromt he 7's as I am sure the 7's listed the omni-spec number somewhere near the top of the sheet.
